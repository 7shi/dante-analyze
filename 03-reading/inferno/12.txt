# Canto 12 — The Descent into the Valley of Violence and the Encounter with the Centaurs

## Scene 1-15: The Ruin and the Minotaur
**Scene Analysis: The Ruin and the Minotaur**

**What happens:**
Dante and Virgil arrive at the point where they must descend into the Seventh Circle (the Valley of Violence). Dante describes the terrain as a terrifying, jagged cliff. To convey the steepness and the sense of ruin, he uses a simile, comparing the descent to a landslide that occurred near Trento, where the Adige river flows—a cliff so sheer that anyone at the top would find no way down. At the edge of this precipice, they encounter the Minotaur (described as the "infamy of Crete"). Upon seeing the two poets, the Minotaur is seized by a fit of rage and begins to bite itself, a physical manifestation of the blind, self-consuming nature of anger.

**Who speaks to whom:**
There is no dialogue in this scene. The passage consists entirely of Dante’s narrative description.

## Scene 16-21: Virgil's Rebuke
**Scene Analysis: Virgil's Rebuke**

**What happens:**
As the Minotaur lashes out in a blind rage (biting itself), Virgil steps in to assert control. He shouts at the creature, mocking its aggression by reminding it that he is not Theseus (the "Duke of Athens"), the hero who killed the Minotaur in the Labyrinth. Virgil tells the beast to move aside, explaining that Dante is not some "trained" animal or a product of the same unnatural origins as the Minotaur (referring to the wooden cow), but a man who has come to witness the punishments of the violent.

## Scene 22-27: The Frenzy of the Bull
**Scene Analysis: The Frenzy of the Bull**

**What happens:**
Dante uses a vivid simile to describe the Minotaur's erratic behavior. He compares the monster to a bull that has been struck by a mortal blow; the bull is no longer able to charge in a straight line but instead leaps frantically and aimlessly. Seeing the creature caught in this self-destructive frenzy, Virgil (the "shrewd one") seizes the moment. He urges Dante to hurry toward the opening/gap in the cliff and descend quickly while the Minotaur is too blinded by rage to block their path.

## Scene 31-45: The Earthquake and the Fall
**Scene Analysis: The Earthquake and the Fall**

**What happens:**
As they continue their descent, Dante is lost in thought. Virgil notices this and speaks to him, correctly guessing that Dante is reflecting on the jagged ruin of the cliff and the beastly rage of the Minotaur (which Virgil has just quelled). Virgil then shares a piece of the geography's history: he reveals that during his previous descent into the lower reaches of Hell, this rock had not yet fallen. He explains that shortly before the fall of Lucifer (the one who brought the "great prey" of fallen angels from the heavens down to the city of Dis), a massive earthquake shook the valley. The tremor was so violent that Virgil thought the universe was experiencing the force of Love—the prime mover which, according to some beliefs, has turned the world into chaos multiple times. This seismic event caused the cliffs to collapse, creating the ruins they see now.

## Scene 49-51: Dante's Moral Reflection
**Scene Analysis: Dante's Moral Reflection**

**What happens:**
As they prepare to enter the river of blood, Dante pauses for a brief, poignant moral reflection. He addresses two personified passions: "blind greed" (*cieca cupidigia*) and "foolish anger" (*ira folle*). He laments how these two forces drive human beings to commit sins during their short earthly lives and subsequently lead them to a terrible, sacrificial fate in eternity. This reflection serves as a bridge between the encounter with the rage-filled Minotaur and the descent into the punishment of the violent.

**Who speaks to whom:**
Dante speaks in an apostrophe (a rhetorical address) to personified passions: Blind Greed and Foolish Anger.

**Resolution of Numbered Tags:**
*   **[1]** Humanity / people in general (including Dante himself), who are "spurred on" by these passions.
*   **[2]** Blind greed and foolish anger (the personified passions being addressed).
*   **[3]** Humanity / people in general (including Dante himself), who are "immolated" or sacrificed by these passions in the afterlife.

## Scene 67-72: Identification of the Centaurs
**Scene Analysis: Identification of the Centaurs**

He then identifies the Centaur in the middle as the "great" Chiron, famously known for being the tutor/nourisher of Achilles. Finally, he identifies the third Centaur as Pholus, describing him as having been consumed by anger. Through these brief biographical sketches, Virgil contextualizes the mythological figures for the reader and the pilgrim.

## Scene 83-96: Virgil's Plea for Passage
**Scene Analysis: Virgil's Plea for Passage**

**What happens:**
Following Chiron's observation that Dante is a living man, Virgil steps forward to explain their presence. He confirms that Dante is indeed alive and that he, Virgil, is tasked with guiding him through the "dark valley." Virgil explains that their journey is driven by necessity rather than pleasure, revealing that a certain soul (Beatrice) left the celestial singing of "alleluias" to entrust him with this mission. To ensure safe passage, Virgil emphasizes that Dante is not a thief and that he (Virgil) is not a random wandering soul. He then asks Chiron to assign one of the Centaurs to guide them to a crossing point and to carry Dante on its back, since Dante, as a living being, lacks the ability of a spirit to move through the air.

## Scene 115-120: The Shadow of the Thames
**Scene Analysis: The Shadow of the Thames**

**What happens:**

This identifies the soul as an English tyrant (historically interpreted by commentators as a reference to the cruelty of English monarchs, possibly King John or those responsible for the death of the Princes in the Tower).
