# Canto 21 — The Encounter with the Malebranche and Malacoda

## Scene 1-6: Arrival at the new, dark ditch
In this opening scene of Canto 21, the narrative focuses on the transition between the Fourth and Fifth Bolgia. Dante and Virgil are crossing the bridges that span the ditches of Malebolge. Virgil is speaking to Dante, though the poet notes that Virgil's conversation does not concern the "comedy" (the poem itself). They arrive at the edge of the next ditch, where they stop to look down and hear the sounds of the suffering souls. Dante observes that this particular ditch is exceptionally dark.

**Tag Resolutions:**
- `[1:+egli]` (he): **Virgil**. He is the guide who is talking to Dante but is not concerned with discussing the poetic composition of the journey.
- `[2:+noi]` (we): **Dante and Virgil**.
- `[3:+noi]` (we): **Dante and Virgil**.
- `[4:+noi]` (we): **Dante and Virgil**.
- `[5:+io]` (I): **Dante**. The narrator/protagonist observing the darkness of the ditch.

## Scene 7-18: The Venetian shipyard simile
In this scene, Dante uses a vivid simile to describe the environment of the Fifth Bolgia. He compares the sight of the boiling pitch in Hell to the Venetian shipyards (*arzanà*) during the winter. In Venice, pitch is boiled to recaulk and repair ships that are unfit for sailing because the winter weather prevents them from going to sea. Dante describes the various activities of the shipyard workers: some build new ships, some repair old ones, some work on the bow or stern, some make oars, some sew sails, and some fix the masts.

He then transitions from the simile back to the reality of the Inferno: just as the pitch boils in the shipyard, a thick layer of pitch boils in the ditch. However, unlike the shipyard where fire is used, this pitch is boiled by "divine art" (the power of God's judgment), and it is so sticky that it clings to the banks of the ditch.

There is no dialogue in this scene; it is a descriptive passage narrated by Dante.

**Tag Resolutions:**
- `[1:+elli]` (they): **The Venetians/shipbuilders** in the shipyard who cannot sail during the winter.
- `[2:chi]` (who/the one who): **A shipbuilder/worker** in the shipyard.
- `[3:chi]` (who/the one who): **A shipbuilder/worker** in the shipyard.
- `[4:quel]` (that/the one): **The ship** (specifically, the ship that has made the most journeys). Note: This refers to the object (*legno*), not a person.
- `[5:chi]` (who/the one who): **A shipbuilder/worker** in the shipyard.
- `[6:chi]` (who/the one who): **A shipbuilder/worker** in the shipyard.
- `[7:chi]` (who/the one who): **A shipbuilder/worker** in the shipyard.

## Scene 19-21: Observing the boiling pitch
**Scene Analysis**

In these three lines, Dante shifts from the detailed simile of the Venetian shipyards back to the immediate reality of the Fifth Bolgia. He is looking down into the ditch and describing what he sees. He notes that he can see the substance filling the ditch, but because it is boiling so violently, the surface is a mass of bubbles. This creates a visual barrier; the pitch is swelling and churning, making it impossible for him to see anything beneath the surface (such as the sinners submerged within it) at this specific moment.

**Tag Resolutions**

- `[1:I']` (I): **Dante**. The narrator and protagonist who is observing the scene.
- `[2:lei]` (her/it): **The boiling pitch** (or the ditch/bolgia containing it). In Italian, both *pece* (pitch) and *bolgia* (ditch) are feminine. Given that the substance is "swelling" and "boiling," it refers to the mass of boiling pitch.
- `[3:+io]` (I): **Dante**.
- `[4:essa]` (it/her): **The boiling pitch** (or the ditch/bolgia). This refers back to the same feminine entity as `[2:lei]`, specifying that Dante cannot see *inside* the pitch/ditch due to the bubbles.

## Scene 25-33: The appearance of the demon
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante describes his reaction to Virgil's urgent warning. Startled, he turns around quickly—comparing himself to a man who is desperate to see the very thing he needs to flee from, driven by a sudden, sharp fear. As he looks back, he sees a black demon running toward them along the rocky ledge. Dante is struck by the demon's terrifying appearance: its fierce expression, its harsh demeanor, its wide-open wings, and the lightness of its step as it charges.

## Scene 34-36: The demon's physical ferocity
**Scene Analysis**

In these three lines, Dante continues his description of the black demon's terrifying physical presence and brutality. He focuses on the demon's upper body—specifically his "sharp and haughty" shoulder—to emphasize his strength and arrogance. The demon is depicted in the act of tormenting a soul: he is hoisting a sinner up using the strength of his flanks, while gripping the sinner tightly by the tendons (the "nerbo") of the feet. This imagery highlights the predatory and effortless violence with which the Malebranche treat the inhabitants of the Fifth Bolgia.

**Tag Resolutions**

- `[1:+elli]` (he): **The black demon**. He is the subject of the action, possessing the "sharp and haughty shoulder" mentioned in line 34.
- `{2:un peccator}` (a sinner): **A sinner**. This is an unnamed soul residing in the boiling pitch of the Fifth Bolgia, currently being tormented by the demon.
- `[3:quei]` (that one): **The sinner**. This refers back to the sinner mentioned in tag [2], who is being held by the feet.

## Scene 37-42: The demon's declaration
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, the black demon reaches the bridge where Dante and Virgil are standing. He shouts to his fellow demons, the Malebranche, alerting them to the presence of the two visitors. The demon speaks in a mocking, sarcastic tone, pretending that Dante and Virgil are "elders of Santa Zita" (corrupt officials from a specific district in Florence). He urges the other demons to throw them into the boiling pitch. He then makes a biting joke about Florence, claiming that the city is so full of barrators (corrupt politicians) that every single citizen is guilty of the crime, with the sole (and likely ironic) exception of a man named Bonturo.

**Tag Resolutions**

- `{1:nostro ponte}` (our bridge): **The bridge** spanning the Fifth Bolgia where Dante and Virgil are currently located.
- `[2:+ei]` (he): **The black demon**. He is the one who has just run up to the bridge and is now speaking.
- `{3:Malebranche}` (Malebranche): **The Malebranche**. The group of demons who guard the Fifth Bolgia.
- `{4:li anzïan di Santa Zita}` (the elders of Santa Zita): **Dante and Virgil**. The demon is using this as a mocking label, identifying them as corrupt Florentine officials from the Santa Zita district.
- `[5:+voi]` (you): **The Malebranche**. The black demon is addressing his fellow demons.
- `[6:i']` (I): **The black demon**. The speaker.
- `{7:Bonturo}` (Bonturo): **Bonturo**. A specific resident of Florence, mentioned by the demon as the only person in the city who is not a barrator.

## Scene 43-46: The demon's departure
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief transition, the black demon completes his interaction with the soul he was holding. He drops the sinner back into the boiling pitch of the Fifth Bolgia. Dante describes the demon's agility and ferocity, comparing him to a "loose mastiff" (a hunting dog) chasing a thief. The demon dives headlong into the boiling substance and then resurfaces, twisting or coiling as he emerges. This sequence emphasizes the predatory nature of the Malebranche and their effortless movement within the environment of their torment.

## Scene 47-54: Warnings from the bridge demons
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, the Malebranche demons who are stationed on the bridge confront Dante and Virgil. They shout warnings and threats, making it clear that the visitors are not welcome. They mockingly refer to them as the "Holy Face," emphasizing the contrast between the purity of the divine and the filth of the boiling pitch. The demons specifically warn Dante not to lean too far over the edge of the ditch if he wishes to avoid being scratched by their claws. As they speak, they snap at him aggressively. Finally, they advise him that the only way to survive or navigate this area is to stay hidden and, if possible, sneak away unnoticed.

## Scene 55-57: The cooking simile
In this brief scene, Dante employs a culinary simile to describe the cruelty and method of the Malebranche demons. He compares the demons' treatment of the sinners in the boiling pitch to that of cruel cooks. Just as cooks use hooks to push meat (or, sarcastically, their "vassals") down into a boiling cauldron to ensure it doesn't float to the surface, the Malebranche use their hooks to force the barrators deep into the boiling pitch, preventing them from escaping the heat by floating.

**Tag Resolutions:**

- `{1:i cuoci}` (the cooks): **The Malebranche demons**. In this simile, the demons are compared to cooks who manage the "cauldron" of the Fifth Bolgia.
- `{2:lor vassalli}` (their vassals): **The sinners in the Fifth Bolgia**. Dante uses "vassals" sarcastically to describe the souls who are now subject to the absolute and brutal power of the demons, effectively treating them as the "meat" being cooked in the cauldron.

## Scene 58-63: Virgil's advice to Dante
In this scene, Virgil takes decisive action to protect Dante from the aggressive Malebranche demons. Recognizing the danger, Virgil instructs Dante to hide behind a piece of rocky debris (*uno scheggio*) so that the demons cannot see him. Virgil reassures Dante, telling him not to be afraid even if the demons insult or attack him (Virgil), as Virgil has a plan. He reveals that he has dealt with such "bargaining" or confrontations with these demons in the past, implying he knows how to manipulate or handle them.

He then commands the demons to let them pass, explaining that it is the will of Heaven that he (Virgil) guide another person (Dante) through this "wild path" of the Inferno.

## Scene 133-139: Malacoda's reassurance and the demons' exit
In this scene, Malacoda attempts to soothe Dante's fears while the demon escort prepares to lead them forward. However, as the group of demons turns to lead the way along the left bank, they reveal their true intentions through a series of crude, secret signals.
