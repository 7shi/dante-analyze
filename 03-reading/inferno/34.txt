# Canto 34 — The Center of the Earth and the Encounter with Lucifer

## Scene 1-9: Approaching the Mist
In this scene, Dante and Virgil are navigating the frozen wasteland of Cocytus. Virgil notices the wind picking up and warns Dante that the "banners" (the gusts of wind) of the ruler of Hell are blowing toward them. He urges Dante to look ahead to see if he can make out the source. 

As Dante looks into the distance, the visibility is poor—similar to a thick fog or the onset of night. Through this gloom, he sees a distant shape that resembles a windmill. The wind becomes so intense that Dante is forced to huddle close to Virgil for protection, as there is no other shelter (no "cave") available to shield him from the blast.

## Scene 10-15: The Shadows in the Dark
In this scene, Dante the narrator describes the chilling sight of the sinners trapped in the ice of the lake Cocytus. He notes that he is recounting this experience with fear. He observes that the souls are encased in the ice, making them appear semi-transparent, like blades of grass frozen in glass. He describes their distorted and frozen postures: some are lying flat, some are standing upright (some head-down, some feet-down), and others are bent double like a bow.

## Scene 16-21: The Arrival at Dite
In this scene, Dante and Virgil have finally reached the point where Virgil believes it is time to reveal the ruler of Hell to Dante. Virgil steps aside to ensure Dante has an unobstructed view of the "creature" (Lucifer). As he does so, Virgil speaks to Dante, pointing out the center of the abyss (referred to here as Dite) and warning Dante that he must summon all his courage and strength to face what he is about to see.

## Scene 22-27: Dante’s Internal Horror
In this scene, Dante the poet breaks the narrative flow to address the reader directly. He describes his reaction to the sight of Lucifer, claiming that the experience was so overwhelming and terrifying that it is beyond the power of language to describe. He describes a state of paradoxical suspension: he was not dead, yet he didn't feel alive—he was completely paralyzed by horror. He challenges the reader to use their own imagination to conceive of such a state of being.

## Scene 28-36: The Emergence of Lucifer
In this scene, Dante finally describes the appearance and scale of Lucifer. He observes that the "Emperor of the sorrowful kingdom" is trapped in the ice of Cocytus, with only the upper half of his body emerging from the frozen lake. 

Dante is struck by the creature's immense size; he notes that Lucifer's arms are so vast that they make the giants (whom Dante encountered earlier in his journey) seem small by comparison. Dante then shifts his address to the reader, asking them to imagine the total magnitude of Lucifer's body based on the sheer size of the part that is visible above the ice. 

Finally, Dante reflects on the nature of Lucifer's fall. He reasons that if Lucifer was once as beautiful as he is now hideous, and if he dared to rebel against his Creator, it is only fitting that he is now the source of all the misery and sorrow found in Hell.

## Scene 37-52: The Anatomy of the Beast
In this scene, titled "The Anatomy of the Beast," Dante describes the grotesque and terrifying physical form of Lucifer in detail. Dante is struck with wonder (or rather, horror-struck amazement) as he observes that Lucifer has three faces on one head. 

He describes the arrangement and colors of these faces: the central face is red; the face on the right is a pale yellow/white; and the face on the left is black (compared to the people of the Nile region/Ethiopia). Beneath each of these faces is a pair of massive wings. Dante notes that these wings are not feathered like a bird's, but are instead leathery and membrane-like, similar to those of a bat. The scene concludes with the explanation of the wind: as Lucifer flaps these six wings, they create three powerful currents of air that emanate from him, which are responsible for freezing the entire lake of Cocytus.

## Scene 53-60: The Devouring of Sinners
In this scene, Dante describes the horrific activities of Lucifer. He observes that Lucifer is weeping from his six eyes, and tears mixed with bloody foam drip from his three mouths. Lucifer is using each of these three mouths to chew on a sinner, treating them like pieces of meat. Because there are three mouths, Lucifer is simultaneously tormenting three different sinners. Dante notes that for the sinner in the front mouth, the pain of being bitten is negligible compared to the agony caused by Lucifer's claws, which occasionally strip the skin entirely from the sinner's back.

## Scene 61-67: Identifying the Traitors
In this scene, Virgil acts as a guide and commentator, identifying the three specific traitors whom Lucifer is eternally chewing. He explains to Dante that the soul suffering the greatest agony (the one in the central mouth, mentioned in the previous scene) is Judas Iscariot, who is head-first in the mouth with his legs kicking outside. Virgil then identifies the other two sinners: the one hanging from the black face is Brutus, and the third is Cassius. Virgil points out to Dante how Brutus twists in pain without making a sound and notes that Cassius appears severely mangled.

## Scene 68-75: The Descent via the Beast
In this scene, the encounter with Lucifer concludes, and the process of exiting the center of Hell begins. Virgil informs Dante that they have seen everything they needed to see and that it is time to depart. To make their escape, they must physically descend further by climbing down the body of Lucifer, who is stuck in the ice. 

Dante describes the physical maneuver: he clings tightly to Virgil's neck. Virgil, acting as the guide and protector, positions Dante securely and then grabs onto the "velvety" (shaggy) sides of Lucifer. Virgil then descends through the thick hair and the frozen ice of the lake, carrying Dante with him as they move past the center of gravity of the earth.

## Scene 76-81: Reaching the Center
In this scene, Dante and Virgil continue their perilous descent down the frozen body of Lucifer. They have reached the area of the hip and thigh. Virgil, the guide, is leading the way with great effort and anxiety. He turns his head to look toward Lucifer's legs/feet to orient himself for the final part of the climb. Virgil grips the thick hair of the beast to pull himself and Dante downward, moving like a climber on a rock face. The physical sensation of this movement, combined with the disorientation of passing the center of the earth, makes Dante feel for a moment as if they are returning deeper into the abyss.

## Scene 82-87: Finding a Ledge
In this scene, the descent from the center of the earth continues. Virgil, exhausted from the physical exertion of climbing down the frozen mass of Lucifer, is panting heavily. He warns Dante to hold on tightly, noting that these "stairs" (the treacherous path they are navigating) are the only way to distance themselves from the "evil" (Lucifer). Virgil then finds a small opening or ledge in the rock, helps Dante up onto the edge so he can sit and recover, and continues to guide him with careful steps.

## Scene 88-93: The Sight of the Upside-Down Legs
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief but pivotal scene, Dante describes the disorientation that occurs as he and Virgil pass the center of the Earth. Having climbed down the body of Lucifer, Dante looks back (or "up" from his new perspective) and is momentarily confused. Because he has passed the center of gravity, the orientation of everything has flipped: he sees Lucifer’s legs pointing upward. This creates a moment of psychological and physical distress for Dante. He then turns to the reader, specifically addressing the "vulgar crowd" (those without the intellectual or philosophical training to understand the geometry of the universe), noting that they might not comprehend the significance of the point he has just passed (the exact center of the Earth).

## Scene 94-99: The Difficult Path
In this scene, titled "The Difficult Path," Virgil urges Dante to stop resting and get back on his feet. Virgil warns him that they have a long and arduous journey ahead and notes that time is passing—the sun has already reached the third hour (roughly 9:00 AM). 

Dante then describes the nature of the terrain they are navigating. He explains that the path they were on was not a gentle or smooth slope, but rather a natural, narrow rocky passage (a "burella") characterized by a rough, uneven floor and a severe lack of light.

## Scene 100-105: Dante’s Confusion
In this scene, Dante has just regained his footing after resting on the rocky ledge. Before Virgil leads him further along the path and away from the depths of the abyss, Dante speaks up to express his profound disorientation. Having passed the center of the Earth, his perception of space and time is skewed. He asks Virgil for an explanation regarding three things: the current location of the ice of Cocytus, why Lucifer now appears to be stuck upside down (the result of the gravitational flip at the Earth's center), and how the sun could have moved from evening to morning in such a short amount of time.

## Scene 106-117: The Center of Gravity
In this scene, Virgil responds to Dante's confusion regarding their orientation and the physical laws of the center of the Earth. He explains the geometry of their descent: Dante still believes he is on the "original" side of the center, but Virgil clarifies that they have already passed the point of gravity. 

Virgil describes how he gripped the hair of Lucifer (whom he calls the "wicked worm") to guide them down. He explains that as they descended, and specifically at the moment Virgil turned around to check on him, Dante crossed the exact center of the Earth—the point toward which all weights are drawn. Consequently, Dante is now in the opposite hemisphere, the one facing away from the known lands of the northern world. Virgil mentions that the peak of this current hemisphere is where Adam (the man born and lived without sin) resided. He concludes by explaining that Dante is now standing on the "other side" of the zone of Giudecca.

## Scene 118-126: The Shifting Hemisphere
In this scene, Virgil continues his explanation to Dante regarding their new orientation. He begins by explaining the time difference: because they have crossed into the opposite hemisphere, it is now morning where they are, while it remains evening on the side of the world they left behind.

Virgil then describes the physical and cosmological state of Lucifer and the Earth. He notes that Lucifer remains frozen in the same position they encountered him in. He explains that this specific location is where Lucifer originally fell from Heaven. In a piece of Dante's imaginative cosmology, Virgil explains that the Earth itself was so terrified of Lucifer's fall that it recoiled, shifting its mass away from him toward the northern hemisphere. This "flight" of the Earth created a void or gap (the space Dante and Virgil are now traversing) as the Earth surged upward and away from the fallen angel.

## Scene 127-132: The Sound of the Stream
In this scene, Virgil continues his explanation of the geography and cosmology of the center of the Earth. He describes a place far below the position of the fallen angel, which is so distant that it cannot be seen, but is instead identified by the sound of a small stream (*ruscelletto*). Virgil explains that this stream has worn a hole through a rock and winds its way through the terrain with very little slope.

## Scene 133-139: The Return to the Stars
In this final scene of the *Inferno*, titled "The Return to the Stars," Dante and Virgil begin their ascent out of the depths of Hell. They follow the "hidden path" (the route shaped by the small stream mentioned in the previous scene) to return to the "clear world" of the living.

Determined and without stopping for rest, they climb upward; Virgil leads the way as the first, and Dante follows closely behind as the second. As they ascend, Dante eventually sees the beautiful things of the heavens through a round opening in the rock. The Canto—and the entire first division of the *Divine Comedy*—concludes with the two of them emerging from the abyss to see the stars once again.
