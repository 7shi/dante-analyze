# Canto 22 — The Chaos and Combat of the Malebranche

## Scene 1-12: The Knightly Vision
In this scene, Dante is observing the chaotic and noisy behavior of the Malebranche demons as they escort him and Virgil. The demons are signaling to one another with a frantic variety of shouts and gestures. To describe this commotion, Dante pauses the immediate narrative to draw a comparison to a historical memory of military mobilization.

He recalls the atmosphere of war in the city of Arezzo (a city often at odds with Dante's native Florence). He describes the sight of knights breaking camp, the movement of couriers across the landscape, and the vigilance of guards. He remembers the cacophony of signals—trumpets, bells, and drums—used to coordinate the troops. He concludes that he has never seen such a diverse and frantic array of signals used by any army (whether cavalry or infantry) or even by ships navigating by land or star.

## Scene 13-18: Dante’s Observation
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief passage, the narrative returns from the historical digression about the army of Arezzo back to the immediate action in the Fifth Bolgia. Dante and Virgil are continuing their walk, escorted by the ten Malebranche demons. 

Dante makes a sarcastic aside regarding his companions, calling them a "fierce company." He remarks that while he is with them now, he would much rather be in a church with the saints or even in a tavern with the gluttons (a nod to the Third Circle of Hell) than be in the company of these demons. Despite his distaste for the demons, Dante remains observant; he keeps his eyes fixed on the ledge ("la pegola") to study the layout of the bolgia and the suffering of the sinners immersed in the boiling pitch.

## Scene 19-30: Sinners Hiding Like Frogs
In this scene, Dante describes the behavior of the sinners (the barrators) immersed in the boiling pitch of the Fifth Bolgia. He uses two vivid similes from the natural world to illustrate their movements and their fear of the demons.

First, he compares the sinners to dolphins: just as dolphins arch their backs to signal to sailors that it is time to steer the ship, some sinners occasionally raise their backs above the pitch to get a momentary reprieve from the heat. However, they disappear back into the depths almost instantly.

Second, he compares them to frogs at the edge of a ditch, who keep only their noses above the water while hiding the rest of their bodies. The sinners are scattered throughout the bolgia in this same manner. However, the moment the demon Barbariccia approaches them, they dive back down into the boiling pitch to avoid his notice and potential torture.

## Scene 31-36: Graffiacan’s Agitation
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante describes a specific, jarring interaction between the demon Graffiacan and the sinners immersed in the boiling pitch. 

Dante observes one sinner waiting in the pitch, contrasting his stillness with the movement of others. He uses a simile involving frogs: just as one frog might remain still while another leaps away, the sinners' reactions to the demons vary.

Graffiacan, who is positioned close to the sinners, suddenly attacks. He reaches down and grabs the pitch-soaked hair of the sinners, violently hauling them upward. Dante notes that the fluid, agile movement of the demon as he pulls the sinners out of the pitch reminds him of an otter.

## Scene 37-39: Dante’s Meta-Commentary
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief meta-commentary, Dante pauses the narrative to address a potential question from the reader: how does he know the specific names of the Malebranche demons (such as Barbariccia and Graffiacan) when he is a stranger to them?

Dante explains that he was paying close attention during the preceding events. He notes that he observed the demons when they were "elected" or chosen by their leader, Malacoda, to form the escort party. He then listened carefully as they identified themselves or were called by name, thereby recording their identities for the purpose of his poem. This passage serves as a justification for the narrator's omniscient knowledge of the demons' names.

Watching this cruel spectacle, Dante turns to his guide, Virgil. He asks Virgil to find out the identity of this "unfortunate" soul who has fallen into the hands of his tormentors (the demons).

## Scene 46-54: The Navarrese’s Origin
In this scene, Virgil acts upon Dante's request from the previous passage. He approaches the sinner who has just been violently extracted from the boiling pitch by the demon Graffiacan to discover his identity.

Virgil asks the soul where he is from, and the sinner responds by explaining his origins. He reveals that he was born in the Kingdom of Navarre. He describes a troubled upbringing: his mother had placed him in service to a certain lord because the sinner had been fathered by a "ribaldo" (a scoundrel or rascal) who had squandered his own wealth and ruined himself.

The sinner continues his autobiography, noting that he eventually entered the service (the household) of King Theobald. It was while serving the King that he engaged in "baratteria" (the crime of barratry—using a political office for personal gain through corruption). He concludes by acknowledging that this specific sin is the reason he is now suffering and "rendering account" in the intense heat of the Fifth Bolgia.

## Scene 81-90: The Denunciation of Gomita
In this scene, the Navarrese sinner answers Virgil's question about who led him to his current state of damnation. He identifies the culprit as **Frate Gomita**, a monk from Gallura in Sardinia. 

The Navarrese sinner describes Gomita as a "vessel of every fraud," detailing a specific act of corruption: Gomita had the enemies of his mistress (or lady) in his power, but instead of acting against them, he took bribes from them and quietly released them. He notes that Gomita was not merely a petty corrupt official, but a "sovereign" or supreme barrator in various other dealings. Finally, he mentions another associate of Gomita, **Michel Zanche** of Logudoro, noting that the two of them are well-known in Sardinia for their incessant scheming and talking.

## Scene 106-111: Cagnazzo’s Rebuttal
In this scene, the demon Cagnazzo reacts to the Navarrese sinner's proposal to summon other Italian souls. Cagnazzo is not fooled by the offer; he lifts his head and shakes it, alerting the other demons to the sinner's perceived trick. He claims that the sinner has only come up with this plan as a "malizia" (a cunning ruse) to create a distraction or a gap in the demons' perimeter, allowing him to dive back down into the safety of the boiling pitch.

Instead, he replies that he is indeed very "malizioso" (cunning/malicious), but justifies this behavior as a necessary means of ensuring his own "trestizia" (safety or security) from the demons' hooks.

## Scene 127-132: The Struggle in the Pitch
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief, high-action scene, Dante describes the immediate aftermath of the Navarrese sinner's leap into the boiling pitch. 

The demon who had first noticed the sinner's movement and shouted his success (from the previous scene) finds that his realization came too late; the shout did nothing to stop the escape. The Malebranche demons, despite their wings, were unable to react with enough speed to intercept the sinner before he submerged.

Dante employs a vivid simile from nature to illustrate the interaction: he compares the Navarrese sinner to a duck (*anitra*) and the demon to a falcon (*falcon*). Just as a duck suddenly dives underwater the moment a falcon approaches, the sinner vanishes into the pitch. Consequently, the demon—like the frustrated falcon—is forced to fly back up to the surface, empty-handed and "broken" (cross/frustrated) by the failure of the hunt.

## Scene 133-138: Calcabrina’s Entanglement
In this scene, the focus shifts to the aftermath of the Navarrese sinner's successful escape. Calcabrina, one of the Malebranche demons, had been flying close behind the sinner. Interestingly, Calcabrina's motivation wasn't just to catch him, but he was almost "fond" of the idea of the sinner surviving the leap just so the fight and torture could continue.

However, once the barrator (the Navarrese sinner) vanishes completely into the boiling pitch, Calcabrina's aggression has no target. In a fit of rage and frustration, he turns his claws upon his own comrade—the fellow demon who had been negligent and allowed the escape to happen. The scene ends with the two demons locked in a violent struggle, clutching each other above the ditch.
