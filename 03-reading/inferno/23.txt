# Canto 23 — The Flight from the Malebranche and the Encounter with the Hypocrites

## Scene 1-12: The Fable of the Frog and the Mouse
In this opening scene, Dante and Virgil are quietly making their exit from the fifth bolgia, moving away from the chaos of the boiling pitch. They walk in single file, maintaining a silence and a disciplined gait that Dante compares to the way Franciscan friars walk.

As they move, Dante's mind is preoccupied with the fight he just witnessed between the Malebranche demons and the barrators. He finds a parallel between this "present fight" and one of Aesop's fables—specifically the one involving a frog and a mouse. He reflects on the similarity between the current situation and the fable's plot. This line of thinking triggers a subsequent thought (a "spark"), which causes Dante's existing anxiety to increase, doubling his fear.

There is no dialogue in this passage; it consists entirely of Dante's narrative description and his internal reflections.

## Scene 13-24: Dante's Dread of the Demons
In this scene, Dante is consumed by anxiety. While they are making their escape, he reflects on the humiliation the Malebranche demons suffered (specifically the ones who got stuck in the boiling pitch). He reasons that the demons will be furious over this mockery and will hunt them down with the ferocity of a dog chasing a hare. This internal panic manifests physically—his hair stands on end—and he urgently pleads with Virgil to find a hiding place, claiming that he can almost hear the demons closing in behind them.

## Scene 25-33: Virgil's Strategic Plan
In this scene, Virgil responds to Dante's mounting panic. Virgil uses a poetic metaphor involving "leaded glass" to tell Dante that his fear is so visible on his face that it is almost as if Virgil is absorbing it or seeing a perfect reflection of it. Essentially, Virgil is saying, "I can see exactly how terrified you are; your anxiety is written all over you."

Because Virgil recognizes this shared sense of urgency (or because Dante's panic has pushed him to a decision), Virgil proposes a strategic escape. He notes that the layout of the ledge on the right side allows them to climb down directly into the next *bolgia* (the sixth), which will allow them to evade the "imagined hunt"—the pursuit by the Malebranche that Dante is so certain is coming.

## Scene 34-51: The Rapid Descent
In this scene, the transition from Virgil's planning to action is instantaneous. Dante explains that before Virgil could even finish articulating his plan, Dante spotted the Malebranche demons gliding toward them with their wings spread, intent on capturing them. 

Reacting with sudden urgency, Virgil seizes Dante. Dante employs a poignant simile to describe this action, comparing Virgil to a mother who is awakened by the sound of fire; seeing the flames nearby, she grabs her child and flees without hesitation, caring more for her son's safety than her own, fleeing in nothing but her nightgown.

Following this impulse, Virgil throws himself and Dante backward down the steep, rocky slope that forms the wall between the fifth and sixth *bolgia*. Dante compares the speed of their descent to the rush of water in a mill-race as it accelerates toward the wheel. As they plunge downward, Virgil carries Dante pressed against his chest, acting not as a mere guide or companion, but as a protective father would for his son.

## Scene 52-57: Reaching the Fifth Bolgia
In this short scene, Dante and Virgil have completed their rapid descent and landed at the bottom of the sixth bolgia. 

Dante notes that as soon as Virgil's feet touch the ground, the Malebranche demons are already positioned on the ledge above them. However, there is no longer any danger of being captured. Dante explains that this is because Divine Providence (God) has decreed a restriction on the demons: while they are the "ministers" (the appointed jailers and torturers) of the fifth bolgia, they have been stripped of the power to leave that specific area. Consequently, the demons are trapped within the boundaries of their own jurisdiction and cannot descend into the sixth bolgia to pursue the poets.

## Scene 58-66: The Appearance of the Hypocrites
In this scene, Dante and Virgil have arrived in the sixth *bolgia*. They encounter a procession of souls—the Hypocrites—who are walking in a slow, mournful circle. Dante describes them as "painted," suggesting a deceptive outer appearance.

The souls are dressed in heavy capes with low hoods that shade their eyes, similar to the habits worn by the monks of the monastery of Cluny. Dante notes a stark contrast in the construction of these garments: the outside is gilded to look brilliant and precious, but the inside is made of solid lead. This serves as a physical manifestation of hypocrisy—a shining exterior hiding a heavy, oppressive interior. Dante concludes with a reference to Federigo (Federigo da Pisa, a figure known for his extreme frugality), noting that even someone as stingy as Federigo would have preferred to stuff those capes with straw rather than bear the crushing weight of the lead.

There is no dialogue in this passage; it is Dante's narrative observation.

## Scene 67-72: The Burden of the Cloaks
In this scene, Dante and Virgil join the procession of the Hypocrites. Dante begins with an exclamation about the "eternally tiring" nature of the leaden cloaks the souls must wear. Dante and Virgil turn to the left, walking alongside the souls and focusing on their sorrowful weeping. 

However, because the leaden cloaks are so incredibly heavy, the Hypocrites move with agonizing slowness. As a result, Dante and Virgil—who are not burdened by such weight—find themselves constantly overtaking the souls. Every time they shift their hips to move forward, they find themselves walking beside a "new" member of the procession.

## Scene 85-90: The Recognition of the Living
In this scene, the two Hypocrites who called out to Dante and Virgil finally reach them. They gaze at Dante with squinted, suspicious eyes, remaining silent at first. Then, they turn to one another and whisper, speculating about the nature of the two visitors. They observe that Dante appears to be alive, noting the rhythmic movement of his throat (breathing). They wonder that if these two are indeed dead souls, by what special privilege are they allowed to walk without wearing the crushing leaden cloaks that all other Hypocrites must bear.

## Scene 94-99: Dante's Inquiry
In this scene, Dante responds to the two Hypocrites who have just approached him and asked for his identity. He identifies himself as a native of Florence, noting that he was born and raised by the Arno river. Crucially, he informs them that he is still alive, stating that he possesses the same physical body he has always had. Having answered their question, Dante turns the inquiry back to them; he asks who they are, commenting on the visible sorrow (the tears) streaming down their cheeks and inquiring about the nature of the punishment they are enduring (referring to the glittering, gilded leaden cloaks).

## Scene 100-108: The Identity of the Hypocrites
In this scene, one of the two Hypocrites responds to the questions Dante asked in the previous scene. The speaker begins by explaining the nature of their punishment: the "foul" cloaks are made of lead and are so heavy that they cause the souls to creak like weighted scales. 

The speaker then reveals their identities. He explains that he and his companion were "Joyful Friars" (*Frati godenti*) from Bologna. He identifies himself as Catalano and his companion as Loderingo. He mentions that they were taken from Dante's homeland (Tuscany/Florence) together, comparing this event to the way a solitary man is often removed from a place to preserve the peace. He concludes by noting that their reputation—likely for their hedonistic and hypocritical lifestyle—is still remembered by the people around Lake Garda.

## Scene 114-123: The Monk's Explanation

Catalano explains that this individual advised the Pharisees that it was necessary to sacrifice one man to appease the people. Because of this, he is now pinned naked across the path, forced to feel the weight of every single person who passes over him. Catalano specifically identifies this man as "the father-in-law" (Caiaphas) and notes that other members of the council who brought ruin to the Jews are suffering similarly in this bolgia.

## Scene 133-138: The Obstacle of the Stone
In this scene, Brother Catalano responds to Virgil's request for a safe exit from the sixth *bolgia*. He informs Virgil that there is a way out that is even better than what Virgil might be hoping for. He explains that a massive rock, which normally moves along the great outer circle and crosses over all the jagged valleys (the *bolge*), is broken in this particular section, meaning it does not cover the gap here. Consequently, he tells Dante and Virgil that they will be able to climb out of the bolgia by using the ruins of this rock, which lean against the slope and overhang the bottom of the pit.

## Scene 139-141: The Error of the Demon

Virgil stands for a moment with his head bowed in reflection. He then makes a dry, ironic comment, suggesting that whoever designed the enclosure for the sinners in this area miscalculated the requirements, as the structural failure of the rock creates an unintended opening.

## Scene 142-144: The Lie of the Devil
In this scene, Brother Catalano responds to Virgil's ironic comment about the "error" of the Divine Architect. Catalano, wanting to defend the perfection of God's design, suggests that the gap in the rock is not a mistake by God, but rather a result of the devil's deceit. He mentions that during his time in Bologna, he heard that the devil is plagued by many vices, most notably that he is a liar and the "father of lies." He is essentially attributing the structural "failure" to diabolical trickery rather than divine oversight.

## Scene 145-148: The Departure
In this brief concluding scene of Canto 23, Virgil decides it is time to move on. He begins to lead the way with large, hurried strides, his facial expression showing a hint of irritation or anger (likely stemming from the tedious nature of the conversation with the Hypocrites or Catalano's simplistic attempt to explain divine architecture). Consequently, Dante leaves the "burdened ones"—the Hypocrites, specifically Catalano and Loderingo, who are weighed down by their leaden cloaks—and follows closely behind Virgil, stepping in the footprints of his guide's "dear feet."
