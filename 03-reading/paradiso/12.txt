# Canto 12 — The Praise of Saint Dominic and the Gathering of the Saints

## Scene 1-9: The Rotation of the Celestial Gear
In this scene, the transition between the Fourth Heaven (the Heaven of the Wise) and the Fifth Heaven begins. As soon as the speaker finishes his discourse, the celestial sphere (described as a "holy mill" or "wheel") begins to rotate. The movement of the Fourth Heaven is synchronized with the sphere above it, and the music generated by this celestial rotation far surpasses any music or poetry created by humans.

**Tag Resolutions:**

*   `{1:la benedetta fiamma}`: **Thomas Aquinas**. He is the radiant soul who has just finished his detailed explanation regarding St. Francis and St. Bonaventure.
*   `[2:+ella]`: **The celestial sphere of the Fourth Heaven** (referenced as "la santa mola" in line 3).
*   `[3:+ella]`: **The celestial sphere of the Fourth Heaven** (referenced as "la santa mola" in line 3).
*   `{4:muse}`: **The Muses** (the classical Greek goddesses of inspiration for literature, science, and the arts).
*   `{5:serene}`: **The Muses**. (An epithet referring back to the "muse" in the previous line).

## Scene 10-21: The Intertwining Garlands
In this scene, Dante describes the physical movement of the celestial spheres as he and Beatrice transition between heavens. He employs a complex simile, comparing the movement of two concentric circles of light (garlands) to the appearance of a rainbow.

He first references classical mythology: Juno commands her handmaid (Iris) to deliver a message, and the rainbow appears as a result. He then shifts to a biblical reference, noting that the rainbow serves as a sign to humanity of the covenant God made with Noah, promising that the world would never again be destroyed by a flood. 

Returning to the present moment, Dante explains that the two "eternal" garlands of light are rotating around him and Beatrice, with the outer circle responding to the movement of the inner circle, mimicking the synchronization he described in the previous scene.

**Tag Resolutions:**

*   `{1:Iunone}`: **Juno** (the queen of the gods in Roman mythology).
*   `{2:ancella}`: **Iris** (the messenger goddess and handmaid of Juno, who appears as a rainbow).
*   `{3:quella vaga}`: **Iris** (referred to as "that beautiful one").
*   `{4:la gente}`: **Humanity** (the people of Earth who see the rainbow as a sign).
*   `{5:Dio}`: **God**.
*   `{6:Noè}`: **Noah**.
*   `[7:noi]`: **Dante and Beatrice**.

## Scene 22-30: The Voice Emerges
In this scene, the grand celebration of the Fourth Heaven—characterized by harmonious singing and the flashing of radiant lights—comes to a synchronized halt. Dante compares this simultaneous cessation to the way eyes open and close together in response to pleasure. Immediately after this silence, a voice emerges from one of the nine radiant souls residing in this sphere. Dante is instantly drawn to the source of the voice, comparing his movement to the way a compass needle is pulled toward the North Star.

**Tag Resolutions:**
* `{1:le luci nove}`: **The nine blessed souls of the Fourth Heaven** (the radiant figures who represent the wise).
* `[2:mi]`: **Dante**.

## Scene 31-36: The Theology of the Two Guides
In this scene, the radiant soul (who is later identified as Saint Dominic) begins to speak to Dante and Beatrice. The soul explains that the love they feel motivates them to speak in favor of "the other guide" (Virgil). The soul acknowledges that Virgil is the reason why the soul's own merit is praised among the blessed. The soul argues that it is only right that where one guide (Beatrice) is present, the other (Virgil) should also be brought, so that their shared purpose in guiding Dante can be reflected in their shared glory.

## Scene 37-45: The Allegory of the Church's Defense
In this scene, Saint Dominic continues his speech, shifting from the specific topic of Virgil to a broader historical and spiritual allegory about the defense of the Christian faith.

Dominic describes the "Army of Christ" (the Church) as being in a state of decline—moving slowly, hesitantly, and in scattered numbers. He explains that Christ, the eternal Emperor, intervened to support this militia. He emphasizes that this support was given through grace, not because the Church had earned it through merit. Christ, acting as the Eternal Emperor, came to the aid of His "bride" (the Church) by providing two "champions." The actions and teachings of these two figures were instrumental in bringing straying people back to the correct path of faith.

**Tag Resolutions:**

*   `{4:sua sposa}`: **The Church** (the Bride of Christ).
*   `{5:due campioni}`: **Saint Dominic and Saint Francis** (the two champions sent to reform the Church).

## Scene 46-60: The Birth of Saint Dominic
In this scene, the speaking soul (Saint Dominic) describes his origins. He begins by situating his birthplace geographically, using poetic and natural imagery. He points to the west, where the West Wind (Zefiro) brings spring to Europe and where the sun sets over the ocean. He identifies the specific location as Calaroga (Caleruega in Spain), noting that it is under the protection of a house/region marked by the symbol of a lion (referring to the heraldry of the region/Castile and León).

Dominic then describes himself in two ways: as a passionate lover of the Christian faith and as a "holy athlete" (a spiritual warrior). He characterizes his nature as being benevolent toward his followers but stern and uncompromising toward the enemies of the faith (heretics). Finally, he notes that his innate virtue was so great from the moment of his conception/birth that his mother experienced a prophetic vision regarding his future mission.

## Scene 61-78: The Vision and Signs of Sanctity
In this scene, Saint Dominic continues his autobiography, describing the spiritual foundations of his life, starting from his baptism and early childhood.

He uses the metaphor of a marriage to describe his baptism ("sacred fountain"), where he was wedded to Faith, ensuring mutual salvation for both. He then recounts a prophetic dream had by his mother; she saw the "miraculous fruit" (the child) that would result from the union between his father and her own womb. Dominic explains that the Holy Spirit inspired his name, "Domenico" (meaning "of the Lord"), to reflect that he belonged entirely to God.

Dominic then describes his mission using an agricultural metaphor: he is the "farmer" chosen by Christ to help tend to Christ's "garden" (the Church). He characterizes himself as a messenger and close confidant of Christ, noting that his earliest love was for the teachings and counsels of Christ. Finally, he recalls how, as a child, his nurse frequently found him in a state of ecstatic trance on the ground, during which he would say, "I have come to this," signaling his early awareness of his divine purpose.

## Scene 79-87: The Rapid Growth of the Doctor
In this scene, Saint Dominic continues his autobiography, beginning with a tribute to his parents. He praises his father, Felix, and his mother, Joanna, noting that his mother's name (Joanna, which relates to "grace") was prophetic of her spiritual value. 

Dominic then contrasts his own lifelong pursuit with the pursuits of the "world." While the world strives and labors for earthly things—following in the footsteps of figures like the "Ostiense" (Saint Augustine) and "Taddeo" (Saint Thaddeus)—Dominic's drive was fueled by a love for the "true manna" (the Word of God/Divine Revelation). Because of this focus, he quickly became a "great doctor" (a learned theologian). He concludes with an agricultural metaphor: he describes himself as a diligent worker in the vineyard (the Church), noting that when the vine-dresser (the steward of the faith) is negligent or guilty, the vineyard quickly "whitens" (meaning it becomes plagued by mildew or heresy).

## Scene 88-105: The Missionary Zeal of Dominic
In this scene, Saint Dominic describes his approach to the Papacy and the subsequent establishment of his mission. He emphasizes his lack of interest in worldly power or privileges, contrasting himself with the "poor righteous" (the Franciscans), who received dispensations from the Pope. Dominic explains that he did not seek financial gain, vacant sees, or tithes. Instead, he requested the Pope's permission to fight against the "errant world" (heretics) to protect the "seed" of the faith—the Divine Word, which is surrounded by the twenty-four books of the Old Testament.

Armed with apostolic authority and spiritual zeal, Dominic acted as a powerful "torrent," sweeping away the "heretical shrubs" with great force, especially where the opposition was strongest. Finally, he explains that his life and work gave rise to "diverse rivers"—the Dominican Order—which continue to nourish and irrigate the "Catholic garden" (the Church), ensuring that its "little shrubs" (the faithful) remain vibrant and alive.

## Scene 106-114: The Vacancy in the Second Circle
In this scene, Saint Dominic concludes his autobiography by using a chariot metaphor to link his mission with that of Saint Francis. He describes the "chariot" (*biga*) as the mechanism through which the Church defended itself and won its spiritual battles. He represents one wheel of this chariot. 

Dominic argues that if his own role as a "wheel" was so effective, it should be obvious to Dante that the "other wheel"—Saint Francis—is equally excellent. He references Thomas Aquinas, who had already praised Francis's merits in the previous canto. However, Dominic then observes a physical void in the celestial arrangement: a part of the Fourth Heaven's orbit is "derelict" or empty. He uses the imagery of "mold" where the "axle-tree" (*gromma*) used to be, signaling that the other champion (Francis) is no longer in this sphere because he has ascended to a higher heaven.

**Tag Resolutions:**

*   `{1:la Santa Chiesa}`: **The Holy Church**.

*   `[3:+ella]`: **The Holy Church** (the Church won the struggle).
*   `[4:ti]`: **Dante** (Dominic is addressing Dante, telling him that the excellence of the other champion should be clear to him).
*   `{5:Tomma}`: **Thomas Aquinas**.

## Scene 115-126: The Vastness of the Second Ring
In this scene, Saint Dominic continues his discourse, moving from his personal history to the current state of the blessed souls in the Fourth Heaven and the nature of his spiritual mission.

First, he describes the movement of the souls. He refers to "his family"—referring to the followers of Saint Francis (the Franciscans). He explains that because of the circular rotation of the celestial sphere, the relative positions of the souls shift; those who were once leading are now behind, and those who were behind are now in front.

Dominic then shifts to a metaphor about the "bad cultivation" of the faith (heresy). He describes the heretics as "weeds" (*il loglio*) who will lament when they realize the "ark" (the Church, the vessel of salvation) has been taken away from them—meaning they are excluded from salvation.

Finally, Dominic mentions the "volume" (the history or the rule) of his own Order. He claims that anyone searching through the pages of this record would find a statement of his identity and mission. He quotes a phrase: "I am that who [stands at the threshold/usually...]," asserting his role as a defender of the faith. He clarifies that his mission is distinct from the approaches found in places like Casal or Acquasparta (locations associated with certain dissident or extreme spiritual movements), where people either flee from the divine law or restrict it too harshly. Dominic presents his own path as the balanced, orthodox middle ground.

## Scene 127-141: The Assembly of the Saints
In this scene, Saint Dominic continues his discourse by acting as a celestial guide, introducing Dante to the other inhabitants of the Fourth Heaven (the Heaven of the Wise). He provides a "roll call" of the theologians, prophets, and scholars who reside there, emphasizing the diversity of wisdom—ranging from biblical prophecy to linguistic grammar—that is rewarded in this sphere.

Dominic begins by claiming a spiritual kinship with Saint Bonaventure, stating that he is the "life" (the spiritual foundation or influence) of Bonaventure's work. He then lists several other figures: Saint Illuminatus and Saint Augustine (linking Augustine to the tradition of the "barefoot poor" or apostolic poverty), Hugh of Saint Victor, Peter Lombard, and Peter Olivi. He further expands the list to include Nathan the Prophet, John Chrysostom, Saint Anselm, and the grammarian Donatus. Finally, he points out Rabanus Maurus and the prophetic Abbot Joachim of Fiore.

## Scene 142-145: The Unified Praise
In this brief concluding segment of his speech, Saint Dominic explains the motivation behind his words and the current gathering. He explains that he was prompted to praise the "great champion" (referring to Saint Francis) due to the intense kindness and courtesy shown by both Thomas Aquinas and Virgil. He notes that this same spirit of courtesy is what brought the traveling party (Dante and Beatrice) to him.
