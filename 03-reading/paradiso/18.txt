# Canto 18 — The Vision of the Celestial Lights and the Inscription of Justice

## Scene 1-6: Beatrice's Command
In this scene, Dante and Beatrice are in a moment of pause following the harsh prophecy of exile delivered by Cacciaguida in the previous canto. Beatrice is content with her words, while Dante is internally struggling, trying to balance the "sweetness" of his current divine company with the "bitterness" of his future earthly fate. Beatrice notices his distress and commands him to shift his focus, reminding him that she is now in the presence of God, the ultimate source of justice who corrects all wrongs.

## Scene 7-18: Dante's Emotional Response
In this scene, Dante is describing the emotional and spiritual effect of looking into Beatrice's eyes after her words of comfort and correction. He is narrating to the reader his struggle to accurately describe the experience because the intensity of the divine vision exceeded the capacity of his human mind to record it.

Dante explains that as he looked at Beatrice, all his earthly desires disappeared. He saw the light of God's eternal pleasure radiating through her, which provided him with a profound sense of contentment and peace, effectively neutralizing the bitterness of the prophecy of his exile.

## Scene 19-21: Beatrice's Second Instruction
In this brief but pivotal scene, Beatrice uses a smile to soothe Dante's remaining distress and then directs his attention away from her and toward the surrounding celestial environment. 

Beatrice's smile "wins over" or overcomes Dante's internal struggle, effectively calming him. She then speaks to him, commanding him to turn around and listen, explaining that the experience of Paradise (the divine light and truth) is not contained solely within her eyes, but is present in the wider vision he is about to witness.

## Scene 22-27: The Spirit's Presence
In this scene, Dante uses a simile to describe a moment of intense emotional and spiritual recognition. He compares the way a look of deep affection can completely captivate a person's soul to the experience he has just had. Following Beatrice's instruction to turn around, Dante turns toward a "holy lightning" (a flash of divine light). In that light, he recognizes the presence of a spirit who wishes to continue their conversation.

Based on the narrative progression of the poem (having just spoken with his ancestor in the previous canto), the spirit in the light is Cacciaguida. Dante recognizes Cacciaguida's desire to speak further with him.

## Scene 28-36: The Threshold and the Cross
In this scene, Cacciaguida begins to explain the nature of the sphere they have entered—the fifth heaven (Jupiter). He uses the metaphor of a tree that is eternally fruitful and evergreen to describe the divine order and the abundance of grace in this realm. He explains that the spirits residing here are those who were renowned and outspoken on earth, such that their eloquence would have enriched the Muses. Cacciaguida then directs Dante's attention toward the "horns of the cross" (the specific configuration of lights in this heaven), telling Dante that the person he is about to name will perform a sudden, flashing sign (like lightning) in that location.

## Scene 37-42: The Procession of Heroes
In this scene, Dante describes the visual manifestations of the spirits as they are named. He observes a sequence of divine signs that coincide with the naming of biblical heroes who were just and courageous leaders.

As the name of Joshua is spoken, Dante sees a streak of light. He notes that the visual sign happened so quickly—or with such power—that the action preceded his conscious processing of the spoken name. This is a reference to the biblical account where Joshua commanded the sun to stand still. Then, when the name of the "high Maccabee" (Judas Maccabeus) is spoken, Dante sees another light moving in a whirling motion. The atmosphere is one of intense joy and divine power.

## Scene 43-48: The Continued Parade

He uses a vivid simile, comparing his focused gaze to that of a falconer watching his bird in flight, to emphasize the intensity and speed with which his eyes follow these spirits across the formation of the celestial cross.

Dante's gaze moves from the figures of the Carolingian era (Charlemagne and Roland) to other great leaders and knights (William, Rainardo, Duke Godfrey, and Robert Guiscard), all of whom are exemplary figures of justice and leadership in the sphere of Jupiter.

## Scene 58-63: The Growth of Virtue
In this scene, Dante uses a general analogy to describe his own spiritual progression. He starts with a universal observation: a person who performs good deeds feels increasing pleasure, and as a result, they notice their own virtue growing day by day. 

Dante then applies this logic to his own current experience in the sphere of Jupiter. He realizes that his own "arc"—his spiritual capacity and his range of vision—has expanded as he revolves in synchronization with the celestial movements. By expanding this "arc," he is able to perceive the divine miracle of the celestial lights with even greater clarity and beauty.

## Scene 70-78: The Formation of Letters
In this scene, Dante witnesses a spectacular visual manifestation of divine order. As he looks into the bright, joyful light of the sphere of Jupiter, he sees the love radiating from the blessed spirits organizing itself into a legible form. The light begins to spell out the letters of their language before his eyes.

To describe this, Dante uses a simile: he compares the spirits to birds rising from a riverbank that fly in changing formations (circles or other shapes) as if to congratulate their shepherd. Returning from the simile to the reality of the vision, Dante explains that the "holy creatures" (the blessed souls of Jupiter) are flying and singing, and by their movement, they are forming letters. In the provided text, they are forming the letters D, I, and L (which are the beginning of the word "DILETTI" or, in the full context of the canto, the word "GIUSTIZIA" / Justice).

## Scene 79-81: The Rhythms of the Lights
In this scene, Dante describes the precise choreography of the blessed spirits in the sphere of Jupiter as they form the word "GIUSTIZIA" (Justice). He observes a rhythmic alternation between movement and stillness, and between sound and silence.

The spirits move in synchronization with the music of their singing. Once they have successfully arranged themselves into the shape of one of the letters (one of the "signs"), they pause their movement and fall silent. This creates a punctuated effect, allowing Dante to clearly perceive each letter of the divine word as it is formed.

## Scene 88-93: The Inscribed Words
In this scene, the visual spectacle described in the previous lines reaches its conclusion. The blessed spirits of the sphere of Jupiter have arranged themselves into a total of thirty-five letters (five times seven), consisting of vowels and consonants. Dante carefully observes and records the words being spelled out by these living letters.

The inscription is in Latin and is divided into two parts. The first part, which Dante notes first, is "DILIGITE IUSTITIAM" ("Love Justice"). The second part follows: "QUI IUDICATIS TERRAM" ("Here you judge the earth"). The text is not merely a decorative painting, but a divine statement regarding the nature of the souls in this sphere: they are the just rulers who, in their celestial state, are tasked with the administration of divine justice over the earthly realm.

- `[1:io]`: Dante (the observer recording the letters)

- `[3:+voi]`: The blessed spirits (the "you" plural in the imperative "Love Justice," addressed to the spirits of Jupiter)
- `[4:+io]`: Dante (the one who first noted the first part of the message)
- `[5:+voi]`: The blessed spirits (the "you" plural in "Here you judge the earth," referring to the souls of Jupiter)

## Scene 100-108: The Explosion of Sparks
In this scene, Dante describes a sudden and dramatic shift in the arrangement of the blessed spirits. He uses a vivid simile to describe the movement: he compares the spirits to the innumerable sparks that fly up when dry, burnt logs are struck. He mentions that "the foolish" often use such sparks to make wishes or predict the future (a superstitious practice).

Following this simile, Dante describes how more than a thousand lights suddenly rise from the previously formed inscription, ascending at varying heights, much like the sun that ignites them. Once each light has settled into its new designated position, Dante observes the final result: the collective formation of these spirits now resembles the "head and neck of a needle" (the pointed top part of a needle).

**Tag Resolutions:**
- `{1:li stolti}`: The foolish (generic people on earth who use sparks for divination/superstition)

He notes that she had been contentedly watching the spirits as they formed the letter 'M', and as the formation shifted into the new, needle-like shape, she followed the movement with a subtle shift of her own.

## Scene 118-123: Prayer against Greed
In this scene, Dante addresses Beatrice with a solemn request. Having witnessed the divine order of Justice in the sphere of Jupiter, Dante is reminded of the corruption and greed (simony) plaguing the earthly Church.

Dante prays to the Divine Mind (God), who is the source of Beatrice's power and motion, asking that Beatrice look down upon the "smoke" (the corruption) that obscures her radiance on earth. His ultimate hope is that God will once again breathe a wind of righteous indignation—similar to the biblical account of Jesus cleansing the temple—to purge the Church of those who treat the faith as a business ("buying and selling inside the temple").

## Scene 124-129: The Critique of Modernity
In this scene, Dante addresses the blessed spirits he has just seen forming the word "Justice," calling them the "militia of heaven." He asks these just souls to intercede and pray for the people of Earth, who have been led astray by the poor examples set by their current leaders. 

Dante then offers a scathing critique of the nature of conflict. He observes that in the past, war was fought openly and physically with swords. In contrast, the "war" of his own time is fought through insidious theft and greed—specifically by stealing the "bread" (the spiritual and physical resources) that God provides freely to everyone. This is a pointed reference to the simony and corruption of the Church, where the "battle" is no longer about honor or righteousness, but about the accumulation of wealth at the expense of the poor.

## Scene 130-136: A Warning to the Poet
In this scene, Cacciaguida delivers a stern warning to Dante regarding his poetic mission. He cautions Dante that if he writes solely to "cancel" or destroy (referring to his scathing critiques of the Church and political leaders), he must remember that the founders of the Church, St. Peter and St. Paul, are still alive in heaven and are watching. Cacciaguida warns that by attacking the "vineyard" (the Church), Dante is essentially opposing the legacy of those who died to establish it.

Cacciaguida then anticipates a possible defense Dante might offer: that he has fixed his spiritual desire on a more solitary, intellectual, or personal path—specifically referring to the experience of St. Paul, who lived as a solitary scholar before his sudden ("by leaps") conversion and eventual martyrdom. In this imagined defense, Dante would claim that he recognizes this personal, spiritual journey rather than the institutional authority represented by "the fisherman" (Peter) and "Paul" (the official apostle). Cacciaguida is highlighting the danger of using a selective reading of faith to justify the destruction of the Church's earthly structure.
