# Canto 22 — The Vision of the Celestial Lights and the Descent to Earth

## Scene 1-21: Beatrice's Rebuke
**Narrative Summary**

Dante is overwhelmed with amazement and fear following the thunderous shout of the blessed souls. In his distress, he turns to Beatrice for reassurance, comparing himself to a small child who runs to the person they trust most. Beatrice responds with maternal tenderness, gently rebuking him for his fear. She reminds him that he is in Heaven, where everything is holy and every action is motivated by divine love and zeal. She explains that the shout he heard was actually a collective prayer for justice. She informs him that the "sword" of divine vengeance does not act arbitrarily or instantly, but according to the will of God. Finally, she directs his attention away from his fear and toward the other illustrious spirits they are about to encounter.

## Scene 22-30: The Vision of the White Lights
**Narrative Summary**

As Beatrice directs him, Dante turns his gaze and sees a hundred tiny, sparkling spheres (the souls of the blessed) that enhance each other's brightness with their intersecting rays. Dante describes his own emotional state using a simile: he feels like someone who suppresses a strong desire and is afraid to ask for what they want, fearing the consequences or the intensity of the longing. To satisfy this longing, Dante focuses his attention on the largest and most brilliant of these "daisies" (the lights), bringing it into focus to content his curiosity.

## Scene 37-51: The History of the Monastery
In this scene, the brilliant light that Dante focused on begins to speak. The speaker is **Saint Benedict**, the founder of the monastery of Monte Cassino. He describes the history of the mountain of Cassino, explaining that before his arrival, the summit was frequented by people who were deceived and misguided. Benedict explains that he was the one who first brought the name of Jesus Christ to that place, bringing the divine truth that elevates all the blessed souls. He notes that God's grace allowed him to lead the surrounding villagers away from the influence of the "learned impious" (the pagan traditions and philosophers who had misled the world). Finally, Benedict points out other lights around him, identifying them as holy men, specifically mentioning Macarius and Romoaldo, as well as his own fellow monks who remained steadfast in their faith within the monastery.

## Scene 52-60: Dante's Request
In this scene, Dante speaks directly to Saint Benedict. He tells the Saint that the affection Benedict has shown him in their conversation, combined with the goodness Dante perceives in the intensity of the lights surrounding them, has greatly increased his trust and confidence. He uses a simile, comparing the expansion of his trust to a rose opening up under the influence of the sun. Because of this feeling of security and trust, Dante asks Benedict if it is possible for him to receive the grace of seeing Benedict's actual human form ("uncovered image") rather than just the brilliant sphere of light.

## Scene 61-69: Description of the Empyrean
**Narrative Summary**

Saint Benedict responds to Dante's request to see his human form. He explains that such a desire—the longing to see the full, uncovered image of the blessed—can only be fulfilled in the Empyrean, the highest and final sphere. He notes that this is where the desires of all the blessed souls, including his own, are perfectly and completely satisfied. Benedict describes the Empyrean as a place that transcends physical location and constraint; it is a state of spiritual perfection where every part of the soul's longing is realized. Because the journey's "ladder" (the progression through the celestial spheres) leads toward this ultimate destination, the image Benedict currently presents remains elusive and "flies away" from Dante's sight for now.

## Scene 70-84: The Decay of Monastic Discipline
**Narrative Summary**

Saint Benedict continues his explanation to Dante, using a biblical allusion to describe the path to the Empyrean. He evokes the image of the Patriarch Jacob, who saw a ladder reaching toward heaven, filled with angels. Benedict uses this "ladder" as a metaphor for the spiritual ascent. However, he shifts to a sorrowful tone, lamenting that in his time, no one is climbing this ladder. He explains that his monastic rule has been neglected and exists now only as written words on damaged pages. He describes the physical and spiritual decay of his order: monasteries have become like caves, and the monks' cowls (symbols of their vows) are now merely bags used to hoard "bad flour" (representing greed and worldly gain). Benedict asserts that the corruption within the monastic life—specifically the pursuit of wealth and the practice of nepotism—is an offense to God's pleasure even more grave than the sin of usury. He concludes that the Church's resources and appointments are being sought not for the glory of God, but for the benefit of relatives and other unworthy causes.

## Scene 85-96: The Inevitability of Change
In this scene, Saint Benedict reflects on the inherent weakness of human nature to explain why the holy orders he and others established have fallen into decay. He argues that the "flesh of mortals" is too weak for a good beginning to guarantee a lasting result. To illustrate this, he cites three examples of monastic foundations rooted in poverty and piety: Peter Damian (who spoke earlier in the Canto), himself (Benedict), and Saint Francis of Assisi. He tells Dante that if he compares the humble origins of these orders to their current state, he will see a total reversal—a transition from "white" (purity) to "brown" (corruption). Benedict concludes by saying that it would be more likely for the Jordan River to flow backward or the sea to flee (biblical miracles) than for the current state of monastic corruption to be easily remedied.

## Scene 100-105: Dante's Rapid Ascent
In this scene, the focus shifts from the discourse of Saint Benedict to the movement of the characters. After the assembly of blessed souls has swept upward, Beatrice (the "sweet woman") uses her divine power to propel Dante upward as well. With a single gesture, she overcomes the limits of Dante's physical nature, accelerating him at a speed that is completely impossible in the earthly realm. Dante notes that nothing in the physical world—where things naturally rise and fall—moves with such rapidity. His ascent is so swift that it transcends any physical comparison, almost as if he had wings.

## Scene 106-120: The Sign of Taurus
In this scene, Dante briefly breaks the narrative flow to address the reader and the celestial environment. He reflects on the incredible speed of his ascent, claiming that in the time it took him to pass from the sign of Taurus (into Gemini), a reader wouldn't even have had time to put a finger in a fire. He then turns his address to the glorious stars (the lights/blessed souls), acknowledging that his own intellect was born and hidden by the will of God ("the father of every mortal life") at the moment of his birth. He concludes by noting that through divine grace, he has now been allowed to enter the celestial rotation and reach their region.

## Scene 133-138: The View of the Earth
In this scene, Dante obeys Beatrice's command to look back toward the Earth. From his current height in the celestial spheres, he gazes down through the seven spheres and sees the Earth (the "globe"). Seeing it from this divine vantage point, he is struck by its insignificance and corruption, describing its appearance as "vile" and smiling at it. This visual experience leads to a moral reflection: Dante now approves of the wisdom that treats the material world as something of lesser importance. He concludes that those who set their sights on a higher, spiritual goal rather than worldly pursuits are the ones who can be truly called upright or virtuous.

## Scene 139-150: The Motion of the Planets
**Narrative Summary**

In this scene, Dante looks back down toward the center of the universe and observes the celestial bodies from his divine vantage point. He first sees the Moon ("the daughter of Latona") and realizes that the "shadow" (the Earth's shadow causing eclipses) which had previously led him to believe the Moon's substance was inconsistent ("rare and dense") was merely an optical illusion. He then gazes at the Sun (Hyperion) and observes its orbital motion relative to the inner planets, Mercury (Maia) and Venus (Dione). He then perceives the position of Jupiter, situated between its "father" (Saturn) and its "son" (Mars). Finally, he sees the entire system of the seven planets, which collectively reveal to him their true scale, their speed, and their vast distances from one another.

## Scene 151-154: The Earth as a Small Garden
In this scene, Dante continues his retrospective gaze at the Earth from his high celestial vantage point. He describes the Earth as a "little plot of ground" (*aiuola*), a diminutive term that emphasizes the planet's insignificance compared to the vastness of the heavens. He reflects on how this tiny place is the source of humanity's fierce conflicts and greed. As he looks, he is positioned near the constellation Gemini. He is able to see the entire world at once, from the mountains to the river mouths. After this sobering realization of Earth's smallness and the futility of human strife, he turns his gaze away from the world and back to Beatrice.
