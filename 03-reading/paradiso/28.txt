# Canto 28 — The Vision of the Celestial Hierarchies and the Angelic Orders

## Scene 1-12: The Mirror of Memory
**Scene Analysis: The Mirror of Memory**

**What happens:**
Dante is reflecting on the transition between the revelation he just received and his reaction to it. Beatrice has just finished explaining the "truth" regarding the current state of human existence (the "miserable mortals"). Dante describes his reaction using a detailed simile: he compares himself to a man who sees the reflection of a flame in a mirror before he realizes there is a fire behind him; the man then turns around to verify that the source of the light matches the reflection. Similarly, after hearing the truth from Beatrice, Dante turns his gaze back to her beautiful eyes. He concludes by noting that those eyes are the source from which Love cast the "cord" (the bond/snare) that captured him.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator and protagonist. He is recalling his actions and emotions.
*   **Beatrice** is the guide who has just provided the intellectual revelation (though she is not speaking in these specific lines).
*   **The "Imaginary Man"** is the subject of the simile, acting as a proxy for Dante's cognitive process of seeing a truth and then looking at the source.
*   **Love (Amor)** is the personified force that captured Dante through Beatrice.

**Tag Resolutions:**

*   **[2:quella]**: Beatrice.
*   **[3:'mparadisa]**: Beatrice (the one who "paradises" or leads Dante's mind toward paradise).
*   **[4:colui]**: The imaginary man in the simile.
*   **[5:se]**: The imaginary man in the simile.
*   **[6:+ei]**: The imaginary man in the simile.

## Scene 13-30: The Radiant Point and Concentric Spheres
**Scene Analysis: The Radiant Point and Concentric Spheres**

**What happens:**
Dante shifts his gaze from Beatrice back toward the celestial structure (the "volume" of the heavens). He observes a single point of light of such blinding intensity that it forces him to close his eyes. He uses a comparison to emphasize its brightness: even the dimmest star in the sky would appear as a full moon if placed next to this point. He then describes a halo of light surrounding the point, comparing it to the atmospheric glow seen around the moon (using the image of a "swan's wing" and vapor). Finally, he describes a series of concentric circles of fire revolving around the central point. These circles spin faster than any physical motion in the known universe. He notes that there are six such circles, each surrounding the previous one in a nested sequence.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator and the observer. He is the one looking, reacting to the light, and describing the geometric and luminous arrangement of the Empyrean.
*   **Beatrice** is the silent guide; while she is the catalyst for his gaze, she does not speak or act in this specific passage.

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **[1:io]**: Dante.
*   **[2:mi]**: Dante.
*   **[3:+io]**: Dante.

## Scene 31-39: The Expansion of the Seventh Sphere
**Scene Analysis: The Expansion of the Seventh Sphere**

**What happens:**
Dante continues his description of the celestial architecture. He describes the seventh circle of fire, noting that its width is so immense that the entire sphere of Mercury (the messenger of Juno) would be too small to contain it. He notes that there are also an eighth and a ninth circle. He observes a specific physical law governing these circles: the further a circle is from the central point of light (the first), the slower it rotates. Furthermore, he observes that the flame of a circle is purer the closer it is to the "pure spark" (the central light of God). Dante expresses his belief that this purity is a result of the circle's proximity to the Divine source.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator and observer. He is analyzing the geometry and the luminosity of the Empyrean and formulating a conclusion about the relationship between proximity to God and the quality of the light.
*   **The messenger of Juno** is a mythological reference used as a measure of scale.

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **{1:l messo di {Iuno}}**: Mercury (the messenger of Juno).
*   **[2:+io]**: Dante.

## Scene 40-45: Beatrice's First Intervention
**Scene Analysis: Beatrice's First Intervention**

**What happens:**
Beatrice notices that Dante is deeply preoccupied or anxious—likely due to the overwhelming nature of the celestial machinery he has been observing. She intervenes to explain the metaphysical significance of the central point of light. She tells him that the entire universe and all of nature depend upon that single point. She then instructs him to focus on the innermost circle (the one closest to the point) and explains the reason for its extreme speed: it is driven by the "burning love" that pierces it.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Beatrice** is the speaker. She observes Dante's state of mind, provides the theological explanation of the central point of light, and explains the motivation behind the motion of the first circle.
*   **Dante** is the listener. He is observed by Beatrice as being "in cura" (worried/suspended in doubt) and receives instructions on what to observe and understand.

## Scene 46-57: Dante's Theological Doubt
**Scene Analysis: Dante's Theological Doubt**

**What happens:**
Dante addresses Beatrice with a logical and theological paradox. He observes that in the Empyrean, the order of divinity is centered: the closer a circle is to the central point of light (God), the more divine and faster it is. However, he recalls that in the "sensible world" (the physical universe), the opposite is true: the spheres are considered more divine the further they are from the center (the Earth). Because of this contradiction, Dante asks Beatrice to explain why the "example" (the physical world) and the "exemplar" (the divine archetype/heavenly order) operate in opposite directions. He admits that he cannot resolve this discrepancy through his own contemplation.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the speaker. He expresses his confusion and asks a specific question about the nature of the universe's structure.
*   **Beatrice** is the listener. She is being asked to provide the theological explanation for the inverse relationship between the physical and spiritual hierarchies.

*   **[4:m']**: Dante.
*   **[5:m']**: Dante.
*   **[6:convienmi]**: Dante (the "mi" part of the verb *convenire*).
*   **[7:io]**: Dante.
*   **[8:me]**: Dante.

## Scene 58-60: Beatrice's Reassurance
**Scene Analysis: Beatrice's Reassurance**

**What happens:**
Beatrice responds to Dante's admission of confusion. She uses a metaphor, comparing the theological paradox he is trying to solve (the inverse order of the physical and spiritual spheres) to a "knot." She tells him that if his "fingers" (his human intellect/reason) are not sufficient to untie this knot, it is no surprise, because the mystery is intentionally tied so tightly ("made sodo") that it is not meant to be untied by human effort alone. She is effectively reassuring him that his inability to solve the puzzle is a result of the problem's inherent divine complexity, not a personal failure.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Beatrice** is the speaker. She addresses Dante with a gentle, slightly pedagogical tone, acknowledging the limits of human reason.
*   **Dante** is the listener. He is the one whose "fingers" (intellect) are struggling with the complexity of the divine architecture.

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **[tuoi]** (in "li tuoi diti"): Dante (referring to his intellectual capacity to "untie" the mystery).

## Scene 61-78: The Principle of Divine Proportion
**Scene Analysis: The Principle of Divine Proportion**

**What happens:**
Beatrice continues her explanation, instructing Dante to listen closely and scrutinize the area around the central point of light. She explains the metaphysical law governing the "corporate circles" (the spheres of light/angelic orders): their size (wide or narrow) is determined by the amount of "virtue" (divine grace/power) that flows through them. She explains that greater goodness necessitates a greater capacity for salvation, which in turn requires a "larger body" (a larger circle). 

She then addresses the paradox Dante raised: the outermost circle of the Empyrean—the one that encompasses the entire physical universe—corresponds to the innermost circle (the one that loves and knows the most). Essentially, she is explaining a system of inverse correspondence: the "most divine" part of the physical world (its outermost sphere) corresponds to the "most divine" part of the spiritual world (the innermost circle). She tells Dante that if he shifts his perspective from physical appearances (the "roundness" of the substances) to the underlying spiritual virtue, he will see a "miraculous consequence": a proportional relationship where the "greater" (the outermost physical) corresponds to the "more" (the most divine spiritual), and the "lesser" to the "less".

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Beatrice** is the speaker. She is acting as a theological teacher, explaining the complex geometric and spiritual relationship between the physical universe and the Empyrean.
*   **Dante** is the listener. He is being instructed on how to intellectually process the visual evidence he sees so that he can understand the divine proportion.

## Scene 79-87: Clarity of Understanding
**Scene Analysis: Clarity of Understanding**

**What happens:**
Dante describes the effect that Beatrice’s explanation has on his state of mind using a vivid atmospheric simile. He compares his sudden intellectual clarity to the way the sky becomes bright and serene when the North Wind (Borea) blows from its gentlest side, purging the air of the haze and fog that previously obscured the view, allowing the heavens to "smile" with all their beauty. Similarly, once Beatrice provides her clear answer, Dante's confusion vanishes, and the truth becomes as evident and luminous to him as a star in the sky.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator. He is describing his internal transition from doubt to understanding.
*   **Borea (the North Wind)** is the subject of the simile, the force that clears the air.

## Scene 88-96: The Multiplication of Sparks
**Scene Analysis: The Multiplication of Sparks**

**What happens:**
Once Beatrice finishes speaking, the visual spectacle of the Empyrean intensifies. Dante describes the concentric circles of light as beginning to spark and glitter, using a simile comparing the effect to iron that sparks when it is boiling. These sparks are not mere light, but the countless souls/angels of the celestial hierarchies. Dante notes that the number of these sparks is unfathomable, referencing the exponential growth of the "chessboard" problem (where grains of wheat double on each square) to emphasize their vast quantity. Dante then shifts from sight to sound: he hears these countless beings singing praises in choirs to the "fixed point" (the central light of God). He observes that this fixed point is what governs their positions, holding them in their specific locations (*ubi*) eternally, just as they have always been.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator and observer. He describes the visual transformation of the circles into sparks and listens to the divine music.
*   **Beatrice** is mentioned implicitly; her silence marks the transition from the intellectual explanation to the sensory experience.
*   **The sparks/souls/angels** are the active participants who glitter and sing praises in choirs.
*   **The "fixed point" (God)** is the center of the universe and the divine force that maintains the eternal positions of the celestial beings.

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **[1:Io]**: Dante.
*   **[2:li]**: The sparks/souls/angels.
*   **[3:+elli]**: The sparks/souls/angels.

## Scene 97-102: The Highest Angels: Seraphim and Cherubim
**Scene Analysis: The Highest Angels: Seraphim and Cherubim**

**What happens:**
Beatrice, who remains attuned to Dante's inner state, notices that he is still plagued by doubt or confusion. She speaks to clarify what he has been seeing. She identifies the first (innermost) circles of light as the Seraphim and the Cherubim. She explains their nature: they move with extreme velocity because they are driven by an intense desire to resemble the "point" (the central light of God). Beatrice clarifies the divine logic: their capacity to mirror God is directly proportional to their capacity to see Him; the more "sublime" they are in their vision of God, the more they are able to resemble Him.

**Who does what and who speaks:**

*   **Dante** is the listener. He is the one whose "doubting thoughts" are noticed by Beatrice.
*   **The Seraphim and Cherubim** are the subjects of Beatrice's explanation; they are the beings moving rapidly in the first circles, striving to mirror God through their vision.

## Scene 103-108: The Thrones and the Nature of Knowledge
**Scene Analysis: The Thrones and the Nature of Knowledge**

**What happens:**
Beatrice continues her systematic explanation of the angelic hierarchies. After describing the Seraphim and Cherubim, she identifies the third order of angels located in the next concentric circle of light. She calls them the "Thrones of the divine aspect." She explains that these three groups together (Seraphim, Cherubim, and Thrones) constitute the "first triad" (the first three orders of the nine celestial hierarchies). Beatrice further explains the spiritual nature of the Thrones: they find their delight in the knowledge of the Truth. This knowledge is tied to the depth of their vision; the deeper their vision penetrates into the Divine Truth, the more they find the peace and stability where every intellect eventually rests.

**Who does what and who speaks:**

*   **Dante** is the listener, absorbing this revelation about the structure of the celestial orders.

*   **The Seraphim and Cherubim** are the ones the Thrones are circling.

## Scene 109-114: The Essence of Beatitude
**Scene Analysis: The Essence of Beatitude**

**What happens:**
Beatrice provides a fundamental theological lesson on the nature of divine happiness (beatitude). She explains the causal sequence of the spiritual experience in Paradise: she asserts that the state of being blessed is founded upon the *act of seeing* (the intellectual vision of God), rather than the *act of loving* (which is a consequence that follows the vision). Beatrice clarifies that the reward is proportional to the vision, and this reward in turn produces grace and goodwill. She concludes that this is the logical progression—from degree to degree—of the spiritual ascent.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Beatrice** is the speaker. She is articulating the metaphysical laws of beatitude to her pupil.
*   **Dante** is the listener. He is receiving the explanation of how the blessed spirits (including the angels he is observing) experience their relationship with God.
*   **The blessed soul/intellect** is the implicit subject of the process Beatrice describes (the entity that sees, loves, and proceeds through the degrees of grace).

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **[1:si]**: This is an impersonal pronoun ("one can see" or "it can be seen"). In the context of the dialogue, it refers to the observer who is analyzing the structure Beatrice has described, which is **Dante**.
*   **[2:si]**: This is another impersonal pronoun ("one proceeds"). It refers to the subject of the process of beatitude—the **blessed soul** (or the "intellect" mentioned in the previous scene) that moves through the described stages of vision, love, and grace.

## Scene 115-120: The Hymn of the Third Order
**Scene Analysis: The Hymn of the Third Order**

**What happens:**
Dante describes the second triad of the angelic hierarchy (which, according to the celestial order, consists of the Dominions, Virtues, and Powers). He notes that this triad "sprouts" in an eternal spring, contrasting the Empyrean with the earthly world where the constellation Aries (the sign of the spring equinox) marks a season that eventually ends. In this eternal realm, the angels perpetually sing "Hosanna." This praise is delivered through three distinct melodies, which organize themselves into three orders of joy. This joy then permeates or penetrates (likely into the souls of the blessed or the heart of the experience).

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator. He is observing and describing the second triad of angels and the nature of their eternal song.
*   **The second triad of angels** (the Dominions, Virtues, and Powers) are the active participants; they are the ones singing "Hosanna" in three melodies.
*   **Joy (letizia)** is the force that penetrates/enters as a result of the music.

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **{1:L'altro ternaro}**: The second triad of angels (composed of the Dominions, Virtues, and Powers).
*   **[2:+egli]**: The second triad of angels (the members of the triad who are singing).
*   **{3:tre melode}**: The three melodies produced by the second triad.
*   **[4:+ella]**: Joy (*letizia*), the feminine noun in the previous phrase, which is the force that penetrates or enters.

## Scene 127-129: The Universal Influence of the Spheres
**Scene Analysis: The Universal Influence of the Spheres**

**What happens:**
Dante describes the dual movement and influence of the nine angelic hierarchies. First, he notes that these orders exist in a state of perpetual wonder or adoration (*ammirano*). Second, he explains their relationship with the material world ("below"): they exert such a powerful influence over the lower realms that they effectively "conquer" or prevail over them. Finally, Dante describes a cosmic circuit of attraction: the angels are themselves drawn toward God, and by virtue of that attraction, they in turn draw all of creation toward God. This establishes the angels as the essential intermediaries through which divine will and love flow from the Creator to the created universe.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator. He is describing the metaphysical function of the angelic orders.
*   **The angelic orders** (the nine hierarchies) are the subjects of the description. They are the ones who admire, influence the lower world, are drawn toward God, and pull others toward God.
*   **God** is the "fixed point" and the ultimate attractor toward whom all movement is directed.

## Scene 130-135: Dionysius and Gregory
In this scene, Dante provides a brief but poignant reflection on the limits of human theology when confronted with the actual reality of the Divine. He mentions two influential church figures—Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite and Pope Gregory the Great—to illustrate the difference between intellectual speculation and direct spiritual vision.

**What happens:**
Dante explains that Dionysius applied himself with great passion to studying and classifying the angelic orders, eventually naming and distinguishing them in the way that Dante himself is currently doing (cataloging them as he observes them). However, Dante notes that Gregory the Great took a different approach (or arrived at a different conclusion). Gregory's perspective changed upon entering Paradise: as soon as he opened his eyes and saw the actual celestial hierarchy, he laughed at his own previous attempts to categorize and define the angels, realizing how inadequate human classification is compared to the actual divine mystery.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Dante** is the narrator. He is comparing his own current descriptive task with the historical efforts of Dionysius and Gregory.
*   **Dionysius** is the theologian who worked hard to map out the angelic hierarchies.
*   **Gregory** is the Pope who, upon reaching the Empyrean, recognized the folly of such human attempts at classification.

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **{1:Dïonisio}**: Dionysius the Areopagite.
*   **[2:+elli]**: The nine angelic orders/hierarchies.
*   **[3:si]**: Dionysius (he applied *himself* to the task).
*   **[4:io]**: Dante (who is currently naming and distinguishing the orders).
*   **{5:Gregorio}**: Pope Gregory the Great.
*   **[6:lui]**: Dionysius (Gregory diverged from Dionysius's approach).
*   **[7:si]**: Gregory (he diverged/separated himself).
*   **[8:+elli]**: Gregory (the one who opened his eyes in the Empyrean).
*   **[9:sé]**: Gregory (laughing at himself).
*   **[10:+elli]**: Gregory (the one who laughed).

## Scene 136-139: The Final Revelation
**Scene Analysis: The Final Revelation**

**What happens:**
Beatrice concludes her lecture on the angelic hierarchies by addressing the discrepancy between divine reality and human theological understanding. She tells Dante that if this "secret truth" (the complex nature of the celestial circles) were spoken to a mortal on earth, he should not be surprised (or "wonder") at the difference. She explains that the person who actually saw the truth "up here" in the Empyrean (referring back to Gregory the Great from the previous scene) was the one who revealed it to humans on earth, but he did so by adapting the truth—using a version of the truth that was different from the absolute reality to make it accessible or appropriate for mortal minds. Essentially, she is explaining that human theology is a translation or a simplified approximation of the divine truth.

**Who does what and who speaks:**
*   **Beatrice** is the speaker. She is providing a final metaphysical clarification on how divine truths are transmitted to humanity.
*   **Dante** is the listener. Beatrice is instructing him not to be surprised by the gap between what he is seeing now and what he has read in earthly texts.
*   **Gregory the Great** (referenced as the one who "saw it up here") is the bridge between the two worlds—the one who witnessed the divine order and then communicated a version of it to mortals.
*   **Mankind/Mortals** are the recipients of the adapted truth on earth.

**Tag Resolutions:**
*   **{1:mortale}**: Mankind / a mortal person on earth.
*   **[2:+io]**: Beatrice (the speaker).
*   **[3:+tu]**: Dante (the listener).
*   **[4:chi]**: Gregory the Great (the one who saw the truth in the Empyrean).
*   **[5:+lui]**: Gregory the Great (the one who revealed the truth to mortals).
