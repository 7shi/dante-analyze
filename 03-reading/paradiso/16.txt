# Canto 16 — Cacciaguida's Prophecy and the Lament for Florence

## Scene 1-12: Reflections on Lineage and Time
In this scene, Cacciaguida is speaking to Dante. Having just finished his autobiography, Cacciaguida reflects on the nature of lineage and the futility of earthly pride. He argues that boasting about "nobility of blood" is meaningless to him now that he is in Heaven, where human appetite/desire is no longer "twisted" (distorted by earthly passion). However, he admits that in the purity of Heaven, he *did* take pride in his lineage, not as a matter of vanity, but as a reflection of divine will. He warns that family prestige is like a "cloak" that shrinks over time unless the descendants actively maintain it through virtue and merit. He then shifts his focus back to the very beginning of his family line to illustrate the passage of time.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `[1:te]` (you): The nobility of their blood (*nobiltà di sangue*).
*   `{2:la gente}` (the people): Dante's kinsmen/descendants (those still living "down there" on Earth).
*   `[3:+tu]` (you): Dante's kinsmen/descendants.
*   `[4:mi]` (me): Cacciaguida.
*   `[5:+io]` (I): Cacciaguida.
*   `[6:io]` (I): Cacciaguida.
*   `[7:me]` (myself): Cacciaguida.
*   `[8:tu]` (you): The nobility of their blood (*nobiltà di sangue*).
*   `[9:+elli]` (they): Dante's kinsmen/descendants.
*   `[10:voi]` (you): The first ancestor of the family who came to Rome.
*   `[11:s']` (yourself): The first ancestor of the family who came to Rome.
*   `{12:la sua famiglia}` (his family): The family of the first ancestor who came to Rome.

## Scene 13-15: Beatrice's Laughter
In this brief scene, the focus shifts from Cacciaguida’s long-winded explanation of genealogy and nobility to Beatrice’s reaction. Beatrice, who had been standing slightly apart and listening in silence, finds Cacciaguida’s preoccupation with earthly lineage somewhat amusing (perhaps because, in the light of Heaven, such concerns are trivial). She lets out a suppressed laugh, which Dante describes as a "cough."

Dante makes a literary allusion here, comparing Beatrice’s modest, stifled laughter to that of Queen Guinevere. Specifically, he refers to the "first written fault" of Guinevere—likely a reference to the moment in Arthurian romance where her suppressed laughter or cough betrayed her hidden passion for Lancelot. This comparison highlights Beatrice's grace and the subtle, human nature of her amusement.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:Beatrice}`: Beatrice.
*   `[2:+ella]`: Beatrice.
*   `[3:quella]`: Guinevere (the woman to whom Beatrice is being compared).
*   `{4:Ginevra}`: Guinevere.

## Scene 16-27: Dante's Inquiry
In this scene, Dante begins to speak, addressing Cacciaguida. He expresses a profound sense of joy and spiritual elevation, telling his ancestor that Cacciaguida’s presence and words give him the confidence to speak boldly and lift his spirit to a state where he feels like a higher, better version of himself. 

Overcome with happiness, Dante then asks Cacciaguida for historical details about their family and the city of Florence. He wants to know about Cacciaguida’s own ancestors, the era of his childhood, the state of the Baptistery of San Giovanni at that time, and who the most prominent and distinguished citizens of the city were during that period.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `[1:Io]`: Dante.
*   `[2:Voi]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `{3:il padre mio}`: Cacciaguida (addressed as "my father" in the sense of a patriarchal ancestor).
*   `[4:voi]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `[5:mi]`: Dante.
*   `[6:voi]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `[7:mi]`: Dante.
*   `[8:i']`: Dante (referring to his current, spiritually elevated state).
*   `[9:io]`: Dante (referring to his usual, earthly self).
*   `[10:+voi]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `{11:cara mia primizia}`: Cacciaguida (meaning "my dear first-ancestor" or "first-fruit").
*   `{12:li vostri antichi}`: Cacciaguida's ancestors.
*   `[13:+voi]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `{14:San Giovanni}`: San Giovanni (the Baptistery of San Giovanni in Florence).
*   `[15:chi]`: The people/citizens.
*   `{16:le genti}`: The people/citizens of Florence.

## Scene 28-39: Cacciaguida's Origins
In this scene, Dante describes the visual and auditory reaction of Cacciaguida to his request for information. Dante notes that Cacciaguida’s spirit (manifested as a light) grows more brilliant, like a coal fanned into flame by the wind. The spirit then speaks to Dante in a voice that is sweet and soft, using an archaic style of language rather than the "modern" vernacular of Dante's time. Cacciaguida begins his answer by calculating the time elapsed since his birth, noting that his mother (who is now a saint) gave birth to him 580 years prior to the current moment, and that his spirit has returned to visit his birthplace that many times.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `[1:io]`: Dante.
*   `{2:luce}`: Cacciaguida (who appears as a light in the Sphere of Jupiter).
*   `[3:+ella]`: The light (Cacciaguida). The feminine pronoun agrees with the Italian noun *luce*.
*   `[4:si]`: The light (Cacciaguida).
*   `[5:+ella]`: The light (Cacciaguida).
*   `{6:mia madre}`: Cacciaguida's mother.
*   `[7:s']`: Cacciaguida's mother.
*   `[8:me]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `[9:+ella]`: Cacciaguida's mother (referring to her being "grave," or heavy/pregnant with child).

## Scene 40-48: The Ancestral Boundaries
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues to answer Dante's questions about his origins and the history of Florence. He begins by placing his birth and that of his ancestors in a specific temporal and spatial context, utilizing astrological imagery (the "annual game" refers to the zodiac). He decides that it is more dignified to remain silent about the specific identities and origins of his forefathers rather than dwelling on genealogical details. Finally, he provides a striking comparison of the city's growth: he notes that the number of men capable of bearing arms in his day (situated between the influence of Mars, the god of war, and the protection of St. John the Baptist) was only one-fifth of the population of Florence in Dante's current time.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:Li antichi miei}`: Cacciaguida's ancestors.
*   `[2:io]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `[3:quei]`: The signs of the zodiac (or the divisions of the year).
*   `{4:i miei maggiori}`: Cacciaguida's ancestors.
*   `[5:chi]`: Cacciaguida's ancestors.
*   `[6:ei]`: Cacciaguida's ancestors.
*   `[7:si]`: Cacciaguida's ancestors.
*   `[8:+elli]`: Cacciaguida's ancestors.
*   `[9:color]`: The citizens of Florence during Cacciaguida's time.
*   `{10:Marte}`: Mars (the god of war).
*   `{11:l Batista}`: St. John the Baptist.
*   `[12:+elli]`: The citizens of Florence during Cacciaguida's time.
*   `[13:quei]`: The citizens of Florence in Dante's time.

## Scene 49-57: The Dilution of Citizenship
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his historical critique of Florence, shifting from the general size of the population to the *quality* of the citizenry. He laments that the citizenship of Florence has become "mixed" (polluted) by the influx of people from the surrounding countryside (Campi, Certaldo, and Fegghine). He argues that the city's purity was only present in the "last art" (the final era of the old, pure nobility). Cacciaguida expresses a strong preference that these rural folk had remained as neighbors in their own villages (like Galluzzo and Trespiano) rather than moving into the city. He concludes with a visceral expression of disgust, claiming that having these people inside the city walls means enduring the "stench" of peasants from Aguglion and Signa, whom he characterizes as greedy hagglers.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:ultimo artista}`: This refers to the last period or era of purity (the era of Cacciaguida's own time), as opposed to the "mixed" era of Dante's time.
*   `{2:quelle genti}`: The rural inhabitants/peasants (specifically those from Campi, Certaldo, and Fegghine).
*   `[3:io]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `{4:del villan d'Aguglion}`: The peasant/villager from Aguglion.
*   `[5:quel]`: The peasant/villager from Signa.

## Scene 58-66: The Loss of Florentine Virtue
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his scathing critique of the modern Florentine character. He contrasts the noble, land-owning spirit of his own time with the mercantile greed of Dante's time. 

Cacciaguida uses a hypothetical, slightly complex metaphor involving Julius Caesar to illustrate the "aberrant" nature of the current citizens. He suggests that the modern Florentines are so driven by profit and greed that if they had existed in the time of Caesar, their nature would have been as cruel as a stepmother's. However, he pivots to the specific tragedy of the Florentine nobility: the great families have abandoned their ancestral lands and the virtues associated with them. He lists specific estates (Simifonti, Montemurlo, Acone, Valdigrieve) and the families (the Counts, the Cerchi, the Buondelmonti) who should have remained in those rural strongholds of nobility rather than becoming polluted by the city's mercantile corruption.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:la gente}`: The modern citizens of Florence (specifically the mercantile, greedy class).
*   `{2:Cesare}`: Julius Caesar.
*   `{3:noverca}`: The modern Florentines (metaphorically described as a "stepmother" to Caesar due to their cruel/aberrant nature).
*   `{4:madre}`: The modern Florentines (metaphorically described as a "mother" in the hypothetical "if" clause).
*   `{5:figlio}`: The son of Julius Caesar.
*   `{6:l'avolo}`: Cacciaguida’s ancestor (the same first ancestor who migrated from Rome mentioned in Scene 1).
*   `{7:de' Conti}`: The Conti family (the Counts).
*   `{8:i Cerchi}`: The Cerchi family (a prominent Florentine family).
*   `{9:i Buondelmonti}`: The Buondelmonti family (another prominent Florentine family).

## Scene 67-78: The Instability of Cities
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his critique of Florence, moving from the specific corruption of the nobility to a general sociological theory about the decline of cities. He argues that "confusion"—the mixing of incompatible social classes and interests—is the root cause of a city's ruin. He uses a series of analogies to illustrate this: he compares the mixture of social classes to a poor diet (referring to Dante's own modern food), and uses metaphors of blind animals and swords to argue that focused, unified strength is superior to a fragmented, disorganized crowd. To prove that the collapse of great families and cities is a common historical pattern, he lists several abandoned or decaying cities (Luni, Orbisaglia, Chiusi, and Sinigaglia). He tells Dante that the disintegration of noble lineages should not surprise him, as all cities eventually reach their end.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:le persone}`: The different social classes of people (specifically the mixing of the nobility with the mercantile/peasant classes).
*   `[2:tu]`: Dante.
*   `{3:le schiatte}`: The lineages/noble families (specifically the Florentine noble families mentioned in the previous scene).
*   `[4:si]`: The lineages/noble families (reflexive: they unravel *themselves*).
*   `[5:ti]`: Dante.

## Scene 79-87: The Unpredictability of Fortune
In this scene, Cacciaguida provides a philosophical justification for the decline of Florence. He argues that everything created by humans is mortal and destined to end, just as humans themselves are mortal. He uses a natural metaphor—the moon's gravitational pull causing the tides to cover and uncover the shores—to describe the cyclical and unpredictable nature of Fortune. He warns Dante that because Fortune operates this way, it should not be surprising that the noble families of Florence will eventually lose their prestige and fame. This serves as a transition, preparing Dante for the specific, harsh prophecy about his city and his own future that is about to follow.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `[1:voi]`: Dante's kinsmen/descendants (or more broadly, human beings, as he is contrasting the longevity of "things" with the shortness of human lives).
*   `{2:la Fortuna}`: Fortune (the personified force that governs earthly luck and success).
*   `[3:io]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `{4:li alti Fiorentini}`: The noble citizens of Florence.

## Scene 88-93: The Catalog of Illustrious Families
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his historical survey of Florence, providing a specific catalog of the noble families that existed during his time. He is essentially listing the "old guard" of the city's aristocracy to illustrate the contrast between the nobility of his era and the corrupted, mercantile society Dante currently inhabits. 

Cacciaguida notes that some families—the Ughi, Catellini, Filippi, Greci, Ormanni, and Alberichi—were already in a state of decline (*nel calare*) during his lifetime. However, he also recalls others who were still as powerful and prestigious as their ancestors, specifically mentioning representatives from the Sannella and Arca families, as well as the Soldanieri, Ardinghi, and Bostichi clans.

## Scene 94-108: The Rise of Corrupt Families
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his historical survey of Florence's nobility, but he begins with a sharp, metaphorical warning about the present. He notes that the city's gates are currently burdened by a "new treachery" so heavy that it will soon sink the "ship" (a metaphor for the city of Florence). This serves as a grim reminder that while he is listing the greatness of the past, the present is in peril.

Cacciaguida then lists the prestigious families that were prominent during his time. He mentions the Ravignani, from whom Count Guido and the "high" Bellincione descended. He notes the political skill of a member of the Pressa family and the wealth of Galigaio (indicated by the gilded sword-hilt and pommel in his home). He continues his catalog by naming several other families: the Vaio, Sacchetti, Giuochi, Fifanti, Barucci, and Galli. He also makes a pointed reference to those who "redden by the staio" (grain-measurers), hinting at the encroachment of mercantile interests. Finally, he mentions the ascent of the Calfucci, Sizii, and Arrigucci families to the "curule" seats of power (a reference to the Roman chairs of magistracy).

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:il conte Guido}`: Count Guido (a member of the Ravignani family).
*   `{2:l'alto Bellincione}`: Bellincione (Dante's grandfather and a member of the Ravignani lineage).
*   `[3:Quel]`: A member of the Pressa family.
*   `{4:Galigaio}`: Galigaio (a member of the Pressa family).
*   `{5:del Vaio}`: The Vaio family (or a representative of that family).
*   `{6:Sacchetti}`: The Sacchetti family.
*   `{7:Giuochi}`: The Giuochi family.
*   `{8:Fifanti}`: The Fifanti family.
*   `{9:Barucci}`: The Barucci family.
*   `{10:Galli}`: The Galli family.
*   `[11:quei]`: The people who "redden by the staio" (referring to the mercantile class, specifically grain dealers).
*   `{12:i Calfucci}`: The Calfucci family.
*   `{13:Sizii}`: The Sizii family.
*   `{14:Arrigucci}`: The Arrigucci family.

## Scene 109-118: The Pride of the New Class
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his historical and sociological critique of Florence. He laments the fall of the old nobility and the rise of a new, corrupt class. He begins by noting that some of the great families he once knew were destroyed by their own hubris (pride). He then contrasts the true nobility of the past with the current officials of the city. He observes that the people currently holding power in the *Consistoro* (the city's governing council) are merely getting rich and "fat" through their offices. 

Cacciaguida specifically mocks a "degenerate" or "over-cooked" lineage (*oltracotata schiatta*). He describes these people as hypocritical and cowardly: they flee from those they fear, act aggressively toward those they can intimidate, and become docile and submissive (like lambs) when money (the purse) is involved. He concludes by noting that while this class has risen to power, they are not truly noble, as they come from "lowly people" (*picciola gente*).

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `[1:io]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `[2:quei]`: The old noble families who were destroyed by their own pride.
*   `{3:i padri}`: The ancestors of the current city officials.
*   `[4:coloro]`: The current city officials/rulers who sit in the *Consistoro*.
*   `[5:+elli]`: The current city officials/rulers who sit in the *Consistoro*.
*   `[6:si]`: The current city officials/rulers who sit in the *Consistoro*.
*   `[7:s']`: The degenerate/over-cooked lineage (*l'oltracotata schiatta*).
*   `[8:chi]`: The people whom the degenerate lineage fears.
*   `[9:chi]`: The people whom the degenerate lineage threatens.
*   `[10:si]`: The degenerate lineage (which becomes docile like a lamb).
*   `[11:+ella]`: The degenerate/over-cooked lineage (*l'oltracotata schiatta*).

## Scene 119-126: The Shifts in the Marketplace
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his scathing list of the "new men" who have infiltrated and corrupted the Florentine social order. He mentions Ubertin Donato, who was displeased that his father-in-law created a familial tie between him and the degenerate class Cacciaguida is describing. He then names specific figures—Caponsacco (who came down from Fiesole), as well as Giuda and Infangato—ironically calling them "good citizens." Cacciaguida concludes by noting a specific detail: these new arrivals entered the city's small perimeter through the Porta della Pera.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:Ubertin Donato}`: Ubertin Donato.
*   `{2:il suocero}`: Ubertin Donato's father-in-law.
*   `[3:il]`: Ubertin Donato (the person the father-in-law made a relative to the other group).
*   `{4:parente}`: Ubertin Donato (the person made a relative).
*   `{5:'l Caponsacco}`: Caponsacco (a newcomer/merchant from Fiesole).
*   `{6:buon cittadino}`: Giuda and Infangato (described ironically as "good citizens").
*   `{7:Giuda}`: Giuda.
*   `{8:Infangato}`: Infangato.
*   `[9:Io]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `[10:s']`: The new citizens/mercantile class (the "one" or "they" who entered the city; used here in an impersonal construction to refer to the influx of people like Caponsacco, Giuda, and Infangato).
*   `[11:quei]`: The people associated with the Pera gate (the namesakes of the *Porta della Pera*).

## Scene 127-135: The Loss of Privileges
In this scene, Cacciaguida continues his sociological critique of Florence, focusing on the erosion of the traditional class boundaries. He speaks of the old nobility who once derived their military status and legal privileges from the Emperor (the "great baron"), whose prestige was reaffirmed during the feast of St. Thomas. Cacciaguida laments a modern shift: the noblemen, once distinguished by their fringed sashes, are now mingling with the *popolo* (the common/mercantile class). He mentions the Gualterotti and Importuni families as examples of this shifting social landscape. He concludes by stating that the Borgo district of Florence would be far more peaceful if it were not for the influx of "new neighbors"—the nouveau riche and mercantile newcomers who have disrupted the city's traditional order.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{1:del gran barone}`: The Emperor (the Holy Roman Emperor).
*   `{2:Tommaso}`: St. Thomas (likely St. Thomas Aquinas, whose feast day is linked here to the prestige of the nobility).
*   `[3:esso]`: The Emperor.
*   `[4:+ei]`: The noblemen/imperial vassals (those who bear the "beautiful banner").
*   `[5:si]`: The noblemen/imperial vassals.
*   `[6:colui]`: The nobleman (represented by the one who wears the fringed sash).
*   `{7:Gualterotti}`: The Gualterotti family.
*   `{8:Importuni}`: The Importuni family.
*   `{9:novi vicin}`: The "new neighbors" (the mercantile/newly wealthy class who have moved into the city).

## Scene 136-144: The Tragedy of Buondelmonte
In this scene, Cacciaguida explains a pivotal historical event that set the stage for the eventual ruin and exile of Dante’s own family. He refers to the "house" (the family) of Buondelmonte, stating that this family was once honorable. However, a specific tragedy occurred involving Buondelmonte's failed romantic pursuit of a woman named Emma. Cacciaguida laments that Buondelmonte mistakenly avoided the marriage because he listened to the advice of others. He suggests that if God had allowed the union between Buondelmonte and Emma the first time Buondelmonte entered the city, the subsequent cycle of violence and the "just indignation" that eventually led to Dante's own sorrow and exile would have been avoided.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `[1:v']` (you): Dante and his kinsmen/descendants (the "you" plural refers to the lineage that suffered the consequences).
*   `{2:suoi consorti}`: The kinsmen/relatives of the Buondelmonti family.
*   `{3:Buondelmonte}`: Buondelmonte (Dante's ancestor).
*   `[4:+tu]` (you): Buondelmonte.
*   `{5:Dio}`: God.
*   `[6:t']` (you): Buondelmonte.
*   `{7:Ema}`: Emma (the woman Buondelmonte loved).
*   `[8:+tu]` (you): Buondelmonte.

## Scene 145-154: The Vision of a Lost Peace
In this scene, Cacciaguida evokes a powerful image of a lost golden age. He directs Dante's attention to a specific location—a "stupid stone" by the bridge (likely the Ponte Vecchio)—which served as a silent witness to the end of Florence's last era of peace. Cacciaguida reminisces about a time when the city was inhabited by the noble families he previously listed, a period when Florence was so stable, just, and unified that there was no cause for grief. He describes the city's population as glorious and just, noting that the city's symbol, the lily, was never displayed in a way that suggested retreat or stained red by the blood of internal civil strife.

**Referent Analysis:**

*   `{3:queste genti}`: The noble families of Cacciaguida's time (the virtuous aristocracy he has been listing).
*   `[4:esse]`: The noble families of Cacciaguida's time.
*   `[5:io]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `{6:Fiorenza}`: Florence (the city).
*   `[7:+ella]`: Florence (the city).
*   `[8:+ella]`: Florence (the city).
*   `{9:queste genti}`: The noble families of Cacciaguida's time.
*   `[10:io]`: Cacciaguida.
*   `{11:il popol suo}`: The people of Florence (the citizenry during that peaceful, just era).
