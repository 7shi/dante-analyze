# Canto 06 — Farinata's Imperial Monologue and Historical Survey

## Scene 1-9: The Movement of the Imperial Eagle
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, the radiant spirit (the luminary) is speaking to Dante and Beatrice. He is providing a historical and symbolic survey of the Roman Empire, represented by the "Imperial Eagle." 

The speaker explains that Constantine shifted the seat of imperial power (the Eagle) away from its original course. He traces the lineage of this authority back to Aeneas (the "ancient one" who married Lavinia). He describes how the Empire remained in the East (the extremity of Europe, near the mountains of its origin) for over two hundred years. During this time, the Empire governed the world through a succession of rulers ("from hand to hand") until the imperial authority eventually returned to the speaker's own native land (Italy).

## Scene 10-27: The Imperial Persona and Spiritual Journey
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, the radiant spirit reveals his identity to Dante and Beatrice. He identifies himself as the Emperor Justinian. 

Justinian explains his life's work and spiritual journey. First, he mentions his legal reforms, noting that he pruned the Roman laws of everything "excessive and vain," referring to the creation of the *Corpus Juris Civilis*. 

He then confesses a past theological error: he once believed in Monophysitism (the belief that Christ had only one nature). However, he was corrected and led back to the "sincere faith" (orthodoxy) by Pope Agapetus I. Having aligned himself with the Church, Justinian claims that God granted him the inspiration for his "high work" (the law code). He explains that he dedicated himself entirely to God and handed the military command over to his general, Belisarius. He interprets Belisarius's divine success as a sign from heaven that it was time for Justinian to stop his military pursuits and focus on his legislative work.

## Scene 28-33: The Significance of the Political Sign
**Narrative Analysis**
In this brief scene, Emperor Justinian is transitioning from his personal history to answering Dante's original question. He explains that in order to provide a clear answer, he must first establish some logical groundwork ("alcuna giunta"). His goal is to help Dante understand the reasoning behind the conflict surrounding the "most holy sign" (the Imperial Eagle/the Roman Empire)—specifically, why some people claim the authority of the Empire for themselves and why others fight against it.

## Scene 34-48: The Glory of the Roman Lineage
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, Emperor Justinian continues his historical survey for Dante, emphasizing the inherent virtue and divine legitimacy of the Roman Empire, symbolized by the Imperial Eagle. He argues that the Empire's worthiness of reverence is proven by its long and storied history.

Justinian traces the timeline of the Empire's ascent: it began with the death of Pallas, which cleared the way for Aeneas's lineage to establish their rule. He mentions the three centuries spent in Alba Longa, leading up to the conflict among the three brothers (Romulus, Remus, and Numitor) for control of the throne. He then summarizes the early history of Rome, moving from the abduction of the Sabine women to the tragedy of Lucretia and the era of the seven kings. Finally, he highlights the military triumphs of the Roman Republic, mentioning the victories over Brennus and Pyrrhus, and praising the fame earned by legendary Roman figures such as Torquatus, Quinzio, the Decii, and the Fabii.

## Scene 49-54: The Expansion of Imperial Power
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, Emperor Justinian continues his historical survey of the Roman Empire's triumphs to demonstrate its divine legitimacy. He describes how the Roman Empire (the "Eagle") crushed the pride of the Carthaginians (referred to here as "Arabs"), who had crossed the Alps following their leader, Hannibal. In this passage, Justinian briefly addresses the Po River, noting its flow. He then highlights that under the symbol of the Empire, young Roman generals achieved great fame and victory, specifically naming Scipio (Africanus) and Pompey. Finally, he mentions the hill under which Dante was born (Florence), suggesting that the Roman conquest of that region was a bitter experience for the defeated inhabitants.

## Scene 55-63: The Era of Julius Caesar
In this scene, Emperor Justinian continues his historical survey for Dante and Beatrice. He describes the transition of the Roman world toward a more orderly state, asserting that this was the will of the divine. He highlights the rise of Julius Caesar, who took power by the will of Rome. Justinian then lists Caesar's military conquests in Gaul and Germany—mentioning the Var, the Rhine, the Isar, the Ers, the Seine, and the Rhone rivers—to emphasize the vastness of his achievements. Finally, he mentions Caesar's crossing of the Rubicon, an act of such significance and "flight" (ambition/speed) that Justinian claims no human speech or writing could possibly do justice to the scale of the event.

## Scene 64-72: The Global Reach of the Empire
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, Emperor Justinian continues his historical survey, detailing the vast geographical scope of Julius Caesar's military campaigns to illustrate the expansive reach of the Roman Empire. 

Justinian describes Caesar's movements across the known world: first turning his army toward Spain, then to Dyrrachium and Farsalus (Greece), and eventually reaching as far as the Nile in Egypt. He mentions Caesar's visit to the regions of the Antander and Simois rivers, where he visited the burial place of the Trojan hero Hector. Justinian then describes Caesar's interventions regarding Ptolemy in Egypt and his campaign against Juba in North Africa. Finally, he describes Caesar's return toward the West, where he faced the forces of Pompey, signaled by the sounding of the Pompeian trumpets. This sequence serves to emphasize the sheer scale of Caesar's conquests and the divine-like energy with which he expanded the Empire's borders.

## Scene 73-81: The Tragedies of War and the Era of Peace
In this scene, Emperor Justinian continues his historical survey, transitioning from the conquests of Julius Caesar to the turbulence and eventual peace that followed his death.

Justinian begins by referencing Julius Caesar ([1]), the leader who commanded the Roman legions (represented by the "standard-bearer" or "eagle-bearer" {2} who followed him). He notes that because of Caesar's actions and the subsequent fallout, the conspirators Brutus {3} and Cassius {4} are now condemned to "bark" in Hell. He mentions the suffering of the cities of Modena and Perugia, which were devastated by the civil wars following the assassination.

The narrative then shifts to Cleopatra {5}, who is remembered as sorrowful. Justinian describes her flight from the assassins and her sudden, dark death by the bite of an asp. He notes that she had once run to the "red shore" (Egypt) with Caesar ([6]). 

Justinian then contrasts this era of chaos with the era of peace. He explains that with Augustus ([8]), the Imperial Eagle ([9])—the symbol of Roman authority—brought such great peace to the world that the temple of the god Janus {10} (whose doors were opened during war and closed during peace) was finally shut.

## Scene 82-90: The Third Caesar and Divine Justice
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, Emperor Justinian reflects on the evolving nature of the Imperial Eagle's authority. He explains that the power of the Empire—which had served various purposes for the mortal kingdom—takes on a different appearance when held by the "third Caesar" (Constantine). 

Justinian argues that when viewed with a clear and pure perspective, one can see that "living justice" (a divine quality that Justinian claims also animates himself) was granted to this third Caesar. This divine justice gave the third Caesar the authority and the "glory" to execute vengeance and punish those who opposed the divine will. Essentially, Justinian is justifying the use of imperial power as an instrument of God's justice.

## Scene 97-111: The Corruption of the Factions
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, Emperor Justinian shifts from a general historical survey to a specific critique of the political factions of Dante's time. He explains to Dante that the current suffering of Italy is caused by those who misuse or oppose the "public sign" (the Imperial Eagle).

Justinian identifies two types of offenders: the Guelphs, who oppose the Imperial Eagle by promoting the "yellow lilies" (the symbol of the French monarchy/House of Anjou), and the Ghibellines, who claim the Imperial Eagle for their own private interests rather than for the pursuit of divine justice. He argues that any faction—specifically the Ghibellines—that separates the Empire from justice is destined for failure.

He then issues a direct warning to Charles of Anjou ("this new Charles"), who, supported by the Guelphs, seeks to undermine imperial authority. Justinian warns Charles to fear the "talons" of the Eagle, reminding him that the Empire's predecessors (the "higher lions") were far more powerful and legitimate. The scene concludes with a theological assertion: that children often suffer for the sins of their fathers, and that God will not abandon the Imperial Eagle (His chosen instrument of justice) in favor of the French lilies.

## Scene 112-123: The Cosmic Balance of Merit
In this scene, Emperor Justinian explains the theological and moral nature of the sphere of Mercury (the "small star"). He is speaking to Dante and Beatrice, shifting from the history of the Roman Empire to an explanation of why certain souls are placed in this specific heaven.

Justinian explains that this sphere is populated by the "good spirits" who, during their earthly lives, were driven by a desire for honor and fame. Because their primary motivation was this earthly ambition (which is a "diversion" from pure divine love), the "rays of true love" (God's light) reach them with less intensity than those in the higher spheres.

However, Justinian notes that there is a specific joy in this state: the inhabitants find happiness in the perfect proportionality between their reward and their merit. Because they perceive that they have received exactly what they deserve—no more and no less—they feel a profound sense of peace. This "living justice" eliminates any possibility of envy or resentment toward those in higher heavens, ensuring that their affection remains pure and free from wickedness.

## Scene 124-135: The Harmony of the Troubadours
**Narrative Analysis**

In this scene, Emperor Justinian uses a musical metaphor to describe the harmony of the souls in the sphere of Mercury. He explains that just as different voices create a sweet melody, the different "seats" (levels of merit) of the spirits in this heaven create a divine harmony.

To illustrate the principle of divine justice he discussed in the previous scene, Justinian points out a specific radiant spirit, Romeo (referring to the troubadour Raimbaut). He notes that while Romeo's work was great and beautiful, it was unappreciated during his lifetime. Justinian points out the irony of divine justice: the Provençals who opposed Romeo are not "laughing" (meaning they are not in Paradise), serving as a warning that those who seek to harm the goodness of others will suffer. 

Justinian then mentions Romeo's legacy, noting that he had four daughters who each became a queen, and mentions his connection to Raymond Berenguer. He concludes by describing Romeo as a humble and pilgrim person.

## Scene 136-142: A Tribute to the Humble Wanderer
In this scene, the narrative focus shifts as Romeo (the troubadour) begins to speak. He addresses Emperor Justinian in a poetic or indirect manner ("parole biece"). Romeo asks for the reasoning behind the specific measure of merit and reward he was granted—symbolized by the mathematical phrase "seven and five for ten." He reflects on his earthly existence, noting that he left the world in a state of poverty and old age. Romeo observes that if the world had truly known the nobility and purity of the heart he possessed while living a life of begging for scraps, the world would have praised him greatly, and even more so than they do now.
