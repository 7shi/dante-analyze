# Canto 27 — The Entrance into the Fire and the Ascent to the Earthly Paradise

## Scene 1-6: The Solar Metaphor
**Scene Analysis: The Solar Metaphor**

**What happens:**
The scene opens with a complex astronomical description used to establish the exact time and date of the action. Dante describes the position of the sun as it begins to set, referencing its alignment with the Iberian Peninsula and the Ganges River (likely indicating the autumnal equinox). This atmospheric setup serves as a cinematic transition: just as the day is ending, a joyful angel suddenly appears before the travelers.

**Who does what:**
*   **Dante (the narrator):** Observes and describes the celestial alignment and the arrival of the divine messenger.
*   **The Angel:** Appears before the group.

**Who speaks to whom:**
There is no dialogue in these six lines; it is purely narrative description.

**Resolution of Numbered Tags:**
*   `{1:il suo fattor}`: **Jesus Christ**. (The "maker" who shed his blood on the cross).
*   `{2:l'angel di Dio}`: **The Angel of God** (specifically the guardian angel who stands at the threshold of the Earthly Paradise).
*   `[3:ci]`: **Dante, Virgil, and Statius**. (The group of three poets traveling together).

## Scene 7-13: The Angel's Command
**Scene Analysis: The Angel's Command**

**What happens:**
The angel is positioned on the bank, just outside the purgatorial flames of the Lustful. He is singing the beatitude "Blessed are the pure in heart" in a voice far more vibrant than that of the humans. As Dante, Virgil, and Statius approach, the angel stops singing and delivers a stern command: they cannot proceed any further unless they first pass through the fire. He instructs them to enter the flames and to listen to the singing emanating from the other side.

**Who does what:**

## Scene 14-18: Dante's Terror
**Scene Analysis: Dante's Terror**

**What happens:**
Upon hearing the angel's command that they must pass through the purgatorial fire to move forward, Dante is seized by an overwhelming sense of dread. He describes his reaction using a vivid simile, comparing his state to that of a condemned man being thrown into a pit. Paralyzed by fear, he extends his hands in a gesture of desperation or warding, unable to stop himself from visualizing the horrific sight of burning human bodies he had previously witnessed during his journey through the *Inferno*.

**Who does what:**
*   **Dante:** Reacts with physical and psychological terror to the angel's instructions. He remembers the sights of Hell and shrinks back from the fire.

**Who speaks to whom:**
There is no dialogue in this scene; it is the narrator describing his own internal state and physical reaction.

## Scene 19-32: Virgil's Reassurance
**Scene Analysis: Virgil's Reassurance**

**What happens:**
Seeing Dante paralyzed by fear at the prospect of entering the flames, Virgil and Statius (the "good guides") turn toward him. Virgil speaks with paternal tenderness to calm Dante's panic. He makes a crucial theological distinction: while there is "torment" (pain) in Purgatory, there is no "death" (permanent destruction or the eternal agony of Hell). 

To restore Dante's confidence, Virgil uses a logical argument based on their history: if he was able to lead Dante safely past the monster Gerion in the depths of the *Inferno*, he can certainly protect him here, in a place much closer to God. Virgil assures Dante that the purgatorial fire is not destructive—it wouldn't even singe a single hair on his head, even if he stayed in it for a thousand years. To prove this, Virgil invites Dante to test the fire himself by touching it with the hem of his clothing. The scene ends with Virgil commanding Dante to cast off his fear and step forward into the fire with confidence.

**Who does what:**
*   **Virgil:** Turns to Dante and delivers a reassuring speech, using both a historical reminder and a physical challenge to overcome Dante's fear.
*   **Statius:** Along with Virgil, he turns toward Dante in support.
*   **Dante:** The recipient of Virgil's reassurance, struggling to overcome his trauma from the *Inferno*.

## Scene 37-42: Dante's Conversion
**Scene Analysis: Dante's Conversion**

**What happens:**
Dante describes the moment his fear and paralysis finally break. He uses a classical literary allusion to Pyramus and Thisbe (from Ovid's *Metamorphoses*) to illustrate this shift. Just as Pyramus, in his dying moments, opened his eyes and looked upon Thisbe when he heard/saw her (symbolized by the mulberry tree turning red with his blood), Dante's "hardness" (his stubborn fear) is softened. The catalyst for this change is the mention of Beatrice's name by Virgil in the previous scene. Hearing her name awakens Dante's will, and he finally turns back to Virgil, ready to proceed.

**Who does what:**
*   **Dante (the narrator):** Experiences a sudden emotional and psychological shift. He moves from a state of frozen terror to a state of readiness, physically turning toward his guide.
*   **Pyramus and Thisbe:** Appear in a simile to represent the power of a beloved's name to provoke a reaction, even in the face of death or despair.
*   **Virgil (the "wise guide"):** The object of Dante's attention as Dante turns back to him.

**Who speaks to whom:**
There is no dialogue in this scene; it is a narrative reflection and a description of Dante's physical reaction.

**Resolution of Numbered Tags:**
*   `{1:Tisbe}`: **Thisbe**. (The female protagonist of the Ovidian myth).
*   `{2:Piramo}`: **Pyramus**. (The male protagonist of the Ovidian myth).
*   `[3:+elli]`: **Pyramus**. (The pronoun referring back to Pyramus).

## Scene 43-48: Entering the Fire
**Scene Analysis: Entering the Fire**

**What happens:**
Following the mention of Beatrice's name, Dante is suddenly transformed from a state of frozen terror to a state of eager readiness. Virgil reacts to this sudden change with a mixture of surprise and amusement. He shakes his head and asks rhetorically (and teasingly) if Dante really intended to stay behind on the safe side of the fire. Virgil's smile is compared to that of a parent who has successfully coaxed a child with a piece of fruit. To lead by example and prove the fire is harmless, Virgil steps into the flames first. As he does, he calls for Statius to follow him, noting that they had been separated for a long time (referring to their disparate journeys through the afterlife before reuniting in Purgatory).

**Who does what:**
*   **Virgil:** Reacts with amusement to Dante's change of heart, teases him, leads the way into the purgatorial fire, and invites Statius to follow.
*   **Dante:** Has just experienced his "conversion" and is now ready to proceed; he observes Virgil entering the fire.
*   **Statius:** Is called forward by Virgil to enter the fire.

**Who speaks to whom:**
Virgil speaks to Dante (the teasing question) and then to Statius (the request to follow).

## Scene 49-54: The Intensity of the Flame
**Scene Analysis: The Intensity of the Flame**

**What happens:**
As Dante steps into the purgatorial fire, he is immediately struck by the sheer intensity of the heat. He describes the heat using a paradoxical hyperbole: the fire is so overwhelming ("beyond measure") that he feels as though he would have thrown himself into a vessel of boiling liquid just to find some relief or "cool down" by comparison. To distract Dante from this physical distress and to keep his spirits high, Virgil continues to speak of Beatrice. Virgil's words serve as a psychological anchor, reminding Dante of the goal ahead. Virgil expresses his own anticipation, stating that he feels as if he can already see Beatrice's eyes.

**Who does what:**
*   **Dante:** Enters the fire and experiences an intense, overwhelming heat that causes him immediate distress.
*   **Virgil:** Observes Dante's struggle and actively comforts him by talking about Beatrice to redirect his focus from the pain to the reward.

**Who speaks to whom:**
Virgil speaks to Dante.

## Scene 76-87: The Simile of the Shepherds
**Scene Analysis: The Simile of the Shepherds**

**What happens:**
Dante uses a vivid, pastoral simile to describe the state of the three travelers as they rest on the mountain steps. He compares the group to a flock of goats and their caretakers. He describes goats that are usually fast and headstrong but are now calm and docile, chewing cud in the shade while the sun is hot. These goats are watched over by a shepherd who leans on his staff, providing a sense of security during their rest. He adds another layer to the image with a swineherd who sleeps outdoors beside his herd, remaining vigilant so that a wild beast does not scatter them. Dante then applies the simile to his own situation: he is the docile goat, and his two guides are the watchful shepherds, all of them sheltered by the overhang of the high rock.

**Who does what:**
*   **Dante (the narrator):** Observes the group's state and constructs this metaphor to illustrate their peace and the protective relationship between him and his guides.
*   **Virgil and Statius:** Act as the "shepherds" in the simile, providing the protection and guidance that allow Dante to rest.
*   **Dante (the pilgrim):** Acts as the "goat," the one being cared for and protected.

**Who speaks to whom:**
There is no dialogue in this scene; it is a narrative description using a simile.

## Scene 94-108: The Dream of Lia
**Scene Analysis: The Dream of Lia**

**What happens:**
Dante, having fallen asleep on the rocky path, experiences a vivid dream. He identifies the timing of the dream by referencing the first rays of dawn hitting Mount Cytherea (a poetic reference to Cyprus, the island of Venus). In the dream, he sees a young, beautiful woman walking through a flowery meadow, gathering blooms and singing. The woman identifies herself as Lia and explains that she is weaving a garland to adorn herself so she can look beautiful in a mirror. She contrasts her behavior with that of her sister, Rachel. While Lia finds satisfaction in the *act* of adorning herself (the work), Rachel finds satisfaction in *beholding* her own beauty in the mirror (the vision).

**Who does what:**
*   **Dante:** The dreamer. He observes the woman and listens to her song.
*   **Lia:** The woman in the dream. She gathers flowers, weaves a garland, and describes the difference between her nature and her sister's.
*   **Rachel:** Mentioned by Lia. She is the sister who spends her time gazing into a mirror.

**Who speaks to whom:**
Lia speaks (through song) to anyone who might ask her name, and effectively to Dante, the observer of the dream.

## Scene 124-132: The Limit of Virgil's Guidance
**Scene Analysis: The Limit of Virgil's Guidance**

**What happens:**
The trio reaches the final step of the ascent, completing their climb of the mountain of Purgatory. At this threshold, Virgil stops and looks intently at Dante. He delivers a significant declaration regarding the limits of his role as a guide. Virgil explains that Dante has now seen both the "temporal fire" (the purifying flames of Purgatory) and the "eternal fire" (the punishing flames of Hell). He admits that they have reached a point beyond which he can no longer see or lead the way, as his guidance is based on human reason, intellect, and "art" (poetry/skill), which are insufficient for the realm they are about to enter. Virgil instructs Dante that he must now rely on his own "pleasure"—his longing and desire for Beatrice—to serve as his guide. He informs Dante that he has now moved beyond the "steep ways" and the realm of human art.

*   **Dante, Virgil, and Statius:** Together, they complete the climb to the "highest step" (the entrance to the Earthly Paradise).
*   **Virgil:** Stops the group, fixes his gaze on Dante, and explains the theological and narrative necessity of his departure as a guide.
*   **Dante:** The recipient of Virgil's final instructions, now tasked with transitioning from a reliance on Reason (Virgil) to a reliance on Divine Love/Desire.

## Scene 133-138: The Beauty of the Paradise
**Scene Analysis: The Beauty of the Paradise**

**What happens:**
Following his declaration that his guidance has reached its limit, Virgil directs Dante's attention to the splendor of the environment they have just entered: the Earthly Paradise. He points out the sunlight shining before them and the natural beauty of the flora—the grasses, flowers, and shrubs—that the earth produces spontaneously in this sacred place. Virgil then tells Dante to wait for the arrival of Beatrice. In a poignant reminder of the journey's origin, Virgil mentions that it was Beatrice's weeping (her "beautiful eyes") that prompted him to descend and lead Dante to this point. Finally, Virgil gives Dante the freedom to either sit and wait or wander among the lush vegetation.

**Who does what:**
*   **Virgil:** Acts as the final pointer, directing Dante's gaze toward the physical beauty of the Paradise and the impending arrival of Beatrice. He reminds Dante of the divine impetus (Beatrice's grief) that started the journey.
*   **Dante:** The recipient of these instructions, now standing at the threshold of the Earthly Paradise, encouraged to contemplate nature while awaiting Beatrice.

## Scene 139-142: The Granting of Free Will
**Scene Analysis: The Granting of Free Will**

**What happens:**
In these final lines of the encounter, Virgil officially releases Dante from his tutelage. He tells Dante that he no longer needs to wait for Virgil's words or signals to know how to act. He affirms that Dante's will (*arbitrio*) is now "free, straight, and healthy," meaning that Dante has been purified enough to make his own correct moral and spiritual choices. However, Virgil adds a final caution: Dante must not let his will deviate from reason (*senno*). Virgil concludes by explaining the nature of this transition: he has "crowned and anointed" Dante, effectively elevating him to a state where Dante is now the master of his own journey, surpassing the limited guidance that Human Reason (represented by Virgil) could provide.

**Who does what:**
*   **Virgil:** Relinquishes his role as the active director of Dante's movements, grants Dante autonomy, and warns him to remain guided by reason.
*   **Dante:** The recipient of this autonomy, now entrusted with his own free will as he enters the Earthly Paradise.

**Who speaks to whom:**
Virgil speaks to Dante.
