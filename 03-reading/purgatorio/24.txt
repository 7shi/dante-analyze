# Canto 24 — Recognition of Souls and the Warning of the Tree

## Scene 1-6: The Watching Souls
In this opening scene of Canto 24, the narrative focuses on the movement of the group and the reaction of the surrounding souls to Dante's presence.

Dante, Virgil, and Statius are continuing their progress along the terrace of Avarice. They are engaged in a conversation with Forese, but this dialogue does not hinder their pace; they move forward briskly, compared to a ship driven by a favorable wind. As they walk, the other souls inhabiting this terrace observe them. These souls are struck with wonder when they realize that Dante is a living man, and they stare at him with curiosity.

## Scene 13-24: News of Piccarda and Bonagiunta
In this scene, Forese answers Dante's inquiry about Piccarda and introduces him to other souls on the terrace of Avarice.

Forese begins by informing Dante that his sister, Piccarda, has already completed her purification and is now joyfully triumphant in Paradise (referred to as "high Olympus"). He then explains a particular rule or custom of their current location: unlike some other areas of Purgatory where souls might remain anonymous, here they are permitted to name one another. This is because the "diet" of the terrace (extreme fasting/starvation) has so severely wasted their physical forms that they are no longer recognizable by sight. To illustrate this, Forese points out two specific souls: first, Bonagiunta da Lucca, and second, a woman from the noble Torso family. Forese notes that this woman was once held in high regard by the Church, but is now purging her greed—specifically her appetite for luxury items like the eels of Bolsena and Vernaccia wine.

## Scene 52-63: The Knot of Poets
In this scene, titled "The Knot of Poets," Dante and Bonagiunta da Lucca engage in a brief but significant exchange about poetic style and lineage. 

Dante responds to Bonagiunta's question about the "nine rhymes" by explaining his creative process: he describes himself as a poet who records the inspirations given to him by Love and expresses them according to an inner dictate.

Bonagiunta acknowledges Dante as a "brother" (a fellow poet) and recognizes in Dante's work the "sweet new style" (*dolce stil novo*). He notes that this new style represents a break from the "knot" (the traditional or restrictive poetic conventions) that bound him and his contemporaries, specifically mentioning Guittone d'Arezzo and the Notary. Bonagiunta observes that Dante's writing closely follows the "dictator" (the originator or guiding spirit of this new style), noting that those who try to push beyond these boundaries lose the ability to distinguish between styles. Satisfied with this realization, Bonagiunta falls silent.

## Scene 64-69: The Simile of the Geese
In this brief scene, Dante uses a simile to describe the movement and behavior of the souls on the terrace of Avarice as he and his guides continue their journey.

He compares the souls to migratory birds flying along the Nile; just as these birds sometimes fly in a clustered flock and other times speed up to fly in a single-file line, the group of souls on the terrace shifts its formation. As they turn their faces to watch Dante and his companions, the crowd slows its pace. This slowing is attributed to two factors: their extreme physical emaciation (the "magrezza" caused by their purgatorial hunger) and a desire to move with less effort.

## Scene 82-93: Forese's Ominous Warning

## Scene 121-126: Mythological Allusions
In this scene, the mysterious voice speaking from the apple tree continues its warning to Dante and his guides. To emphasize the danger of approaching the tree or indulging in its fruit, the voice cites two historical/mythological examples of how the act of eating or drinking can lead to weakness or disaster.

First, the voice refers to the legend of Theseus and the "cursed" soldiers. According to the myth, Theseus's men ate food provided by a cursed city, which left them "sated" (full) but robbed them of their warrior spirit and strength, making them incapable of fighting with their usual vigor.

Second, the voice recalls the biblical story of Gideon. When preparing for battle against the Midianites, Gideon tested his soldiers by observing how they drank water from a stream. Those who knelt down to drink were considered too "soft" or lacked the necessary vigilance, and consequently, Gideon refused to take them as companions into battle. 

The overarching theme is a warning against the complacency and physical indulgence that can result from "satiety," mirroring the punishment on the terrace of Avarice.

## Scene 145-150: The May Breeze
In this scene, "The May Breeze," Dante describes a sudden, sensory experience that occurs immediately after he has been dazzled by the red figure's light.

## Scene 151-154: The Benediction
In this brief final scene of Canto 24, titled "The Benediction," the experience of the gentle, ambrosia-scented breeze is accompanied by a voice. Dante hears a statement—essentially a beatitude—spoken by the brilliant red figure (the angel). The voice declares that those who are illuminated by sufficient grace are truly blessed, because for them, the "love of taste" (physical appetite or greed) does not kindle an excessive, burning desire in the heart. Instead, these blessed individuals maintain a hunger that is always "just" or balanced, suggesting a spiritual appetite that replaces the sinful craving for material luxury.

**Tag Resolutions:**
- `[1:+io]` refers to **Dante**.
- `{2:Beati}` refers to **the blessed souls** (those who have been granted enough grace to overcome the sin of gluttony and avarice).

# recap
- Dante, Virgil, and Statius are traveling together.
- They have just encountered a brilliant red figure (an angel) who instructed them to turn to continue their ascent toward peace.
- The group is poised to move forward, having received a divine benediction and a fragrant, ambrosial breeze.
