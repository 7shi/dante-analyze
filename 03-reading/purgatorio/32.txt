# Canto 32 — The Celestial Vision and the Succession of Beasts

## Scene 1-12: The Sensory Blindness
In this scene, Dante describes a state of sensory overload and subsequent blindness. He is so consumed by the sight of Beatrice—trying to quench a "ten-year thirst" for her presence—that he becomes oblivious to everything else. He is essentially in a trance, captivated by Beatrice's "holy smile," which he compares to a net that captures him.

Because he is staring so fixedly, the four nymph-stars intervene. They find his gaze "too fixed" and physically force his head to turn to the left. Because his eyes were so intensely adjusted to the brilliant light (the "sun") of Beatrice, this sudden movement causes him to lose his vision momentarily, leaving him temporarily blind.

## Scene 13-24: The Movement of the Heavenly Host
In this scene, Dante describes the moment he recovers his vision after the nymph-stars forced his gaze away from Beatrice. He notes that the time it took for his sight to return ("al poco") was brief compared to the intense, forceful sensation ("al molto sensibile") that had caused his temporary blindness. 

Once his vision returns, Dante looks to his right and witnesses the movement of the "glorious army"—the vast host of angels. He describes them turning in synchronization with the sun and the seven planets (the "seven flames"). To illustrate the scale and coordination of this movement, Dante uses a military simile: he compares the angels to a phalanx of soldiers turning under their shields to protect themselves or change direction. The movement is so swift and fluid that the entire celestial militia completes its turn and passes by before the "chariot" (the Griffin or the celestial vehicle they are on) has even begun to pivot.

## Scene 25-33: The Descent of the Griffin
In this scene, the period of stillness and sensory recovery ends, and the celestial journey resumes. 

The nymph-stars, having finished their intervention with Dante, return to their positions (the "wheels" or orbits). The Griffin then begins to move, carrying the passengers—referred to as the "blessed load"—with such smoothness that not a single feather on the creature shakes. Dante, Beatrice, and Statius follow the path of the Griffin as it moves in a tighter, inner arc. 

As they travel through the "high, empty forest" (a metaphorical description of the ranks of angels they are passing through), Dante makes a brief theological reflection: this "emptiness" or void is the result of the original sin committed by Eve (the woman who listened to the serpent). This movement is accompanied by the soothing sounds of angelic music.

## Scene 34-36: Beatrice's Descent
In this brief scene, Dante describes the rapid movement of Beatrice. As the Griffin continues its flight, Beatrice—who has been positioned higher up—descends to be closer to Dante and Statius. Dante uses a metaphor of speed, comparing her descent to a "bolt of lightning" to emphasize how quickly and effortlessly she moves through the celestial space to join them.

## Scene 37-42: The Bare Tree
In this scene, Dante describes a collective moment of recognition and observation. He hears the entire company—the group traveling on the Griffin—murmur the name "Adam." Following this, the group focuses their attention on a specific sight: a tree that is completely stripped of its leaves and foliage on every branch. 

Dante describes the sheer scale of this tree, noting that its crown expands the higher it reaches. He remarks that its immense height would be a wonder even among the famous forests of India. In the symbolic language of the *Paradiso*, this bare tree serves as a celestial representation of Adam, the patriarch of humanity, reflecting his role as the root of the human race.

## Scene 43-48: The Voice of the Griffin
In this scene, the passengers on the Griffin are observing the bare tree (the symbol of Adam). They collectively address the Griffin, noting that he is fortunate not to eat the wood of this tree; although the wood might taste sweet, they suggest it would cause him great digestive distress ("twist the belly"). The Griffin then responds to them, explaining the theological significance of the tree: it remains untouched so that the "seed" (the ancestral root/lineage) of every righteous person is preserved.

## Scene 52-60: The Transformation of the Tree
In this scene, Dante describes a miraculous transformation of the bare tree. He begins with a simile, comparing the tree's change to the way earthly plants recover their vigor and color during the night, between the setting of the sun and the next dawn.

The tree, which was previously described as completely stripped of its foliage (representing Adam in his fallen state), suddenly blooms. Dante specifies the color of this new growth: it is a shade between the red of a rose and the purple of a violet. This transformation signifies a transition from the state of sterility/death (the bare branches) to a state of renewal and life.

## Scene 61-69: The Limits of Description
In this scene, Dante reflects on the aftermath of the miraculous transformation of the tree of Adam. He explains that as the tree bloomed, the company of blessed souls sang a hymn. However, the music was so divine and overwhelming that Dante could not fully comprehend its meaning or endure the intensity of its notes.

The music acted as a celestial sedative, lulling Dante into a deep, spiritual sleep. He compares this experience to the myth of Syrinx (the nymph whose reeds were used by Pan to create music that could put listeners to sleep). Dante describes his own eyes as "pitiless" or "relentless," likely referring to the intense, hungry gaze he had maintained throughout the journey, which finally succumbed to exhaustion and the power of the music. He expresses a wish that he could "paint" or sketch the exact moment he fell asleep—as if he were a professional artist working from a model—but he ultimately admits that the experience of this divine slumber is beyond his power to describe.

## Scene 70-78: The Awakening of the Apostles
In this scene, Dante describes his transition from the divine sleep induced by the celestial music back to consciousness. He explains that he is awakened by a sudden burst of brilliance (a "splendor") that tears through the "veil" of his slumber, accompanied by a voice commanding him to rise and asking what he is doing.

As he awakens, Dante witnesses a vision of the three Apostles—Peter, John, and James. He uses a simile, comparing the sight of these three to the sight of apple blossoms (which are longed for by the angels and represent a state of eternal celestial union). These three figures, having been guided and bound by divine will, return to the "Word" (the *Logos* or Christ). Dante notes that the "sleep" they were awakened from was far greater (more profound/permanent) than the temporary spiritual nap he had just experienced.

## Scene 79-81: The Diminished School

They note that the group is "diminished" because Moses and Elijah are no longer present in the same capacity, and they observe that the "master" (Virgil) has had his "stole" (his ceremonial garment or symbol of office) changed, signifying a transformation in his status or role within this celestial hierarchy.

## Scene 91-93: The Closed Senses
In this brief scene, Dante reflects on his own sensory state as he reunites with Beatrice. He acknowledges that Beatrice may have spoken more extensively ("lo suo parlar diffuso") than he recalls, but he admits he cannot be certain. This is because his eyes were once again completely captivated by her presence. The sight of Beatrice acts as a celestial "blindfold" or "seal," closing him off to all other sensory inputs—including the full details of her speech—because he is so intensely focused on her image. This echoes the "sensory blindness" described at the beginning of the canto.

## Scene 94-99: The Guard of the Nymphs
In this scene, Dante describes the physical arrangement of the figures around the "true earth" (the base of the Tree of Adam) as he observes them upon waking.

Beatrice sits alone on the ground. Dante employs a simile to describe her position, comparing her to a guard left behind by a chariot (the "plaustro")—referring back to the cord/harness he saw tied to the Griffin (the "biforme fera"). Surrounding Beatrice, the seven nymphs have arranged themselves in a circle, creating a living enclosure or "cloister" around her. They are holding lights in their hands, which Dante notes are steady and secure, unaffected by the North wind (Aquilone) or the South wind (Austro).

## Scene 100-105: Beatrice's Command
In this scene, Beatrice speaks directly to Dante, providing him with a profound reminder of his spiritual trajectory and a divine command regarding his mission as a poet.

Beatrice first addresses Dante's transformation. She notes that he will only be a "silvano" (a dweller of the woods—referring to the lost state he was in at the beginning of the *Inferno*) for a short time longer. She promises him that he will instead become a "cive" (citizen) of the celestial Rome (the City of God/the New Jerusalem), where Christ is the ultimate citizen, and where Dante will spend eternity in her company.

Having established his destiny, Beatrice gives Dante a specific instruction for the benefit of the suffering world: he is to keep his eyes fixed on the "carro" (the Griffin and the celestial procession) and carefully observe everything he sees. She commands him that, upon his return to the mortal world, he must write down these visions. This is a pivotal moment of divine authorization for the writing of the *Divine Comedy* itself.

## Scene 109-117: The Eagle's Strike
In this scene, Dante describes a sudden and violent event. He begins with a simile, comparing the speed of what he is about to describe to a bolt of lightning descending from a distant storm cloud. 

Dante observes the "bird of Jove"—a great eagle—diving rapidly downward. The eagle plunges through the branches of the symbolic tree (the Tree of Adam), tearing not only through the new leaves and flowers but breaking through the bark itself. The eagle then strikes the "carro" (the Griffin/celestial chariot) with immense force. The impact is so powerful that the Griffin sways and bends, which Dante compares to a ship tossed by the waves of a stormy sea, shifting precariously from side to side.

**Tag Resolutions:**
- `[1:io]` refers to Dante.
- `{2:Giove}` refers to Jove (Jupiter), whose symbolic bird is the eagle.

## Scene 118-123: The Attack of the Fox
In this scene, Dante observes a new threat attempting to approach the celestial procession.

Immediately following the eagle's strike, Dante sees a fox venture toward the "cradle" (the seating area or the hold) of the triumphal vehicle (the Griffin). Dante describes the fox as appearing "fasting" or starved of any "good meal," which symbolically suggests the fox's nature as one driven by greed, malice, or spiritual hunger.

However, Beatrice intervenes. She reproaches the fox for its blameworthy faults and delivers a beating so severe that it is described as pushing the fox to the absolute limit of what its "fleshless bones" could endure. This represents the divine rejection and punishment of the fraud or heresy that the fox symbolizes.

## Scene 124-129: The Descent of the Needle
In this scene, Dante observes the arrival of a third symbolic beast following the eagle and the fox. He sees "l'aguglia" (the needle, likely referring to a small, sharp-beaked bird or a symbolic representation of a specific spiritual parasite) descend from the same direction as the previous attacks. The needle plunges into the "arca" (the hold or cradle) of the Griffin’s chariot and leaves behind its feathers.

Following this intrusion, a voice descends from heaven. Dante describes the quality of this voice by comparing it to the sound made by someone who is filled with regret or lamentation. The voice addresses the celestial vessel, calling it "my little ship" (navicella mia) and lamenting how "badly" it is loaded. This suggests that the Griffin, which carries the righteous, is being burdened or corrupted by these predatory symbols (the eagle, the fox, and the needle), and the voice from heaven—representing God—expresses sorrow over this contamination of the human lineage/spiritual procession.

## Scene 136-141: The Covering of the Earth
In this scene, Dante describes the aftermath of the attacks on the Griffin's chariot, specifically focusing on the residue left by the "needle" (the bird-like creature from Scene 124-129).

Dante notes that something remained—the feather left behind by the needle. He suggests that this feather might have been "offered" (placed there) with a seemingly "healthy and benign intention," though it was part of an attack. Using a simile comparing the process to the way lively weeds (gramigna) cover the earth, Dante describes how this remnant quickly covers itself and is, in turn, covered up. 

The scene emphasizes the incredible speed of this restoration; the feather, along with the wheels and the shaft (temo) of the chariot, are obscured or restored in the briefest of moments—the time it takes for a sigh to leave the mouth. This signifies the celestial vehicle's ability to purge or absorb the remnants of these symbolic attacks, returning to its divine purity.

**Tag Resolutions:**
- `[1:Quel]` refers to the feather (left by the "needle"/bird).
- `[2:si]` refers to the feather (left by the "needle"/bird).

## Scene 142-147: The Appearance of the Monster
In this scene, the focus is on the sudden and surreal transformation of the celestial vehicle.

**Plain English Summary:**
The "holy structure" (the Griffin's chariot/vehicle) undergoes a metamorphosis. It sprouts several heads from its various parts: three heads appear upon the shaft (the *temo*), and one head appears in each of the four corners. Dante describes the nature of these heads: the first three are horned like oxen, while the four corner heads each possess a single horn (like unicorns). Dante concludes by noting that such a strange and marvelous "monster" had never been seen before.

**Tag Resolutions:**
There were no numbered tags provided in the text for this scene. However, for the sake of narrative continuity:
- **'l dificio santo** (the holy building/structure) refers to the Griffin's chariot/vehicle.
- **mostro** (monster) refers to the transformed appearance of the chariot with its multiple horned heads.

## Scene 154-160: The Final Violence
**Scene Analysis: The Final Violence**

In this scene, the tension between the observer (Dante) and the surreal figures he is witnessing reaches a violent climax. Dante’s "greedy and wandering eye"—his intense curiosity as he observes these celestial anomalies—attracts the attention of the giant. 

The giant, described as a "fierce drudo" (a sorcerer or menacing figure), reacts with sudden rage and suspicion. He turns his anger toward the prostitute, brutally whipping her from head to toe. Following this outburst, the giant releases the "monster" (the multi-headed, horned vehicle described in the previous scene) and vanishes into the celestial forest. As he departs, he moves in such a way that the prostitute and the monster act as a physical screen or shield between himself and Dante.
