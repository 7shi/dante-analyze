## Scene 1-12: The separation of the travelers
In this scene, Dante and Virgil are preparing to leave the soul of Oderisi on the first ledge of Purgatory. 

Dante describes how he and Oderisi were walking side-by-side, compared to oxen sharing a yoke. Virgil, acting as the guide, eventually decides it is time to move on. He tells Dante to leave Oderisi behind and "cross over," using a nautical metaphor to explain that in Purgatory, every soul must "steer their own boat"â€”meaning each person is responsible for their own penance and spiritual progress.

Dante physically turns away from Oderisi to follow Virgil, though he notes a lingering emotional heaviness; while his body moves forward, his thoughts remain behind with the soul he is leaving. As Dante and Virgil walk away, the narrator emphasizes the contrast between their "lightness" (as they are not currently undergoing the penance of the ledge) and the "burden" carried by the souls remaining there.

## Scene 13-24: The warning to watch the path
In this scene, Virgil gives Dante a piece of advice as they walk along the first ledge of Purgatory. He tells Dante to look down at the ground beneath his feet. Virgil explains that the path is marked with inscriptions identifying the people who were buried in earthly graves; these markers serve as memories of their former lives, and they often cause the pious to weep when they remember the dead. Dante observes these inscriptions and notes that they are more finely crafted than those found on earth, and they continue along the path as it winds around the mountain.

## Scene 55-63: The remnants of war and ruin
This scene continues Dante's observation of the "exempla" (cautionary images) carved into the stone pavement of the first ledge of Purgatory. These images serve as permanent reminders of the consequences of pride.

First, Dante sees an image of the ruin and carnage caused by Tamyris (a king of the Massagetae). He recalls the historical/legendary moment when Tamyris defeated Cyrus the Great; Tamyris mockingly tells Cyrus that since he thirsted for the blood of others (through conquest), Tamyris will now satisfy that thirst by drenching him in his own blood (referring to the act of pouring blood into Cyrus's severed head).

Next, the pavement depicts the rout of the Assyrian army following the death of their general, Holofernes (who was beheaded by Judith), as well as the remnants of that massacre.

Finally, Dante sees the ruins of Troy (Ilion). He reflects on how the once-mighty city was reduced to ashes and caves, noting that the sign carved in the stone perfectly captures how low and vile the proud city had fallen.

## Scene 85-96: The arrival of the angel
In this scene, the angel arrives to guide Dante and Virgil further up the mountain of Purgatory.

Dante begins by reflecting on Virgil's previous warning about not wasting time. He notes that he was already well-accustomed to Virgil's reminders to keep moving, so he did not allow himself to remain too absorbed or "closed" in his study of the images on the pavement.

The "beautiful creature" (the angel) then arrives before them. Dante describes him as dressed in white, with a face that shimmers like the morning star. The angel opens his arms and then his wings, gesturing for the travelers to follow. He speaks to Dante and Virgil, inviting them to come forward and noting that he is now near the steps (the transition to the next level), where the ascent becomes easier.

The angel then shifts from addressing the travelers to making a general observation about mankind. He remarks that very few people actually respond to this divine invitation. He addresses humanity, noting that although humans were born to "fly upward" (toward God), they are fragile and fall at the slightest "wind" (temptation or sin).

## Scene 109-114: The transition in song
In this brief concluding scene of Canto 12, Dante and Virgil prepare to move from the first ledge of Purgatory to the second. As they turn to begin their ascent, they hear a chorus of voices singing the first Beatitude: "Blessed are the poor in spirit!" This song is so beautiful and profound that Dante feels ordinary speech is incapable of describing it.

This auditory experience prompts a reflection by Dante on the fundamental difference between the atmosphere of Purgatory and that of Hell. He compares the "mouths" (the entrances) of the two realms: while the way into the circles of Hell is heralded by ferocious screams and laments, the way forward in Purgatory is opened by song and divine harmony.

## Scene 115-126: The mystery of the weightless ascent
In this scene, Dante and Virgil begin their ascent up the "holy steps" to the second ledge of Purgatory. As they climb, Dante is surprised to find that the ascent feels significantly lighter and easier than walking along the flat path of the first ledge. Curious about this shift in physical sensation, Dante asks Virgil what "heavy thing" has been removed from him to make the effort feel so negligible.

Virgil explains that the lightness is tied to Dante's spiritual purification. He points out the "P"s (the marks of Pride) carved into Dante's forehead; he tells Dante that once these marks are completely erased (as one of them already is), his "good will" (spiritual desire for God) will so completely govern his physical body that his feet will not only feel no fatigue but will actually find pleasure in the act of climbing.

## Scene 127-136: The discovery of the inscription
In this scene, Dante reacts to Virgil's revelation that there are letters carved into his forehead. Since he cannot see his own forehead, Dante describes his actions using a simile: he acts like someone who suspects there is something on their head because others have hinted at it, but who must use their hand to confirm it since sight is impossible.

Dante reaches up with the fingers of his right hand to feel his skin. Through touch, he discovers six carved letters located between the "keys" (the temples) of his head. As he does this, Virgil watches him and smiles, likely amused by Dante's tactile curiosity and the confirmation of the marks of Pride.
