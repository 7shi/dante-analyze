# Canto 28 — The Encounter with the Enchanted Lady in the Forest

## Scene 1-6: Departure into the Forest
In this scene, Dante is filled with eagerness to explore the "divine forest" (the Earthly Paradise) that surrounds him. The new day's light is softened by the thick foliage of the woods. Without further hesitation, he leaves the riverbank and begins to walk slowly across the landscape, which is described as being olive-green in every direction.

There is no dialogue in this passage; it is a first-person narrative account of Dante's movements.

**Tag Resolution:**
- `[1]` refers to Dante.

## Scene 7-12: The Gentle Breeze
In this scene, a gentle and steady breeze blows through the Earthly Paradise. The breeze brushes softly against Dante's forehead, feeling no more forceful than a light wind. This breeze is consistent enough that it causes all the leaves of the trees to tremble and bend in a single direction—toward the area where the holy mountain casts its first shadow.

**Tag Resolution:**
- `[1]` refers to Dante.

## Scene 13-21: The Singing Birds
**Analysis of the Scene: The Singing Birds**

**What happens:**
The scene continues the description of the Earthly Paradise's atmosphere. Although the breeze (from the previous scene) is bending the leaves, it is not so strong that it disrupts the birds in the treetops. Instead, the birds sing with great joy in the early hours of the day. The rustling of the leaves creates a rhythmic, humming accompaniment (a "drone" or *bordone*) to the birds' melodies. Dante compares this experience to the pine forests on the shore of Chiaia (in Naples), where birds flutter from branch to branch when the scirocco wind is released.

**Who does what:**
* **The birds:** Sing joyfully and move between the branches.
* **The leaves:** Provide a musical backdrop to the birds' songs.
* **Aeolus:** Mentioned in a simile as the one who releases the scirocco wind.

**Tag Resolution:**
* `{1:Ëolo}`: Aeolus (the Greek god/personification of the winds).

## Scene 28-33: The Dark Stream
In this scene, Dante provides a detailed description of the stream (*rio*) he has encountered. 

He observes a paradox: although the water appears dark (*bruna bruna*), he claims it is actually purer and clearer than any other water he has seen, because it "hides nothing." The reason for its dark appearance is not the quality of the water itself, but the environment; the stream flows beneath a "perpetual shadow" created by the dense canopy of the Earthly Paradise, which blocks out the light of both the sun and the moon.

**Who does what:**
* **Dante:** Observes and reflects on the clarity and appearance of the stream.
* **The stream:** Flows darkly under the shadow of the trees.

**Analysis of the Scene: The Sight of the Lady**

**What happens:**

However, as he moves beyond the stream, something suddenly appears to him. This appearance is so striking and wondrous that it instantly captures his entire attention, diverting his mind away from all other thoughts. (In the context of the poem, this is the moment he first sees Matelda).

## Scene 49-51: A Mythological Comparison
**Analysis of the Scene: A Mythological Comparison**

**What happens:**
Dante continues his address to the mysterious lady (Matelda). He tells her that her image—walking through the woods, singing and gathering flowers—reminds him of the mythological figure Proserpina (Persephone). Specifically, he evokes the time before Proserpina was abducted by Pluto, when she spent her days in the meadows of spring. He notes the sorrow of that time: Proserpina lost her mother (Ceres/Demeter), and in turn, the mother lost her daughter and the joy of the springtime.

**Who does what:**
* **Dante:** Speaks to Matelda, drawing a parallel between her current presence in the Earthly Paradise and the youth of Proserpina.
* **Matelda:** The subject of the comparison (the "You" being addressed).
* **Proserpina:** The mythological figure mentioned in the comparison.
* **The mother (Ceres):** The mythological figure mentioned as having lost her daughter.

## Scene 61-66: The Radiance of her Eyes
**Analysis of the Scene: The Radiance of her Eyes**

## Scene 67-75: Her Joyful Presence
**Analysis of the Scene: Her Joyful Presence**

**What happens:**
Matelda stands on the opposite bank of the stream, smiling at Dante. She continues to handle the colorful flowers with her hands, contrasting the lushness of the Earthly Paradise with the barren, "seedless" heights of the mountain Dante has just climbed. Although they are separated by only three paces, the stream remains a physical barrier. Dante describes the intensity of his longing to reach her by using a classical analogy: he compares his desire to cross this small stream to the legendary struggle of Leander, who swam across the Hellespont to reach his lover. Dante suggests that Leander's longing to swim between Sestos and Abydos was no greater than the frustration Dante feels being kept apart from Matelda by the water. He also mentions Xerxes, who attempted to bridge the Hellespont, as a symbol of human pride.

**Who does what:**
* **Matelda:** Stands on the other side of the stream, smiling and touching the flowers.
* **Dante:** Observes Matelda and reflects on his intense desire to cross the stream to reach her, comparing his feeling to that of Leander.
* **Xerxes (Serse):** Mentioned as a historical/mythological figure who crossed the Hellespont.
* **Leander (Leandro):** Mentioned in a simile as the lover who braved the Hellespont.

## Scene 97-102: The Mountain's Ascent
**Analysis of the Scene: The Mountain's Ascent**

**What happens:**
Matelda continues her explanation of the nature of the Earthly Paradise and the geography of the mountain. She explains the physical reason why the mountain rises so high toward the sky. She describes the "disturbance" caused by the tides (*l'essalazion*) of the water and the earth, which are driven by the heat (a reference to the influence of the moon and sun). According to Matelda, the mountain was elevated to such a great height specifically to protect humanity from the destructive power of these tides (floods and seismic shifts), ensuring that the summit remains "free" and safe from such turmoil.

**Who does what:**
* **Matelda:** Speaks to Dante, providing a cosmological/geological explanation for the height of the Mountain of Purgatory.
* **The mountain:** Rises toward the heavens to serve as a sanctuary.
* **The tides/elements:** Create disturbances that follow the "heat."
* **Man:** The beneficiary of the mountain's height, being protected from the "war" of the elements.

**Tag Resolution:**
* `{1:l'uomo}`: Man/Humanity (specifically referring to the first man, Adam, and the human race in general).

## Scene 103-114: The Mechanics of the Wind and Trees
In this scene, Matelda continues her role as a teacher, providing Dante with a cosmological and biological explanation for the phenomena he has observed in the Earthly Paradise.

**What happens:**
Matelda explains the source of the breeze and the sound of the forest. She describes how the air rotates in synchronization with the "first sphere" (the Moon's orbit). Because the summit of the mountain is so high and immersed in this "living air," the rotating motion of the atmosphere strikes the dense forest, creating the rhythmic humming or rustling sound that Dante noticed earlier. 

Furthermore, she explains a mystical-biological process: when the wind strikes the trees, the plants transfer their "virtue" (their essence or innate power) into the breeze. As the wind continues to rotate and travel, it carries these essences to other parts of the earth. Depending on the nature of the land and its relationship with the sky, different regions "conceive" and produce different types of trees and plants based on the diverse virtues carried by the wind.

**Who does what:**
* **Matelda:** Speaks to Dante, explaining the mechanics of the wind, the sounds of the woods, and the distribution of botanical diversity across the world.
* **Dante:** The silent listener, receiving this explanation.
* **The air/breeze:** Rotates with the celestial spheres, strikes the forest, and transports the "virtues" of the plants.
* **The plants/forest:** Produce sound when struck by the wind and infuse the air with their essence.

## Scene 121-126: The Two Springs
**Analysis of the Scene: The Two Springs**

**What happens:**
Matelda continues her pedagogical explanation, this time focusing on the stream (*rio*) that Dante has just crossed. She explains that this water is not like the water in ordinary earthly rivers. Natural rivers are subject to the water cycle—evaporating as vapor or freezing into ice—and their flow (their "strength" or *lena*) fluctuates, increasing or decreasing based on these conditions. 

In contrast, she explains that the water in the Earthly Paradise comes from a "steady and certain" fountain. This divine source does not rely on weather or natural cycles; instead, it is maintained by the will of God. The fountain receives exactly as much water from God's will as it pours out, and it divides its flow into two separate directions (this is a symbolic reference to the rivers Lethe and Eunoe).

**Who does what:**
* **Matelda:** Speaks to Dante, explaining the supernatural and divine nature of the stream's source.
* **Dante:** The listener who is observing the water.
* **God:** The source of the fountain's constancy, maintaining the water level through His will.
* **The ordinary river:** Used as a point of comparison to highlight the difference between natural and divine water.

**Tag Resolution:**
* `[1:+tu]`: Dante (the person Matelda is addressing).
* `[2:+egli]`: the river (*fiume*).
* `[3:+ella]`: the water (*l'acqua*).
* `{4:Dio}`: God.
