# Canto 29 — The Celestial Procession of the Empyrean

## Scene 1-15: The Path Along the River
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Matelda concludes her explanation with a song-like quality, quoting a Latin verse ("Blessed are they whose sins are covered!"). She then begins to walk along the bank of the river, moving with a grace and lightness compared to the nymphs of mythology. Dante follows closely behind her, matching her small steps. As they walk, the riverbank curves, leading them toward the east. After a short distance, Matelda turns completely toward Dante and urges him to pay close attention to what is happening around them.

## Scene 16-30: The Sudden Light and Melody
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, the atmosphere of the Earthly Paradise is suddenly transformed. A blinding light flashes throughout the great forest. At first, Dante is unsure if it is a bolt of lightning, but he notices a key difference: while lightning vanishes instantly, this light persists and grows increasingly brilliant. As the light intensifies, a sweet melody begins to flow through the luminous air.

This divine sensory experience triggers a powerful emotional response in Dante. He describes a feeling of "good zeal" (spiritual eagerness) that makes him feel a kinship with Eve. He imagines Eve at the moment of her creation—pure, innocent, and existing in a world where heaven and earth were in perfect harmony. He suggests that just as Eve, being newly formed, would have been unable to tolerate any veil hiding the divine glory from her, he himself feels an overwhelming desire to experience these "ineffable delights" without any obstruction.

## Scene 31-42: The Poet's Invocation
In this scene, the narrative momentarily pauses as Dante experiences a moment of overwhelming spiritual anticipation. He is suspended in a state of eager longing for the eternal joys he senses are approaching. Suddenly, a brilliant light—described as being like a burning fire—appears in the air beneath the green branches of the forest, preceding the group. This visual manifestation is accompanied by the sweet sound of singing.

Overwhelmed by the magnitude of what he is witnessing and the difficulty of describing it, Dante turns away from the action to perform a poetic invocation. He appeals to the "sacrosante Vergini" (holy Virgins), citing the hardships he has endured—hunger, cold, and sleeplessness—as justification for asking their mercy and aid. He concludes by calling upon the personified Mount Helicon and the Muse Urania to grant him the poetic inspiration and strength necessary to translate these "strong" (sublime and complex) divine experiences into human verse.

## Scene 43-54: The Golden Candelabra
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante describes a visual transition as he and his guides approach the source of the light and music. From a distance, he sees seven golden objects that appear to be trees. However, this is an optical illusion caused by the distance between the observers and the objects. As Dante draws closer, the distortion of perspective vanishes, and he realizes that these are not trees at all, but "candelabri" (candelabras or lampstands). 

These figures are not inanimate objects but are singing "Osanna" (Hosanna), indicating they are blessed souls. Dante notes that atop each of these figures is a beautiful, flaming light that is far brighter than the light of a full moon on a clear midnight.

## Scene 55-63: Beatrice's Rebuke
In this scene, Dante is overwhelmed by the spectacle of the singing, luminous figures (the golden candelabra). He turns to Virgil to share his admiration, and he finds that Virgil is equally stunned. Dante then returns his gaze to the celestial procession, noting how slowly the figures are moving—comparing their pace to that of men captivated by new brides.

As the procession arrives, Beatrice (the woman following the lights) notices that Dante is preoccupied with the brilliance of the candelabra. She rebukes him, asking why he is so focused on the "living lights" and why he is neglecting to look at the person coming behind them.

## Scene 64-69: The White-Clad People
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante observes the continuation of the celestial procession. Following the seven golden candelabra and Beatrice (the "leaders"), a great multitude of people appears. These figures are all dressed in white garments of such intense brilliance that Dante notes no such whiteness had ever been seen in the Earthly Paradise. 

As this procession moves forward, they walk along the bank of the river. The water is so clear and still that it acts as a mirror; as the procession passes on the left, Dante catches sight of his own reflection in the water.

## Scene 70-81: The Rainbow Trails
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante positions himself on the bank of the river to get a clearer view of the celestial procession. As he watches the seven luminous flames (the golden candelabra) move forward, he notices a stunning visual effect: the flames leave behind them a trail of color in the air, as if a painter had used a brush to streak the sky. 

These trails consist of seven distinct stripes of color, mirroring the spectral colors of a rainbow (which Dante attributes to the influence of the Sun and the Moon/Delia). Dante observes that these shimmering trails are vast, exceeding the reach of his own vision, and estimates that the leading flames are approximately ten paces ahead of him.

## Scene 82-87: The Twenty-Four Elders
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, the celestial procession continues to grow as a new group appears under the brilliant sky observed by Dante. Twenty-four elders (resembling the elders from the Book of Revelation) arrive, walking in pairs. They are crowned with *fiordaliso* (likely cornflowers or a symbolic white floral crown), adding to the visual splendor of the group.

As they move, the elders sing in unison. Their song is a hymn of praise addressed to Beatrice, proclaiming her blessed among all the "daughters of Adam" (all women) and wishing for her beauties to be eternally blessed. This reinforces Beatrice's role as the representative of Divine Grace and the pinnacle of human purity.

## Scene 88-96: The Six-Winged Creatures
In this scene, Dante continues to describe the celestial procession as it moves along the bank of the river. He notes the landscape on the opposite bank, where the greenery and flowers are visible, unobstructed by the passing crowd of "elected people." Following this group, four supernatural creatures arrive. These creatures are crowned with green foliage and possess six wings each. Dante observes that these wings are covered in eyes, and he uses a mythological simile, comparing them to the eyes of Argus (the hundred-eyed giant of Greek myth), suggesting a state of divine, all-seeing vigilance.

## Scene 97-105: The Vision of Ezekiel
In this scene, the narrative shifts from a description of the celestial procession to a meta-poetic address. Dante stops his detailed description of the six-winged creatures, explaining to the reader that he cannot afford to spend more verses on their forms because he has other, more pressing descriptions to attend to (the "expense" of the rest of the poem). 

To fill the gap, Dante directs the reader to the biblical book of Ezekiel, noting that the prophet's depiction of these beings—arriving amidst wind, cloud, and fire—is an accurate representation of what he is seeing. He concludes by noting a slight discrepancy regarding the wings: while he feels that St. John (the Evangelist) agrees with his own observation, the creatures he sees still differ slightly from the description provided by John.

## Scene 106-114: The Triumph of the Griffin
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante describes the centerpiece of the celestial procession. Within the space enclosed by the four six-winged creatures (the seraphim), a triumphal chariot appears. This chariot, which has two wheels, is drawn by a Griffin. 

Dante provides a detailed description of the Griffin's movements and appearance. The Griffin stretches its wings high—specifically between the middle feathers and the rows of plumes—allowing it to cleave through the air without causing any disturbance to the surrounding environment. Its wings are so elevated that they are barely visible. The creature possesses a dual nature in its coloring: the parts of its body that are avian (the eagle half) are made of gold, while the parts that are leonine (the lion half) are white, streaked with vermillion (red).

**Tag Resolutions**

*   [1:lor]: The six-winged creatures (the seraphim described in the previous scene)

## Scene 115-120: Comparison to Imperial Glory
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante uses a series of comparisons to emphasize the unparalleled beauty and majesty of the Griffin's chariot. He first compares it to the height of earthly imperial glory, noting that even the triumphal chariots of Rome's most celebrated figures—Scipio Africanus and Augustus Caesar—were not as magnificent. 

He then moves from earthly history to classical mythology, comparing the Griffin's chariot to the chariot of the Sun (associated with the myth of Phaethon). Dante asserts that the solar chariot would seem "poor" or dull in comparison. He briefly recalls the myth of Phaethon, who, while attempting to drive the sun-chariot, veered off course and threatened to consume the world in fire. This prompted the "devoted Earth" to pray for salvation, leading Jupiter (the king of the gods) to act with "secret justice" by striking Phaethon down to save the world. By placing the Griffin's chariot above both the greatest Roman triumphs and the most radiant mythical object, Dante underscores the supernatural and divine nature of the procession.

## Scene 121-129: The Three Dancing Women
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante observes three women dancing in a circle around the right wheel of the Griffin's triumphal chariot. He describes them by their striking colors, which serve as symbolic representations of the three Theological Virtues:

1.  The first woman is a deep red, so intense that she would be barely distinguishable if she were inside a fire (representing Charity/Love).
2.  The second woman appears as if her flesh and bones were made of emerald (representing Hope).
3.  The third woman looks like freshly fallen snow (representing Faith).

Dante describes the choreography of their dance, noting that they alternate their lead; sometimes they seem to be led by the white woman, and other times by the red woman. Their pace—shifting between slow and fast—is governed by the singing of the red woman (the last mentioned "questa").

## Scene 133-141: The Two Old Men
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante continues his detailed survey of the figures accompanying the Griffin's chariot. After describing the Virtues, he notices two old men. Although they are dressed differently, they share the same dignified, honest, and firm demeanor.

Dante identifies these two figures as symbolic representations of the two branches of healing. The first man is presented as a follower or "familiar" of the great Hippocrates; he represents medicine in its restorative, non-invasive sense, associated with the care of living creatures. The second man represents the "opposite care"—surgery. He carries a bright, sharp sword (representing the scalpel), a sight that causes Dante a momentary flash of fear as he watches from his side of the river. Together, these two figures symbolize the dual nature of the medical art: the soothing physician and the cutting surgeon.

## Scene 142-144: The Humble Figures
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief scene, Dante continues his detailed inventory of the figures accompanying the Griffin's chariot. He first observes four individuals who are distinguished by their "humble appearance" (*umile paruta*). These figures are traditionally interpreted as the four Evangelists.

Following the entire procession, Dante sees a single old man bringing up the rear. This figure is depicted in a paradoxical state: he is "sleeping" (*dormendo*), yet his face remains "shrewd" or "keen" (*arguta*). This figure is a personification of Silence (often associated with the Greek god Harpocrates), symbolizing the necessity of silence and the inadequacy of human speech when confronting the divine mysteries of the Empyrean.

**Tag Resolutions**

*   [1:+io]: Dante
*   {2:un vecchio solo}: The old man (the personification of Silence)

## Scene 145-150: The Floral Crowns
**Scene Analysis**

In this scene, Dante provides a closer detail about the appearance of the seven leading figures of the procession. He refers back to the seven golden candelabra (the "seven lights") and the primary group they lead. He notes that these seven figures are adorned with floral crowns; however, instead of white lilies (which are typically symbols of purity), their crowns are composed of roses and other red flowers. 

Dante admits a momentary optical ambiguity: he explains that if he had been viewing them from a slightly greater distance, he would have sworn that the red of the flowers was actually the flames of the lights themselves burning above their eyebrows. This detail emphasizes the interplay between the physical manifestation of the blessed souls and the divine light they emit.

## Scene 151-154: The Sudden Halt
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief scene, the celestial procession finally reaches the point where it is directly opposite Dante. At that precise moment, a sudden, thunderous sound echoes through the air. This sound acts as a signal or a barrier, causing the "worthy people" of the procession to abruptly halt their progress. The entire group comes to a standstill, beginning with the leading figures (the "first signs" or banners) of the procession.
