# Canto 07 — The Encounter with Sordello and the Vision of the Limbo Valley

## Scene 1-9: Virgil's Identification
In this scene, the warm, emotional greetings between Sordello and Virgil have wound down after a few repetitions. Sordello, wanting to formally identify the man he is speaking with, turns to Virgil and asks him who he is. 

Virgil responds by providing a historical and spiritual autobiography. He notes that he died and was buried by Octavian (Augustus) long before the souls currently in Purgatory were directed toward the mountain. He explicitly names himself as Virgil and explains the reason for his current state: he was "lost" to heaven (placed in Limbo) not because of any specific evil act, but simply because he lacked faith (being a virtuous pagan). Dante closes the scene by identifying the speaker as his guide.

## Scene 10-21: Sordello's Inquiry
In this scene, Sordello is reacting to the sight of Virgil. He is overwhelmed with emotion and disbelief, experiencing a moment of cognitive dissonance where he cannot quite believe his eyes. He humbles himself before Virgil, embraces him warmly, and addresses him with profound reverence, acknowledging Virgil's literary genius and their shared hometown of Mantua. Sordello then asks Virgil why he has appeared to him and whether he has come from Hell.

## Scene 22-39: Virgil's Description of Limbo
In this scene, Virgil is responding to Sordello's inquiry about his origins and his presence in Purgatory. Virgil confirms that he has come from the "sorrowful kingdom" (Hell), specifically from Limbo. He explains that he did not arrive here by his own power, but was moved by a divine force ("virtù del ciel") to accompany Dante. 

Virgil reflects on his own spiritual condition, noting that he is barred from seeing God ("the High Sun") not because of any evil deeds he committed, but because of what he failed to do: he lacked the faith (the "non fare") required for salvation. He describes Limbo as a place of darkness and sighs rather than active torture, where he resides with two groups: infants who died before they could be baptized and virtuous pagans who lived without vice but without the three theological virtues (Faith, Hope, and Charity). Finally, Virgil turns the conversation back to Sordello, asking if Sordello can provide any information or clues to help them reach the official beginning of Purgatory more quickly.

## Scene 40-48: The Difficulty of the Ascent
In this scene, Sordello responds to Virgil's request for guidance. He explains that there is no specific designated "spot" or boundary he must stay within; he is free to move up and around the slopes of Ante-Purgatory. He offers to accompany Virgil (and Dante) as a guide as far as he is permitted to go. 

Sordello then points out that the day is fading ("the day declines"), and since it is impossible to ascend the mountain at night, they need to find a place to stay for the evening. He notes that there are other penitent souls further to the right and offers to lead Virgil and Dante to them, suggesting that meeting these souls will be a pleasant way to spend their time.

## Scene 49-60: Sordello's Warning on Night Travel
In this scene, Dante asks for clarification regarding Sordello's previous statement that the mountain cannot be ascended at night. Dante asks whether a person attempting to climb in the dark would be stopped by someone else (an external guard or force) or if they simply would be unable to do so.

Sordello responds with a physical gesture, rubbing his finger in the dirt to draw a line. He tells Virgil (and by extension, the group) that they would be unable to cross even that small line once the sun has set. He explains that the obstacle is not a physical barrier or a guard, but the "nocturnal darkness" itself, which thwarts the will to ascend by rendering the action impossible. However, he notes that while one cannot go up, it is perfectly possible to travel downward or wander along the slopes of the mountain during the night.

## Scene 64-78: The Splendor of the Valley
**Scene Summary**

The party (Dante, Virgil, and Sordello) begins to walk away from the shores of Ante-Purgatory. As they move, Dante notices that the mountain's slope is indented, forming a valley or a "lap" (*grembo*). Sordello notices this as well and suggests that they head toward this sheltered area to spend the night and wait for the sunrise.

They follow a crooked path that leads them into the side of this valley. Once there, Dante is struck by the extraordinary vibrancy of the flora. He describes the grass and flowers in such vivid terms that he claims even the most precious and brightly colored materials known to man—fine gold, silver, indigo, and emeralds—would look pale and "overcome" by comparison to the natural brilliance of the vegetation in that spot.

## Scene 79-84: The Singing Souls
In this scene, Dante describes the sensory experience of the valley. He notes that the beauty of the place isn't just visual; instead, Nature has created an atmosphere of "indistinct" sweetness through a multitude of fragrances. As they move further in, Dante discovers a group of souls sitting among the grass and flowers. These souls are singing the "Salve Regina" (a hymn to the Virgin Mary), and they are positioned in such a way that they cannot be seen from outside the valley.

## Scene 85-96: The Lament of Emperor Rudolf
In this scene, Sordello (the "Mantoan") speaks to Dante and Virgil as the sun begins to set. He offers to guide them through the group of souls they have encountered in the valley. He explains that by following him, Dante and Virgil will be able to better recognize the identities and the expressions of all the souls gathered there. 

Sordello specifically draws their attention to one figure: a man sitting higher than the others who remains silent while the rest sing. Sordello identifies this man as Emperor Rudolf. He delivers a sharp political critique of the Emperor, stating that Rudolf had the power to heal the "wounds" (the political strife and decay) that had devastated Italy, but he neglected his duty. Consequently, Rudolf now finds his "recreation" or solace only belatedly through others.

## Scene 97-102: The Story of Ottacchero
In this scene, Sordello continues to act as a guide and commentator for Dante and Virgil, introducing them to the souls resting in the valley. After discussing Emperor Rudolf, Sordello points out another figure whose presence provides comfort to Rudolf. 

Sordello identifies this man as Ottokar (Ottacchero), the former ruler of Bohemia (described geographically as the land where the river Moldau flows into the Elbe). Sordello offers a brief dynastic critique, stating that Ottokar was a far superior ruler to his son, Wenceslaus (Vincislao). Sordello characterizes Wenceslaus as "the bearded one" and dismisses him as a man driven by lust and laziness.

## Scene 103-106: The Death of the Young Man
In this brief scene, Sordello continues his critique of Wenceslaus (Vincislao), the son of Ottokar. He begins by mocking Wenceslaus's appearance, specifically his "little nose" (`{1:quel nasetto}`), noting that while he physically resembles his father Ottokar (`[2:colui]`), who possesses a benign aspect, he lacks his father's virtue. Sordello recalls that Wenceslaus died prematurely and in flight ("plucking the lily" refers to dying in youth/virginity), suggesting a cowardly or wasted end. Finally, Sordello directs the attention of Dante and Virgil (`[4:+voi]`) toward the soul of Wenceslaus (`[5:+ei]`), pointing out that he is currently beating his own chest (`[6:si]`) in a gesture of penitence and remorse.

## Scene 107-111: The French Ancestors
In this scene, Sordello continues to act as a commentator and guide for Dante and Virgil, pointing out the souls in the valley. He directs their attention to another soul who is in a state of deep melancholy, resting his cheek on his palm and sighing. 

Sordello explains the cause of this soul's distress by referencing his lineage. He notes that this man's father and father-in-law are the sources of the "evil of France" (referring to the political and moral corruption of the French monarchy). These ancestors are aware that the man's own life was vicious and "dirty" (sinful), and it is this realization—or the shame associated with his lineage and conduct—that causes the piercing grief the man is currently experiencing.

## Scene 112-120: The Virtuous Kings
In this scene, Sordello continues his role as the commentator and guide, pointing out another soul in the valley to Dante and Virgil. He identifies a man who appears physically well-proportioned and imposing (*membruto*). 

Sordello notes that this man is singing together with the one with the "masculine nose" (which refers back to Ottokar, contrasting with the "little nose" of his son Wenceslaus). Sordello praises the well-proportioned man, stating that he was a paragon of every kind of virtue (*valor*). 

Sordello then reflects on the tragedy of lost lineage: he observes a youth sitting behind this virtuous man and remarks that if this youth had succeeded the virtuous man as king, virtue would have passed successfully from one generation to the next ("from vessel to vessel"). He contrasts this hypothetical ideal with the actual state of royal successions. He concludes by mentioning James (*Iacomo*) and Frederick (*Federigo*), the current rulers of certain realms, implying that their inheritance or the current state of their rule does not match the standard of virtue exhibited by the soul he is currently describing.

## Scene 121-123: A Reflection on Virtue
In this brief scene, Sordello continues his reflection on the nature of virtue and royal succession, expanding on the "vessel to vessel" metaphor from the previous scene. He laments that true human probity (virtue/integrity) is a rare occurrence, comparing it to something that only seldom "rises through the branches" (like a rare bloom or fruit). 

He explains the desire of a truly virtuous leader: the one who possesses and "gives" this virtue (through example, education, or legacy) does so with the hope that their successor—specifically the youth mentioned in the previous scene—will be recognized as virtuous because of the influence and example set by the predecessor. It is a commentary on the ideal of a virtuous dynasty, which Sordello views as a rarity in the actual history of the rulers he is observing.

**Tag Resolutions:**

*   `[1:quei]`: The virtuous man (the well-proportioned soul Sordello has been praising)
*   `[2:lui]`: The virtuous man (the source of the virtue)
*   `[3:+egli]`: The youth (the soul sitting behind the virtuous man, the intended successor)

## Scene 124-126: The Mention of Pier
In this scene, Sordello continues his role as a commentator and guide for Dante and Virgil, pointing out the souls in the valley and reflecting on their identities. 

Sordello indicates that his words (his observations and critiques) are directed not only at the virtuous man he was previously discussing but also toward "the big-nosed one" (Ottokar). He explains that these words apply to Ottokar no less than they do to "the other" person—Pier della Vigna—who is singing alongside Ottokar. Sordello concludes by noting that Pier is a figure for whom Puglia and Provence are in mourning.

## Scene 127-129: The Legacy of Constance
In this scene, Sordello continues his meditation on the rarity of virtue and the tragedy of failed royal successions. Having praised the "virtuous man" (historically identified as Emperor Frederick II), he now reflects on the descendants of that line.

Sordello laments that the "plant" (the offspring) grown from the "lesser seed" (the descendants who did not inherit the father's greatness) is diminished. He illustrates this by mentioning the daughters of Frederick II, Beatrice and Margherita, and notes that Constance (Frederick's mother) still boasts of her husband (Henry VI), implying that the pride in the lineage's origin outweighs the actual virtue found in the subsequent generations. It is a poignant commentary on the decay of the Hohenstaufen dynasty.

## Scene 130-132: Henry of England
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief scene, Sordello continues his role as the commentator and guide for Dante and Virgil, identifying another soul resting in the valley of Ante-Purgatory. He directs their attention to a man sitting alone, whom he identifies as Henry III of England. Sordello describes him using the epithet "the king of the simple life," referring to Henry's reputation for piety and asceticism. Furthermore, Sordello makes a comparative observation: unlike the decayed lineage of the Hohenstaufen dynasty (discussed in the previous scene), Henry's descendants ("branches") produced a better outcome, implying that his line maintained a higher standard of virtue or spiritual success.

## Scene 133-136: The Sorrow of Guillaume
**Scene Analysis**

In this brief scene, Sordello continues his role as the commentator and guide, identifying another soul among the group in the valley. He points out a man who is positioned lower than the others (`più basso`), sitting or humbling himself among the group while looking upward. Sordello identifies this man as William, the Marquis of Monferrato (`Guiglielmo marchese`). Sordello then provides a historical context for the man's sorrow or the grief associated with him, mentioning that William's involvement in the wars in Alexandria caused great mourning in his home territories of Monferrato and Canavese.
