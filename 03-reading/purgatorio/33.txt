# Canto 33 — Beatrice's Prophecy and the River of Lethe

## Scene 1-6: The Song of the Virtuous Women
In this brief opening scene, the atmosphere shifts toward a somber, liturgical mood. A group of virtuous women (the blessed souls accompanying the procession) begin to sing a Latin psalm, "Deus, venerunt gentes" ("O God, the nations have come"), alternating their voices in groups of three or four. As they sing, they are weeping.

Beatrice listens to them, sighing and filled with piety. Dante observes her expression and notes that she is so deeply moved that her appearance mirrors the sorrow of the Virgin Mary as she stood at the foot of the cross during the Crucifixion.

## Scene 7-12: Beatrice's Proclamation
In this brief scene, the focus shifts from the choral singing of the blessed women to Beatrice herself. The virtuous women (the virgins) stop their psalm and yield the floor to Beatrice. Standing up and blushing intensely—likely due to the emotional weight of the prophecy or the piety of the moment—Beatrice addresses them in Latin. She delivers a cryptic prophecy: she tells them that they will soon cease to see her, but that after a short time, they will see her again.

## Scene 13-24: The Encounter with Dante
In this scene, Beatrice transitions from her public prophecy to a more private interaction with Dante. After addressing the virtuous women, she moves to the front of the group (the seven nymphs). She gestures for a small sub-group to follow her: Dante himself, a virtuous woman, and a wise man (both from the company of the blessed souls).

As they begin to walk, Beatrice almost immediately stops and looks Dante directly in the eyes. She calmly tells him to move closer to her so that he can hear her clearly. Dante obeys immediately and approaches her. Once he is beside her, Beatrice gently chides him, asking why he is not taking the opportunity to ask her questions as they walk together.

## Scene 25-33: Dante's Stuttering Response
In this scene, Dante describes his psychological and physical struggle to speak in the presence of Beatrice. He uses a simile, comparing himself to people who are so overwhelmed by reverence for a superior that they are unable to articulate their words. 

Stammering and lacking confidence, Dante manages to tell Beatrice ("Madonna") that she already knows his needs and what would be a satisfactory answer for him. Beatrice responds with a gentle but firm correction; she tells him that she wants him to cast off his fear and shame so that he can speak clearly and consciously, rather than sounding like a man trapped in a dream.

## Scene 34-45: The Prophecy of the Serpent and the Giant
In this scene, Beatrice takes the initiative to interpret the surreal and disturbing visions Dante witnessed in the previous canto (the multi-headed serpent, the prostitute, and the giant). She explains that these were not random hallucinations but symbolic prophecies regarding the corruption of the Church and the eventual divine retribution that will follow.

Beatrice tells Dante that the "vessel" (representing the Church or the Empire) that was broken by the "serpent" (the forces of corruption/schism) is a reality of the past, and those responsible for this destruction will not escape God's vengeance. She further explains that the "needle" (the prostitute, symbolizing the corrupted Church) who became a monster will eventually become "prey." Beatrice asserts that she sees these events clearly in the divine plan and is relaying them to Dante. Finally, she predicts a future moment involving a "messenger of God" (identified by the number 515) who will destroy both the "fugitive" (the prostitute) and the "giant" (the power conspiring with her).

## Scene 46-51: The Riddle of the Naiads
In this scene, Beatrice continues her conversation with Dante, acknowledging that her previous explanations of the disturbing visions were obscure and cryptic. She admits that her "dark narration" might seem like a riddle to him, comparing her style of delivery to that of mythological riddle-givers like the Sphinx. 

However, she reassures Dante that he does not need to struggle with the interpretation alone. She informs him that the Naiads (the nymphs accompanying them) will soon clarify the facts and "solve this strong enigma." She adds a witty remark that this explanation will come "without loss of sheep or grain," contrasting the free guidance of the blessed souls with the payment typically required to consult an ancient oracle or the heavy price paid for failing a riddle.

## Scene 52-60: The Warning of the Fallen Fruit
In this scene, Beatrice gives Dante a direct command regarding his role as a poet and witness. She instructs him to record the words she has spoken and to convey them to the "living"—the people of Earth—whom she describes with a bleak reminder that human life is essentially a race toward death.

Beatrice specifically warns Dante not to omit the description of the "plant" (the Tree of Adam/the Tree of Life) and the fact that it has been "plundered" twice (likely referring to the original fall of man and the subsequent corruption of the Church/Empire). She explains the spiritual gravity of this act: anyone who desecrates or "robs" this holy tree is committing a blasphemy through their actions and thereby offending God, who created the tree holy for His own divine purposes.

## Scene 61-66: The Weight of Original Sin
In this scene, Beatrice continues her explanation of the spiritual significance of the Tree of Adam, connecting the physical height and exaltation of the tree to the gravity of Original Sin.

Beatrice explains that the "first soul" (Adam) spent over five thousand years in a state of pain and longing, desiring to bite the fruit of this tree. She notes that Adam is the one who ultimately committed the act of biting, and in doing so, brought punishment upon himself. 

Turning back to Dante, Beatrice gently mocks his lack of perception. She asks if his intellect is "sleeping," because he should be able to deduce that the tree is placed so high and is so exalted specifically because of the immense longing and subsequent fall of Adam. The tree's position serves as a cosmic reminder of the distance between humanity and God created by the first sin.

## Scene 67-78: The Command to Remember
In this scene, Beatrice continues her dialogue with Dante, moving from the cosmic explanation of the Tree of Adam to a critique of Dante's own intellectual and emotional state.

Beatrice begins by gently mocking Dante’s previous mindset. She suggests that if his mind hadn't been clouded by "vain thoughts"—comparing his youthful, romantic obsessions to the river Elsa and the tragic lover Pyramus—he would have been able to intuitively grasp the moral lesson of the Tree and the nature of God's justice regarding the forbidden fruit.

Seeing that Dante is still struggling to fully comprehend the divine truth (describing his intellect as being "made of stone" or "hardened"), Beatrice realizes that a simple explanation isn't enough. She commands him to engrave this vision and these lessons deep within his memory. She wants the experience to be "written" (which refers to the act of Dante writing the *Divine Comedy*) or at least "painted" (vividly imprinted) in his mind. She compares this permanent mental record to the way a pilgrim’s staff is adorned with palm leaves, symbolizing a victory or a completed sacred journey.

## Scene 79-84: Dante's Impressionable Mind
In this scene, Dante responds to Beatrice's command that he engrave her lessons and visions into his memory. He uses a vivid metaphor to describe his mental state: he compares his mind to sealing wax, which holds the impression of a seal without altering it. He tells Beatrice that her words have been imprinted on his brain in this permanent, indelible way.

However, Dante also expresses a sense of intellectual struggle. He asks Beatrice why her desired words seem to fly so far above his current understanding (his "sight"). He describes a frustrating paradox: the more effort he puts into trying to grasp the meaning of her prophecy, the more the meaning seems to slip away from him.

## Scene 85-90: The Distance of the Divine
In this scene, Beatrice responds to Dante's admission that her words are beyond his current understanding. She explains the reason for his intellectual struggle: Dante has been trained in a specific "school" of thought (referring to human reason, Aristotelian philosophy, and the Scholastic tradition he followed on Earth and through Virgil's guidance). 

Beatrice points out that the "doctrine" of that human school is fundamentally different from the "divine way" she represents. She uses a cosmic metaphor to describe this gap, telling Dante that the distance between his previous intellectual path and the divine truth is as vast as the distance between the Earth and the highest heaven (the Empyrean). Essentially, she is telling him that human logic alone is insufficient to grasp the divine mysteries she is revealing.

## Scene 94-99: The Reminder of Lethe
In this scene, Beatrice responds to Dante's claim that he has no memory of ever being distant from her or having strayed from her path.

Beatrice smiles and gently corrects him using a logical argument. She reminds Dante that he drank from the River Lethe (the river of forgetfulness at the summit of Purgatory) to erase the memory of his sins. She then presents a syllogism: just as the presence of smoke is proof that there is a fire, the fact that Dante experienced "oblivion" (the loss of memory) proves that there was "guilt" (sin) in his heart and will that needed to be forgotten. Essentially, she is telling him that he doesn't remember being distant from her precisely because he *was* distant, and the divine process of purification erased those memories of failure.

## Scene 100-111: The Noon Sun and the Seven Women
In this scene, Beatrice concludes her lesson about the River Lethe and the nature of memory and sin. She tells Dante that she will now speak more plainly and clearly ("naked words") so that his limited human understanding ("rude sight") can finally grasp the truth she is revealing.

The narrative then shifts to describe the celestial environment. The sun is reaching its meridian (noon), shining brilliantly but appearing to move slowly. Dante uses a simile to describe the sun's position at the zenith: he compares it to a guide leading a group of people, where the guide sees their own tracks or new terrain because the sun is directly overhead.

Finally, the scene focuses on the seven nymphs accompanying the procession. They are positioned at the edge of a fading shadow, a sight Dante compares to the cool, dark shade cast by the greenery and black branches over the cold streams of the Alps.

## Scene 124-129: Beatrice's Instruction to Virgil
This scene depicts Beatrice giving instructions to Matelda regarding Dante's spiritual and mental state. Beatrice observes that Dante's memory has been clouded—a result of his experience drinking from the River Lethe in Purgatory—which has obscured his intellectual clarity. To remedy this, Beatrice directs Matelda to lead Dante to the River Eunoé (the river of memory). She instructs Matelda to use her accustomed way of guiding souls to revive Dante's "faded virtue," essentially restoring his memory of his divine purpose and his purified past so that he can fully comprehend the revelations of Paradise.

## Scene 136-141: The Poet's Constraint
In this brief meta-narrative scene, the action of the story pauses. Dante (the author/narrator) breaks the "fourth wall" to address the reader directly. He explains that he would love to describe in detail the experience of drinking from the River Eunoé (the "sweet drink" mentioned in line 138), as it is a profound spiritual experience. However, he reveals a practical and artistic constraint: he has run out of space in the allocated "papers" or structural plan for this second canticle (*Purgatorio*). He concludes that the "rein of art"—the formal requirements and limits of his poetic composition—prevents him from writing more on this specific subject.

## Scene 142-145: The Renewal of the Soul
In this final brief scene of Canto 33, Dante describes the aftermath of drinking from the River Eunoé. Although he previously explained that he could not describe the actual act of drinking due to the structural constraints of his poem, he now reveals the result of the experience. 

Dante returns from the "most holy wave" feeling spiritually reborn. He uses a botanical simile, comparing his state of mind and soul to young plants that have been renewed with fresh, green foliage. This renewal signifies the restoration of his memory of his own virtuous deeds and his divine purpose, which had been erased by the River Lethe. Having been purified and his memory restored, Dante concludes that he is now completely pure and prepared to make the final ascent to the stars—marking the end of his journey through Purgatory and his readiness to enter Paradise.

- `[1:Io]`: Dante
