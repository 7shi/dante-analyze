# Canto 02 — The Arrival of the Celestial Messenger and the Encounter with Casella

## Scene 1-12: Setting the Scene at Dawn
**Scene Analysis: Setting the Scene at Dawn**

**What happens:**
The scene opens with a cosmic "wide shot" to establish the exact time and location of the action. The narrator describes the sunrise. Specifically, he notes that the sun is at the horizon in Purgatory, which corresponds to the sun being at its zenith (noon) over Jerusalem. As the sun rises, the personified Night is retreating across the east (specifically the Ganges river in India), accompanied by the constellation Libra. Consequently, the personified Aurora (the Dawn), whose cheeks are described as white and red, is fading away as she "ages" into the full light of day. Finally, the focus shifts from the heavens down to the two protagonists, who are lingering or moving slowly along the shore, feeling a sense of hesitation or deep reflection about the journey ahead.

**Who does what:**
- **The Sun ({1}):** Rises on the horizon of Purgatory and reaches its highest point over Jerusalem.
- **Night ({2}):** Recedes across the Ganges.
- **Aurora ({6}):** Fades/ages as the morning progresses.
- **Dante ([5]) and Virgil ([7]):** They are traveling slowly, their hearts and bodies lagging as they contemplate their path.

**Who speaks:**
There is no dialogue in this scene. The text is the narrative description provided by the poet-narrator, Dante.

## Scene 19-36: Description of the Angelic Wings
**Scene Analysis: Description of the Angelic Wings**

**What happens:**
Dante briefly looks away from the approaching light to address Virgil, but when he looks back, the light has grown significantly in size and brilliance. As the light draws closer, white forms emerge from it—first one, then another—which are revealed to be wings. While Virgil had been silent during the approach, he suddenly recognizes the entity as a divine messenger. He immediately takes charge, commanding Dante to humble himself by kneeling and folding his hands in prayer. Virgil explains that the being is a celestial official of God who transcends human physics; the angel requires no oars or sails to cross the distance between shores, using only eternal wings that point toward heaven and do not decay like mortal hair.

**Who does what:**

## Scene 37-45: The Vessel Approaches
**Scene Analysis: The Vessel Approaches**

**What happens:**
As the celestial messenger draws closer, its brilliance increases to such an intensity that Dante cannot keep his eyes open; he is forced to look down to shield his sight. The messenger arrives at the shore in a small, swift, and incredibly light boat—so light that it barely displaces the water. The angel acts as the helmsman, steering from the stern. Dante notes that the angel is so divine that even a mere description of him would bring bliss to the reader. The boat is not empty; it carries a group of more than one hundred souls.

**Who does what:**

## Scene 46-51: The Sacred Song and Signal
**Scene Analysis: The Sacred Song and Signal**

**What happens:**
As the boat arrives, the passengers—the hundred souls—sing in unison. They chant the beginning of Psalm 114 ("When Israel went out of Egypt"), which is a symbolic choice: just as the Israelites were liberated from slavery in Egypt, these souls are being liberated from the "slavery" of their sins and the depths of the afterlife to begin their ascent toward God. Once the singing is complete, the angel performs a ritual action, making the sign of the cross over the souls. This acts as a divine signal or blessing, prompting the spirits to quickly disembark from the vessel and move onto the sandy shore of Purgatory. The vessel itself arrives at the beach with great speed.

**Who does what:**
- **The Spirits ([1], [3], [4]):** Sing the Psalm together and, upon the angel's signal, cast themselves onto the shore.
- **The Angel ([2]):** Makes the sign of the holy cross over the spirits to signal their arrival and transition.

**Who speaks:**
The spirits are singing (chanting a psalm), but there is no spoken dialogue in this scene.

## Scene 67-75: The Souls' Reaction to Dante

This revelation leaves them stunned and pale with wonder. Dante uses a simile to describe their reaction, comparing the souls to a crowd of people swarming a messenger who brings an olive branch (a symbol of peace) to hear news. Driven by intense curiosity, the souls crowd around Dante, staring intently at his face, momentarily forgetting their urgent mission to ascend the mountain and undergo the purification necessary to "make themselves beautiful" (attain a state of grace).

## Scene 91-105: Dialogue with Casella
**Scene Analysis: Dialogue with Casella**

**What happens:**
Dante and Casella engage in a brief conversation. Dante explains the purpose of his journey: he is traveling through the afterlife so that he may eventually return to the world of the living. He then asks Casella why he has been delayed so long before being allowed to enter Purgatory. Casella responds by explaining that his delay was not due to any offense or "insult" (*oltraggio*), but rather the will of the celestial messenger (the angel boatman). He explains that the angel collects souls according to a divine, just will, and that for the past three months, the angel had stopped all entries. Casella describes how he was waiting at the mouth of the Tiber River (where the fresh water meets the salt water of the sea) and was benignly turned back by the angel. He concludes by noting that the angel's boat always returns to that specific location to gather the souls who are not destined for the descent into Acheron (Hell).

- **Dante ([2], [3], [4]):** Explains his goal of returning to the living world and questions Casella about his delay.

- **The Souls ([14], [19], [20]):** The collective group of spirits (including Casella) who seek entry to Purgatory and are gathered from the shores to avoid the descent into Hell.

## Scene 106-114: The Sweetness of the Song

Knowing Casella was a skilled musician in life, Dante asks him if the "new law" of the afterlife has stripped him of the memory or the ability to perform the "loving songs" that used to soothe Dante's sorrows on earth. He asks Casella to please console his distressed soul with a song. Casella complies, beginning to sing a canzone that starts with the line, "Love that reasons in my mind." The singing is so beautiful and sweet that Dante, the poet, remarks that the sweetness of that melody still resonates within him as he recounts the experience.

## Scene 124-133: The Dispersal of the Souls
**Scene Analysis: The Dispersal of the Souls**

**What happens:**
The scene describes the immediate reaction of the souls to Cato's reprimand using a vivid simile. Dante compares the souls to a flock of pigeons gathered to feed on grain; the pigeons are peaceful and devoid of their usual pride until something appears that frightens them, causing them to suddenly abandon the bait out of a more urgent fear. Similarly, Dante observes the "fresh crowd" of new arrivals abruptly stop listening to Casella's song and flee toward the coast. They run in a state of disorientation, compared to a man who wanders without knowing where he is going. Finally, Dante and Virgil also promptly depart from the shore to continue their own journey.
