# Canto 14 — The Encounter with the Spirits of the Arno Valley

## Scene 1-6: The Spirits' Observation
In this scene, two unnamed souls on the second terrace of Purgatory (the terrace of the envious) are observing Dante and Virgil as they walk by.

The first soul is struck by the fact that one of the travelers is clearly alive—noting that he has not yet experienced death and is able to open and close his eyes at will (a biological function the dead souls there lack). 

The second soul responds, admitting they don't know who the man is but noting that he is accompanied by someone else (Virgil). The second soul then encourages the first soul to move closer and gently ask the stranger who he is.

## Scene 7-15: The First Spirit's Greeting
In this scene, the two spirits who have been observing Dante and Virgil from the sidelines finally decide to engage with them. They shift from whispering among themselves to facing Dante directly. One of the spirits speaks, addressing Dante with curiosity and a plea for information. He recognizes that Dante is a "soul" who is uniquely "fixed" within a living body—a rare occurrence in Purgatory. The spirit asks Dante, in the name of charity, to tell them who he is and where he comes from, explaining that Dante's living presence is so extraordinary that it fills them with immense wonder.

## Scene 16-24: The Riddle of the Arno
In this scene, Dante responds to the spirits' curiosity with a riddle rather than a direct answer. He describes a small river that originates in Falterona and flows for a hundred miles through the heart of Tuscany. He explains that he hails from the region of this river, but adds that stating his name directly would be "speaking in vain" (using the word *indarno*, which is a clever pun on the name of the river, *Arno*), noting that he is not yet famous. The spirit who had first spoken to him quickly catches the drift of the riddle and identifies the river as the Arno.

## Scene 25-27: The Second Spirit's Query
In this brief scene, the second of the two spirits speaks up. While the first spirit quickly understood Dante's riddle about the Arno river, the second spirit is puzzled by Dante's indirectness. He asks why Dante concealed the name of the river, comparing this habit of avoiding a specific word to the way people often avoid naming things that are horrific or terrifying.

## Scene 28-51: The Corruption of the Valley
In this scene, the first spirit (the one who had previously solved Dante's riddle) answers the second spirit's question about why Dante was so indirect in naming the Arno river. The spirit admits he doesn't know why Dante spoke in riddles, but he asserts that it is fitting for the name of the Arno valley to be obscured or "perish" because of the moral corruption of its people.

The spirit provides a geographical and moral tour of the river's course. He describes the Arno flowing from the mountains down to the sea, noting that the river's "virtue" or goodness shuns the people it passes. He compares the inhabitants of the valley to animals, suggesting that Circe (the enchantress who turned men into swine) seems to have used them as livestock. As the river descends, the spirit describes a progression of degradation: the river first passes through "pigs" (coarse people), then "Botoli" (aggressive, snarling dogs), and finally, as the river grows larger, it encounters "wolves" (predatory, vicious people). The river itself is personified as being disgusted by the people it encounters, "turning its nose" away from them.

## Scene 52-54: The Cunning Foxes
In this brief scene, the first spirit continues his moral geography of the Arno valley. Following the description of the "pigs," "dogs," and "wolves," he describes the river as it continues its descent through dark waters. There, the river encounters people he characterizes as "foxes" (*le volpi*). These people are defined by their extreme fraudulence and cunning; they are so arrogant in their own deceit that they do not fear any intellect or scheme that might attempt to outsmart or entrap them.

## Scene 55-66: The Prophecy of the Hunter
In this scene, the first spirit continues his discourse, transitioning from a critique of the present moral state of the Arno valley to a prophecy about its future. He declares that he will continue to speak so that others may hear his warning. He addresses Dante, stating that it will be beneficial for Dante to remember the revelations being unfolded to the spirit by a "true spirit" (likely a divine or angelic source of inspiration).

The spirit then delivers a vision: he sees a future "descendant" or kinsman of Dante's (a fellow Florentine) who will rise as a "hunter" to purge the "wolves" (the corrupt inhabitants) from the banks of the Arno. This hunter will be ruthless—metaphorically "selling their flesh while still alive" and eventually killing them like wild beasts, stripping them of both their lives and their honor. The spirit describes the hunter emerging bloodstained from the "dismal forest" of this corruption. He concludes by advising Dante to leave the valley as it is, suggesting that this violent purge is a necessary process to ensure the corruption does not simply return to its original, wild state.

## Scene 67-72: The Second Spirit's Grief

## Scene 73-84: Guido's Introduction
In this scene, Dante is moved by the previous events—the prophecy delivered by the first spirit and the visible distress of the second spirit. This combination of words and expressions makes him eager to know who these two souls are. He asks them for their names, blending his request with humble pleas.

The first spirit (the one who delivered the prophecy) responds. He begins by pointing out a contradiction: Dante wants the spirit to be open and identify himself, whereas Dante himself was reluctant to do so (referring back to the riddle he used to avoid naming the Arno and himself). However, the spirit notes that because God’s grace is so evident in Dante (as he is the only living soul permitted here), the spirit is happy to be generous with the information. He reveals his identity as Guido del Duca. He then explains the depth of his sin of envy, stating that his blood was so "boiled" by envy that if he had seen any person being happy—or if he had seen Dante himself in his current state—he would have burst with hatred.

## Scene 85-96: The Blight of Lineage
In this scene, Guido del Duca continues his speech to Dante. He begins with a personal lament, noting that he "reaps only straw" from his own lineage—meaning his descendants are worthless or the family line has dwindled in quality. He then pivots to a general rhetorical question addressed to all of humanity, questioning why people invest their hearts and desires in things that should be forbidden or are harmful.

Guido then introduces the second spirit who has been accompanying him. He identifies this spirit as Rinier, a man of honor from the house of Calboli. Guido praises Rinier, stating that since Rinier's time, no one in that family has ever matched his value or achieved such prestige.

Finally, Guido expands his critique from a single family to a whole region of Italy (the area between the Po River, the mountains, the sea, and the Reno River). He describes this entire region as being overgrown with "poisonous shrubs," a metaphor for the corrupt, envious, and fraudulent people who inhabit the land. He suggests that the moral blight is so severe that any attempt to "cultivate" or reform these people would be futile.

## Scene 97-102: Lament for Romagna
In this scene, Guido del Duca continues his lament for the moral decline of Romagna. He lists several individuals who were once symbols of honor and virtue in the region, asking rhetorically where they have gone. By invoking the names of these respected figures, Guido emphasizes the contrast between the noble past and the current state of the Romagnuols, whom he dismissively calls "bastards" (meaning they have degenerated from their ancestors). He specifically looks toward cities like Bologna and Faenza, wondering when the region will once again produce men of such high character as the Fabbro family or Bernardin di Fosco.

## Scene 112-123: The Decay of Towns
In this scene, Guido del Duca continues his mournful reflection on the decline of Romagna, focusing on specific noble families and individuals. His tone is one of bitter irony; he suggests that for many of these lineages, the only "mercy" or "good" remaining is their extinction. He argues that it is better for a corrupt family line to end than to continue producing offspring who will only propagate the same vices.

Guido addresses Bretinoro, questioning why the line hasn't already vanished to avoid further evil. He praises Bagnacaval for not having more children, while condemning Castrocaro and Conio for continuing to produce heirs, thereby further entangling their lineage in sin. He mentions the Pagan family, noting that while they might find some relief when the "demon" (the spirit of corruption or literal demonic influence) leaves them, no pure remnant of their honor will ever survive. Finally, he addresses Ugolin de' Fantolin, stating that Ugolin's name is now "secure" (safe from further degradation) precisely because there is no one left to carry it on or further tarnish it through dark, deviant behavior.

## Scene 124-126: Guido's Exhaustion
In this brief scene, Guido del Duca reaches an emotional breaking point. After his extensive and bitter lament over the moral decay of Romagna and the loss of its noble houses, he finds himself overwhelmed by grief. He tells Dante to move on, explaining that he currently finds more solace (or is more compelled) by weeping than by speaking. He concludes by noting that the reflection on the shared fate and "reason" of his people has constricted his mind, leaving him unable to continue the conversation.

## Scene 127-135: The Thunderous Voice
In this scene, Dante narrates the moment after he and Virgil leave the company of Guido del Duca and Rinier. He notes that they were aware the two souls were still listening to them as they departed, though the souls remained silent, allowing the travelers to proceed. 

As Dante and Virgil continue on their way alone, they are suddenly startled by a booming voice that arrives like a bolt of lightning cutting through the air. The voice, coming from the opposite direction, shouts a menacing warning: "I would kill anyone who knows me!" After this brief, violent outburst, the voice vanishes as quickly as it appeared, compared to the way thunder dissipates when a cloud descends.

## Scene 142-151: Aglauro's Warning
In this scene, the air becomes quiet, and the spirit who just identified himself as Aglauro speaks to Dante. Aglauro first comments on the menacing voice heard in the previous scene, describing it as a "hard blow" or "harsh path" (likely referring to the uncontrolled passion of envy or hatred) that a person should keep contained. 

Aglauro then shifts his focus to Dante and Virgil, warning them that they are taking the bait of the "ancient adversary" (Satan), who uses such lures to pull souls toward himself. He points out a spiritual contradiction: while Heaven is calling to them and unfolding its eternal beauties all around them, their eyes (particularly Dante's) remain fixed on the earth. Because of this preoccupation with earthly matters, Aglauro explains that they are being rebuked or "struck" by the one who sees and understands all things (God).
