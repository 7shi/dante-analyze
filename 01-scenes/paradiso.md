# Paradiso scene breakdown

| Lines | Scene | Summary |
|---|---|---|

| 13-27 | The Invocation | The poet prays to Apollo for the poetic inspiration and breath necessary to describe the blessed kingdom. |
| 28-36 | The Poet's Struggle | Dante reflects on the difficulty of his task and the hope for a more successful voice in the future. |
| 37-42 | The Cosmic Engine | A description of the celestial light and the perfection of the cosmic structure. |
| 43-54 | The Vision of Beatrice | Dante witnesses Beatrice turning to look at the sun, pulling his gaze toward her. |
| 55-63 | The Blinding Light | The intense brilliance of the celestial light overwhelms the poet's vision like glowing iron. |
| 64-69 | The Meeting | Dante and Beatrice gaze at one another within the eternal spheres. |
| 70-75 | The Linguistic Struggle | Dante finds himself unable to express the concept of 'transhumanizing' through mere words. |
| 76-84 | The Celestial Harmony | The cosmic music and light ignite a profound and unprecedented desire within the poet. |
| 85-93 | Beatrice's Rebuke | Beatrice speaks to correct Dante's false perceptions and clarify his true location. |
| 94-99 | Dante's Wonder | Dante expresses his astonishment at the realization that he has transcended his earthly body. |
| 100-108 | The Doctrine of Order | Beatrice explains how all things in the universe are ordered to reflect the divine. |
| 109-120 | The Mechanics of the Spheres | Beatrice describes how all creatures and elements are moved through the heavens by instinct and intellect. |
| 121-126 | The Guidance of Providence | The poet learns how Divine Providence maintains the stability and purpose of the celestial motions. |
| 127-135 | The Deviation of Desire | Beatrice explains how certain forces can deviate from the divine path due to false pleasure. |
| 136-142 | Final Instruction | Beatrice concludes her lesson by comparing Dante's ascent to a descending stream and turns back to the sky. |

| 139-141 | The Union of Virtue and Body | She explains how divine virtue interacts with and animates the celestial bodies. |
| 142-144 | The Radiance of Virtue | She compares the radiance of virtue in a body to the joy felt by a living eye. |
| 145-148 | The Nature of Light | Beatrice concludes that the apparent differences in light come from its formal principle, not from density. |
