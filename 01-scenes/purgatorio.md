# Purgatorio scene breakdown

## Canto 1: The Ascent to Purgatory and the Encounter with the Guardian

| Lines | Scene | Summary |
|---|---|---|
| 1-12 | The Poet’s Prologue | Dante introduces his new theme of Purgatory and invokes the Muses and Calliope to assist his song. |
| 13-21 | The Awakening Sky | The celestial landscape changes as the dawn colors appear and the planet Venus rises in the sky. |
| 22-27 | The Four Stars | Dante observes the bright northern stars and laments his inability to see them again. |
| 28-39 | The Discovery of the Sage | Dante notices the elderly figure of Virgil illuminated by the celestial lights. |
| 40-48 | Virgil’s Inquiry | Virgil questions how Dante has escaped the underworld to reach these caves. |
| 49-51 | The Guide’s Gesture | Virgil takes Dante by the hand and signals for him to follow. |
| 52-66 | The Explanation of the Mission | Virgil explains that he was sent by a heavenly woman to guide souls. |
| 67-84 | The Plea for Passage | Virgil requests permission to proceed through the terraces of Purgatory. |
| 85-99 | The Guardian’s Mandate | The interlocutor responds and instructs Dante to use a reed to cleanse his face. |
| 100-108 | The Isle of Reeds | A description of the fragile, reed-covered islet where nothing else can grow. |
| 109-114 | The Departure | The figure vanishes, and Virgil directs Dante to follow him down the plain. |
| 115-120 | The Morning Trek | The pair walks through the lonely, dewy landscape as dawn approaches. |
| 121-129 | The Light of Revelation | The sun hits the dew, and Dante is moved to tears by the sudden clarity of sight. |
| 130-136 | Arrival at the Shore | The travelers reach the deserted coast, where Dante finds a reed to wrap himself in. |

## Canto 2: The Arrival of the Celestial Messenger and the Encounter with Casella

| Lines | Scene | Summary |
|---|---|---|
| 1-12 | Setting the Scene at Dawn | The poet establishes the astrological time of dawn and describes the travelers' position on the sea. |
| 13-18 | A Sudden Light Appears | A brilliant, fast-moving light appears on the western horizon, startling the travelers. |
| 19-36 | Description of the Angelic Wings | Virgil describes the divine messenger and the magnificent, incorruptible white wings appearing around the light. |
| 37-45 | The Vessel Approaches | The divine vessel nears the shore, carrying a celestial pilot and over a hundred spirits. |
| 46-51 | The Sacred Song and Signal | The spirits sing a psalm and react to the pilot's sign of the cross by casting themselves upon the shore. |
| 52-60 | The Souls Seek Guidance | The spirits on the shore ask Dante and Virgil for the path to the mountain. |
| 61-66 | Virgil's Response | Virgil explains that they are also pilgrims and that the path ahead will be easier than the one they have already traveled. |
| 67-75 | The Souls' Reaction to Dante | The spirits notice Dante and move toward him with great affection and joy. |
| 76-84 | The Physical Embrace | One spirit embraces Dante with such intensity that he reciprocates the gesture. |
| 85-90 | Recognition of Casella | Dante recognizes the soul as Casella, who greets him with eternal love. |
| 91-105 | Dialogue with Casella | Casella explains that his current state is due to a divine decree preventing him from crossing the Acheronte for three months. |
| 106-114 | The Sweetness of the Song | Casella begins to sing a verse of 'Amor che ne la mente mi ragiona' with profound sweetness. |
| 115-123 | The Pilot's Interruption | The celestial pilot interrupts the music, urging the spirits to hurry toward the mountain. |
| 124-133 | The Dispersal of the Souls | Like a flock of startled pigeons, the souls suddenly abandon their song and flee toward the coast. |

## Canto 4: The Ascent of the Mountain and the Encounter with Belacqua

| Lines | Scene | Summary |
|---|---|---|
| 1-12 | Philosophical Prologue | Dante reflects on how intense emotion can divide the soul's attention, making one part of the self present and the other absent. |
| 13-18 | The Voice in the Dark | Dante recalls the moment an unexpected voice announced their arrival at a specific destination. |
| 19-24 | The Simile of the Vineyard | Dante uses a vivid metaphor of a peasant harvesting grapes to describe the suddenness and separation of the souls. |
| 25-30 | The Flight of Desire | Dante describes the sensation of flying up the mountain, driven by a great longing. |
| 31-35 | The Difficult Climb | The poets struggle through a broken, narrow path where the ground seems to pull at their feet and hands. |
| 36-39 | Virgil's Command | Dante asks for direction at the edge of a precipice, and Virgil instructs him to keep climbing. |
| 40-48 | The Exhausted Climber | Overwhelmed by the steepness and his own fatigue, Dante asks Virgil to look back at his solitary state. |
| 49-54 | The Rest on the Summit | Encouraged by Virgil, Dante manages a leap to reach a ridge where they sit facing the sunrise. |
| 55-60 | Observing the Sun | Dante observes the position of the sun and realizes its light is striking them from the left. |
| 61-75 | The Astronomical Lecture | Virgil explains the complex movement of the sun and the zodiac through a geographical analogy involving Mt. Sion. |
| 76-84 | Dante's Astronomical Observation | Dante confirms his understanding of the equator and the different perspectives of the sun's path. |
| 85-87 | A Request for Guidance | Dante expresses his desire to know how much further they must climb. |
| 88-96 | The Nature of the Mountain | Virgil explains that the climb becomes easier as they ascend and that the end of the path awaits them. |
| 97-102 | A Voice from the Shadows | A sudden voice interrupts them, leading them to discover a large rock nearby. |
| 103-108 | The Slumbering Figure | They find a person sitting in the shadows of the rock, slumped in a state of extreme lethargy. |
| 109-114 | The Recognition of Belacqua | Dante points out the lazy figure, and the man awakens to address them. |
| 115-122 | The Encounter with Belacqua | Dante recognizes the man as Belacqua, whose lethargy brings a momentary smile to Dante's lips. |
| 123-126 | Questioning the Sloth | Dante asks Belacqua why he is sitting there alone and if he is waiting for a guide. |
| 127-135 | Belacqua's Excuse | Belacqua explains that he cannot climb because he must first complete a period of purgation outside the gate. |
| 136-139 | Resuming the Journey | Virgil urges Dante to continue moving as the sun begins to set and night approaches. |

| 58-63 | Dante's Vow | Dante promises to do what he can for these noble spirits as he travels through the worlds. |
| 64-72 | The First Soul's Request | A soul asks Dante to pray for him in Fano, where he died due to a political vendetta. |
| 73-84 | The Death in the Marsh | The first soul recounts how he was driven into a swamp and died as his blood pooled in the earth. |

| 94-108 | The Battle of Angels | The soul describes being wounded by a blade and the spiritual struggle between an angel and a demon over his soul. |
| 109-114 | The Formation of Mist | The soul explains how the heavy vapors of the earth rose to form a thick, suffocating mist. |
| 115-129 | The River's Embrace | The soul recounts how the rain and floodwaters carried his body into the Arno river, which buried him. |
| 130-136 | The Plea of La Pia | A third soul, La Pia, asks to be remembered and recounts how she died in the Maremma. |
