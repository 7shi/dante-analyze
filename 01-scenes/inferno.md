# Inferno scene breakdown

## Canto 1: The Dark Wood and the Encounter with Virgil

| Lines | Scene | Summary |
|---|---|---|
| 1-12 | Lost in the Dark Wood | Dante describes his state of spiritual confusion and how he lost the straight path in a dark forest. |
| 13-18 | The Sight of the Sunlit Hill | Dante reaches the foot of a hill and sees its summit illuminated by the rays of the sun. |
| 19-21 | Relief from Fear | The sight of the light causes Dante's intense fear to subside momentarily. |
| 22-30 | The Swimmer Simile | Dante compares his traumatized spirit to a swimmer looking back at a dangerous sea. |
| 31-36 | The Appearance of the Leopard | A spotted leopard appears and blocks Dante's path, forcing him to turn back. |
| 37-43 | The Morning Light | The time of day is established as early morning, bringing a brief moment of hope through the cosmic order. |
| 44-48 | The Appearance of the Lion | A hungry and fierce lion appears, causing the very air to tremble with its rage. |
| 49-60 | The Appearance of the She-Wolf | A starving she-wolf appears, driving Dante back down into the darkness of the valley. |
| 61-66 | A Shadow in the Desert | As Dante falls, he sees a faint figure and cries out to him for mercy. |
| 67-75 | Virgil's Introduction | The stranger identifies himself as a Roman poet who sang of the Trojan wars. |
| 76-78 | The Challenge to Ascend | The stranger questions why Dante has returned to such trouble instead of climbing the mountain. |
| 79-81 | Dante's Recognition | Dante realizes with shame that the figure is the great poet Virgil. |
| 82-90 | A Plea for Guidance | Dante praises Virgil as his master and begs him to protect him from the beasts. |
| 91-99 | The Nature of the She-Wolf | Virgil warns that the she-wolf is a greedy beast that will never be satisfied. |
| 100-105 | The Prophecy of the Veltro | Virgil foretells the coming of a hound who will finally bring an end to the wolf's hunger. |
| 106-111 | The Fate of Italy | The prophecy links the end of the beast to the salvation of the Italian people. |
| 112-120 | The Plan for the Journey | Virgil accepts the role of guide and describes the sights of Hell and Purgatory. |
| 121-129 | The Prohibition of the Ascent | Virgil explains that he cannot lead Dante to the summit because of his past rebellion against imperial rule. |
| 130-135 | Dante's Acceptance | Dante thanks Virgil and commits to the journey, hoping to reach the gates of Heaven. |
| 136-136 | The Journey Begins | Virgil begins to move, and Dante follows closely behind him. |

| 43-51 | Virgil's Diagnosis | Virgil responds to Dante's hesitation by identifying his fear as a form of spiritual cowardice. |

| 58-74 | Beatrice's Message | Beatrice speaks with an angelic voice, commanding Virgil to guide Dante to safety. |
| 75-84 | Dante's Inquiry | After a pause, Dante asks Beatrice why she does not descend herself to assist him. |

| 100-114 | Lucia's Intervention | Virgil recounts how Lucia prompted Beatrice to act, which compelled Beatrice to descend to the Limbo. |
